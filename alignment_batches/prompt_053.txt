--- W000802: Sheldon Whitehouse (Democrat, RI, senate) ---
Sheldon Whitehouse is the Senate's most passionate crusader against dark money — and also, somehow, one of its most enthusiastic stock traders. The Rhode Island Democrat has delivered over 300 "Time to Wake Up" speeches on the Senate floor about climate change and the fossil fuel industry's web of anonymous donations. He has introduced the DISCLOSE Act in every Congress since 2012. He has written a book about dark money's corruption of the courts. He has, in short, built an entire brand around the principle that hidden money in politics is an existential threat to democracy. And then he missed his own STOCK Act disclosure deadline for purchases of Target and Tesla stock, because apparently transparency is for other people.

Whitehouse's net worth is estimated between $7 million and $21 million, placing him among the wealthiest members of Congress. He is one of the Senate's most active stock traders, with holdings in Apple, Amazon, and other major corporations — some in sectors overseen by committees on which he sits. When asked about banning congressional stock trading, he said he supports it "in principle" and is "working with proponents of multiple bills." The man who has given 300 speeches about urgency on climate change suddenly needs more time to study the stock trading question.

Then there is Bailey's Beach. Whitehouse belongs to — or rather, his wife belongs to, after he conveniently transferred his shares — the Spouting Rock Beach Association in Newport, one of the most exclusive private clubs in America. When the club was accused of having an all-white membership, Whitehouse responded with the immortal words: "I think the people who are running the place are still working on that, and I'm sorry it hasn't happened yet." He had promised to resign from the club when he first ran for Senate in 2006. He did not. The Vanderbilts and the Astors would be proud.

His largest donor, the League of Conservation Voters, is itself funded substantially by the Sixteen Thirty Fund — classified as a "dark money" group by The New York Times and The Atlantic. So the senator who has made dark money his singular obsession is bankrolled by an organization that runs on dark money. The circle of life in Washington is truly beautiful.

To be fair, Whitehouse has done real legislative work. He co-introduced the SMART Prices Act with Reed to expand Medicare drug price negotiation. He sits on the Judiciary Committee and has been a serious voice on court reform. His End Tax Breaks for Dark Money Act would close loopholes letting wealthy donors get tax deductions for funding political nonprofits. These are substantive positions that align with the 80% Bill.

But when a senator worth up to $21 million, who belongs to a Gilded Age beach club, who trades stocks while writing the rules, and whose top donor is funded by dark money, delivers his 301st speech about the corrupting influence of wealth in politics — at some point, the audience has to wonder whether this is a floor speech or a confession.

--- W000804: Robert J. Wittman (Republican, VA, house) ---
Rob Wittman has represented Virginia's 1st Congressional District since 2007, which is a polite way of saying he has held the same job for nearly two decades while opposing term limits. The district stretches from the Northern Neck to the exurbs of Richmond, encompassing some of the most consequential military installations in the country — including the Newport News shipyard where Huntington Ingalls Industries builds the Navy's nuclear aircraft carriers. This geographical coincidence has proven extremely convenient for Wittman's campaign treasury.

As Vice Chair of the House Armed Services Committee and former chairman of its Seapower and Projection Forces Subcommittee, Wittman is the defense industry's dream congressman. Huntington Ingalls alone has contributed over $181,000 to his campaigns since 2007. Northrop Grumman and BAE Systems round out a donor portfolio so concentrated in defense contracting that 21 percent of his campaign funds come from defense and defense aerospace interests. AIPAC is his second-largest contributor organization. His campaign finance filings show that his money comes from "almost no individuals in his district and mostly from defense companies and single-issue lobbyists."

On the Pentagon audit — one of the rare bipartisan layups in American politics — Wittman has issued statements expressing concern but has not championed legislation to impose real consequences on the Department of Defense for failing its seventh consecutive audit. When you receive $181,000 from the shipbuilder, you tend to audit with a gentle hand.

Wittman also ran afoul of the STOCK Act, purchasing up to $45,000 in shares of TJX Companies, Mastercard, and Accenture while failing to disclose the trades within the required 45-day window. He traded defense contractor stocks while sitting on the committee that oversees their budgets, which is the kind of arrangement that makes the stock trading ban poll at 86 percent.

On January 6, 2021, Wittman was one of 147 Republicans who voted to overturn the 2020 presidential election results, objecting to Pennsylvania's electoral certification after a mob breached the Capitol. He has called police reform a "state and local issue," a convenient federalism argument that evaporates the moment defense spending is on the table. He voted with the 427-to-1 majority on the Epstein Files Transparency Act — brave work there.

Wittman has no public record of supporting bans on congressional stock trading, corporate PAC money, dark money, junk fees, or corporate home buying. He is the platonic ideal of a defense-district incumbent: reliable for contractors, invisible to constituents, and perpetually reelected on the strength of yard signs and aircraft carrier jobs.

--- W000805: Mark R. Warner (Democrat, VA, senate) ---
Mark Warner is living proof that you can be worth a quarter of a billion dollars and still cosponsor campaign finance reform with a straight face. Virginia's senior senator built his fortune flipping cellular spectrum licenses in the early days of telecom deregulation, parlaying insider knowledge of federal telecommunications law into a venture capital empire that seeded companies like Nextel and XM Satellite Radio. He is now the second-wealthiest member of the United States Senate, and he would very much like to talk to you about fiscal responsibility.

As Chairman of the Senate Intelligence Committee and a member of the Banking, Budget, and Finance Committees, Warner occupies some of the most market-sensitive perches in Washington. His stock trading disclosures read like a hedge fund prospectus: in 2024, he purchased up to $3.45 million in municipal bonds, including bonds from the Virginia Resources Authority — the state he represents. In 2022, he bought shares in Acumen Pharmaceuticals, an Alzheimer's biotech company, while leading a congressional Alzheimer's task force and sitting on a subcommittee overseeing government health programs. He has not cosponsored the Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act.

To his credit, Warner cosponsored the DISCLOSE Act to unmask dark money donors and voted for the Inflation Reduction Act's 15 percent corporate minimum tax — even engineering a key amendment during the vote-a-rama to keep the bill alive. He backed Medicare drug price negotiation, calling it "commonsense." But Warner also spent years as part of the Gang of Six pushing to cut Social Security and Medicare benefits in the name of debt reduction, positioning himself as the Democrat most eager to means-test grandma's retirement.

His leadership PAC, Forward Together, raised $1.36 million in the 2024 cycle while distributing $1.56 million to fellow Democrats — the kind of influence operation that makes campaign finance reform feel like a hobby rather than a commitment. Warner supports the DISCLOSE Act but has shown no interest in banning corporate PACs, enacting term limits, or — critically — banning the congressional stock trading that has made him spectacularly wealthy.

Warner is the archetype of the moderate Democrat who agrees with 80 percent of what voters want in theory, then finds a procedural reason to protect the 20 percent that keeps his portfolio humming. He will give you transparency on dark money and drug prices, but ask him to put his own trades in a blind trust and watch the "business guy" suddenly rediscover the virtues of personal liberty.

--- W000806: Daniel Webster (Republican, FL, house) ---
Daniel Webster has been in elected office since 1980, making him one of the longest-serving politicians in Florida history — a distinction that, in any conversation about term limits, lands somewhere between "impressive" and "ironic" [1]. First elected to the Florida House, then the Florida Senate, then the U.S. House in 2010, Webster has spent more than four decades in government while also running the family air conditioning and heating business his parents founded in 1962 [2]. He was the first Republican Speaker of the Florida House since Reconstruction, and he briefly ran for Speaker of the U.S. House in 2015, earning the backing of the Freedom Caucus for his promise to decentralize power and flatten the "pyramid of power" in Congress [3]. He currently serves on the Natural Resources, Science, Space and Technology, and Transportation and Infrastructure committees, representing the retirement-heavy communities of central Florida's 11th District.

On the twenty items in the 80% Bill, Webster's record is a study in quiet opposition. He voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which contained Medicare's first-ever authority to negotiate drug prices — a provision that directly benefits the seniors who comprise a large share of his district [4]. He voted against the For the People Act, which would have addressed gerrymandering, dark money, and campaign finance reform in a single package [5]. He has not cosponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, impose a lifetime lobbying ban, audit the Pentagon, establish paid family leave, mandate police body cameras, or close the carried interest loophole. On the DISCLOSE Act, which would force dark money groups to reveal their donors, Webster has been nowhere to be found.

Perhaps most telling is his stance on term limits. A U.S. Term Limits poll of his own district found that 92 percent of Republican voters favored congressional term limits — yet Webster has refused to sign the U.S. Term Limits pledge and has not cosponsored a term limits constitutional amendment [6]. For a man who has held elected office for over four decades, the reluctance is at least internally consistent, if not exactly populist. His 2022 primary challenger, Anthony Sabatini, used the issue to close within six points of the incumbent, demonstrating just how out of step Webster is with his own base on the question [6].

The donor picture is modest by congressional standards — Webster's net worth was estimated at roughly $814,000 in 2018 [7] — but the funding sources tell a familiar story. His campaigns have drawn heavily from real estate, construction, insurance, and health professional industries, with corporate PACs forming a reliable backbone of his fundraising [8]. These are precisely the sectors that benefit from the absence of the structural reforms in the 80% Bill: no stock trading ban, no corporate PAC ban, no wealth tax, no junk fee regulation. When your air conditioning business has been in the family for three generations and your political career has lasted just as long, the incentive to change the system that built both is, to put it mildly, limited.

Webster is the rare congressman who openly campaigned on reforming how Congress operates — flattening hierarchies, empowering rank-and-file members — while opposing virtually every structural reform that voters actually want. He will talk about process all day long, but on the substance of the 80% Bill — stock trading bans, dark money disclosure, term limits, drug price negotiation, Pentagon accountability — he has been reliably absent or opposed. In FL-11, the air conditioning may be excellent, but the winds of change blow elsewhere.

--- W000808: Frederica S. Wilson (Democrat, FL, house) ---
Frederica S. Wilson is impossible to miss — and not just because of the sequined cowboy hats she has worn on the House floor for more than three decades [1]. A former elementary school principal and Miami-Dade School Board member, Wilson founded the 5000 Role Models of Excellence Project in 1993, a mentoring program for at-risk minority boys that has since grown to serve more than 8,000 students and awarded millions in college scholarships [2]. She has represented South Florida since 2011, currently serving the 24th District, which stretches across predominantly Black communities in Miami-Dade and Broward counties. She sits on the Education and Workforce Committee and the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee, where she serves as Ranking Member of the Water Resources and Environment Subcommittee [3]. She gained national prominence in 2012 for her vocal advocacy after the killing of Trayvon Martin, a constituent whose family she knew personally [4].

On the 80% Bill's twenty items, Wilson's voting record aligns with a substantial majority. She voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which included Medicare's authority to negotiate prescription drug prices [5]. She voted for the For the People Act (H.R. 1), the sweeping democracy reform package that addressed gerrymandering, dark money, and voter access — legislation that effectively encompassed the 80% Bill's goals on fair elections, the DISCLOSE Act, and overturning Citizens United in a single bill [6]. She has been a consistent defender of Social Security, publicly pledging to vote against any measure to privatize the program, cut benefits, or raise the eligibility age [7]. She secured $1.7 million in federal funding for police body cameras for the Hollywood, Florida police department, putting real dollars behind the transparency mandate that most politicians only discuss in press releases [8]. Her 100 percent AFL-CIO scorecard rating in 2024 reflects consistent support for worker-friendly legislation, including paid family leave provisions [9].

Where Wilson falls short of the full 80% Bill agenda is harder to pin down, in part because many of these items — banning congressional stock trading, imposing a lifetime lobbying ban, taxing the ultra-wealthy, banning corporate PACs — have not received standalone floor votes that would force a public position. Wilson holds no significant stock investments, according to her financial disclosures, which removes the personal conflict of interest that plagues many colleagues on the stock trading question [10]. But the absence of vocal cosponsorship on some of these measures — particularly the corporate PAC ban and lobbying restrictions — leaves gaps that a constituent might reasonably want filled. On term limits, as a Democrat in a safe seat she has won repeatedly since 2010, the silence is predictable if not exactly courageous.

The money side of Wilson's career is relatively modest. Her estimated net worth sits around $1.5 to $2.5 million, much of it in bank deposits, real estate, and an insurance annuity rather than stock market investments [10]. Her campaign funding has drawn from lawyers, labor unions, and health professionals — industries generally aligned with her legislative priorities rather than in tension with them [11]. She has not faced the kind of donor-versus-voter contradictions that define many of her colleagues, though the presence of any corporate PAC money in a campaign that could theoretically support banning such PACs is, at minimum, a conversation worth having.

Wilson is one of those members whose biography — educator, mentor, community organizer — maps cleanly onto her legislative priorities in a way that Washington rarely rewards. Her support for Medicare drug negotiation, Social Security, police body cameras, fair elections, and dark money disclosure puts her on the right side of most items on the 80% Bill. The remaining questions are ones of volume and ambition: Will she actively champion the stock trading ban, the lobbying ban, the corporate PAC ban, and term limits — or simply vote the right way if someone else does the heavy lifting? In a safe district where reelection is all but guaranteed, the risk of leading on these issues is low. The only question is whether she takes it.

--- W000809: Steve Womack (Republican, AR, house) ---
Steve Womack, Republican of Arkansas's 3rd congressional district, has represented the northwest corner of the state since 2011, succeeding John Boozman when Boozman moved to the Senate. A retired Army National Guard colonel with over thirty years of service and the former mayor of Rogers, Womack built his congressional identity around fiscal discipline and defense[1]. He chaired the House Budget Committee from 2018 to 2019 and served as its ranking member through 2021, earning the title of "Fiscal Hero" from the Campaign to Fix the Debt in 2022[2]. He holds one of the longest consecutive voting streaks in House history, having cast more than 8,823 consecutive votes as of 2025 — a record that says more about attendance than it does about the substance of what he is voting for[3].

On the 80% Bill issues, Womack occupies the curious position of a self-described fiscal hawk who rarely turns his talons toward the defense budget. He has championed balanced budgets and co-introduced the Sustainable Budget Act to create a bipartisan commission addressing the national debt, declaring that "the exploding national debt is one of the greatest threats to our country"[4]. Yet he has never publicly supported Article 6 (Audit the Pentagon), despite the Department of Defense failing its financial audit eight consecutive years — a peculiar blind spot for someone who claims every dollar of taxpayer money demands accountability[5]. He has acknowledged that Social Security is built on "kind of a flawed actuary" and floated raising the retirement age and means-testing benefits[6], but has not endorsed Article 11 (Fund Social Security) through measures like lifting the payroll tax cap. On Medicare drug negotiation (Article 7), he voted against the Inflation Reduction Act that enabled it. He has taken no public position on banning congressional stock trading (Article 1), the lifetime lobbying ban (Article 3), banning corporate PACs (Article 5), dark money disclosure (Article 13), the DISCLOSE Act (Article 16), or term limits (Article 20) — despite serving his eighth term in a body he insists should be more accountable.

To his credit, Womack displayed a streak of institutional conscience that distinguishes him from many in his caucus. He declined to sign the amicus brief in Texas v. Pennsylvania seeking to overturn the 2020 election results, voted to certify both Arizona's and Pennsylvania's Electoral College votes, and was one of just 35 Republicans who voted to establish the January 6th commission[7][8]. These votes suggest someone who takes democratic norms seriously — or at least more seriously than two-thirds of his Republican colleagues. On Article 8 (Fair Elections), this gives him a marginally better record than the median House Republican, though he has not endorsed anti-gerrymandering legislation or broader election reform.

Womack's donor profile reflects the geography of his district. Northwest Arkansas is home to Walmart's global headquarters, Tyson Foods, and J.B. Hunt Transport, and these corporate giants and their employees have been reliable contributors throughout his career[9]. The defense sector also features prominently, consistent with his military background and reliable votes for defense spending increases[10]. He voted for the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act and the 2025 "One Big Beautiful Bill," backing tax cuts he said would prevent imposing "a major tax increase on the people I represent"[12] — though the primary beneficiaries of carried interest loopholes (Article 17) and ultra-wealthy tax provisions (Article 4) are decidedly not the median voter in Rogers, Arkansas.

Womack is, in many ways, the Republican establishment's platonic ideal: a veteran, a budget chairman, a man who shows up for every vote and says the right things about fiscal responsibility. But a fiscal hawk who will not audit the Pentagon, a budget chairman who backed tax cuts that ballooned the deficit he decries, and an institutionalist who still will not touch congressional stock trading or corporate PAC money — that is not fiscal conservatism. That is theater with a spreadsheet.

--- W000817: Elizabeth Warren (Democrat, MA, senate) ---
Elizabeth Warren has represented Massachusetts in the United States Senate since 2013, winning her third term in November 2024 against Republican John Deaton [1]. Before entering politics, Warren was a Harvard Law professor who proposed the creation of a federal agency to protect consumers from predatory financial practices. That idea became the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, established under the Dodd-Frank Act of 2010, which has since returned more than $21 billion to defrauded consumers [2]. It is the rare case of an academic idea surviving contact with Washington and actually working — which may explain why Warren has spent the years since trying to replicate the formula.

On the 80% Bill's twenty policies, Warren is not just aligned — she is the author or architect of several. Her Ultra-Millionaire Tax would impose a 2% annual levy on net worth above $50 million and 6% above $1 billion, projected to raise $3.75 trillion over a decade [3]. Her 15% Corporate Alternative Minimum Tax was enacted as part of the Inflation Reduction Act, making it one of the few progressive tax proposals to actually become law [4]. She has cosponsored the DISCLOSE Act to force dark money donors into the open [5], joined 47 Senate colleagues in introducing the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act to combat gerrymandering and voter suppression [6], and sponsored legislation to extend paid family leave protections to part-time workers [7]. She supports independent redistricting commissions, automatic voter registration, and the full suite of democracy reforms the 80% Bill envisions.

Warren's fundraising model is itself a statement. During her 2020 presidential run, more than two-thirds of her $6 million first-quarter haul came from donors giving $200 or less [8]. Her Senate campaigns follow a similar pattern. OpenSecrets data shows she received no contributions from pharmaceutical industry PACs or top pharma executives, despite the industry being classified among her top donor sectors — a distinction that exists because OpenSecrets counts any employee donation of $200 or more toward a company's total, meaning an entry-level HR officer's check gets lumped in with corporate strategy [9]. It is a methodological nuance that her opponents rarely bother to mention and that Warren herself has been quick to clarify.

Her role as Senate Democratic Conference Vice Chair during the 118th Congress placed her in party leadership, a position that sometimes pulls senators away from the granular legislative work of committees and floor amendments [10]. Critics note that leadership roles can muffle the very independence that made Warren famous. But her legislative output — 44 bills signed into law across both Democratic and Republican administrations, over 60% of them bipartisan [4] — suggests that the professorial firebrand has learned to work the inside game without abandoning the outside one.

Warren is, by any measure, one of the 80% Bill's strongest natural allies in the Senate. She has spent her career building the intellectual and legislative infrastructure for exactly these reforms. The question has never been whether she supports the agenda — it is whether the Senate, with its filibuster and its glacial pace, will let any of it through.
