--- Q000023: Mike Quigley (Democrat, IL, house) ---
Mike Quigley has represented Illinois's 5th Congressional District — the heart of Chicago's North Side, stretching from Lincoln Square to O'Hare — since winning a 2009 special election to replace Rahm Emanuel, who left to become Obama's chief of staff [1]. A former Cook County commissioner, Quigley built his early career on government transparency and ethics reform, which makes him one of the more interesting test cases for the 80% Bill. He serves on the powerful House Appropriations Committee, where he has sat on the defense subcommittee, giving him a direct window into the Pentagon spending that most of his colleagues prefer not to examine too closely [2]. He is also a member of the Congressional Transparency Caucus, which he co-founded [3].

To his credit, Quigley's record on the 80% Bill is stronger than most. He was an early cosponsor of the STOCK Act and has supported subsequent efforts to ban congressional stock trading [4]. He cosponsored the DISCLOSE Act, which would require super PACs and dark-money groups to reveal their donors [5]. He has backed Medicare drug price negotiation and voted for the Inflation Reduction Act's drug-pricing provisions [6]. He has supported paid family leave legislation, including the FAMILY Act [7]. On campaign finance, he has been a consistent vote for reform, supporting the For the People Act and its successors [8]. He has also cosponsored legislation to require police body cameras and supported broader policing accountability measures [9]. His transparency bona fides extend to the Pentagon: he has publicly called for more rigorous auditing of defense spending, though he has stopped short of cosponsoring the most aggressive audit-the-Pentagon bills that would impose consequences for the Department of Defense's repeated failure to pass a clean audit [10].

Where Quigley gets quieter is instructive. He has not championed a lifetime lobbying ban for former members of Congress — a notable omission for someone who built his brand on ethics reform. He has not cosponsored congressional term limits legislation, which is perhaps unsurprising given that he has served eight terms himself [11]. And while he has supported campaign finance reform in principle, his own fundraising relies heavily on the system he says he wants to change. In his 2024 cycle, Quigley raised over $1.5 million, with a significant share coming from PACs, including those tied to the real estate, finance, and defense industries [12]. His top industry donors have included securities and investment firms, real estate interests, and lawyers and lobbyists — the very sectors that benefit most from the carried interest loophole and the revolving door he has declined to weld shut [13].

Quigley is the kind of Democrat who makes reform advocates hopeful and then quietly frustrated. He will cosponsor the transparency bill, vote the right way on drug pricing, and give a good speech about dark money — and he means it, mostly. But on the structural reforms that would actually cost him and his colleagues something — term limits, a lifetime lobbying ban, weaning off corporate PAC money — he is content to let the status quo stand. His record is genuinely better than average, which in Congress is a bit like being the tallest person in a room full of people sitting down.

--- R000122: Jack Reed (Democrat, RI, senate) ---
Jack Reed has been in the Senate since 1997 — nearly three decades — which means he has had roughly 29 years to fix everything he keeps saying is broken. A West Point graduate and former Army officer, Reed chairs the Senate Armed Services Committee, which is a bit like asking the fox to audit the henhouse, except in this case the henhouse has an $886 billion budget and the fox's top career donors include General Dynamics, Raytheon, Lockheed Martin, and Boeing. Since entering the Senate, Reed has collected nearly a million dollars from defense sector PACs and individuals. General Dynamics alone has funneled over $154,000 into his campaign committees. When asked about the Pentagon's inability to pass a single audit — ever — Reed compared it to "Waiting for Godot." Apparently the irony of the Armed Services chairman shrugging at the Pentagon's missing trillions while cashing defense contractor checks was lost on him.

To his credit, Reed supported the STOCK Act and has pushed for stronger SEC insider trading rules. He backed the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare drug negotiation provisions and co-introduced the SMART Prices Act to expand that authority. He is a vocal defender of Social Security, opposing privatization and benefit cuts. He co-sponsored the FAMILY Act for paid family leave. These are genuine points of alignment with the 80% Bill platform.

But here is where it gets complicated. Reed has been on the Banking Committee for his entire Senate career and watched the 2008 financial crisis unfold from a front-row seat. He has introduced legislation on predatory lending and insider trading, yet Wall Street's fundamental architecture remains largely intact. On the question of ending forever wars, Reed — the decorated veteran, the man who should know better than anyone the cost of military adventurism — has been reliably hawkish, voting to authorize force and consistently approving bloated defense budgets that exceed even what the Pentagon requests. His opposition to "increasing defense spending at the expense of domestic priorities" is a carefully worded nothing-burger when he keeps voting for the final package anyway.

Reed has not championed term limits (funny, that, from a 30-year incumbent), has no notable position on banning corporate home buying, and has been silent on the Epstein files. His net worth is modest — somewhere between negative $506,000 and $235,000 — which either makes him the most honest man in the Senate or the worst investor on the Armed Services Committee. Either way, after three decades of "fighting" for reform from inside the most powerful military oversight body on Earth, Rhode Island might reasonably ask: what exactly has changed?

--- R000305: Deborah K. Ross (Democrat, NC, house) ---
Rep. Deborah K. Ross is, on paper, one of the most aligned members of Congress with the 80% Bill's platform -- which makes the gaps in her record all the more worth examining. A former ACLU state director and ten-year veteran of the North Carolina General Assembly, Ross has represented NC-2 since 2021 and built a legislative identity around voting rights, patent reform, and civil liberties [1]. She has missed only 22 of 2,688 roll call votes since taking office -- a 0.8% absence rate that puts most of her colleagues to shame [2].

Ross voted for the Freedom to Vote: John R. Lewis Act (Article 8) and has been a leading voice against gerrymandering, introducing the End Prison Gerrymandering Act and the Redistricting Reform Act [3]. She has supported the DISCLOSE Act (Article 16) and measures to overturn Citizens United (Article 13). On voting rights, she walks the walk.

She has also cosponsored paid family leave legislation (Article 14), supported Social Security expansion (Article 11), and backed police body camera mandates (Article 12) [4]. Ross voted for the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare drug negotiation provisions, aligning her with Article 7. On domestic policy, she checks many of the right boxes.

But Ross's record is not without contradictions. Her deep involvement in patent reform -- she authored the PREVAIL Act to reshape the Patent Trial and Appeal Board -- has drawn her into close collaboration with pharmaceutical and technology industry interests [5]. Her top donors include law firms and corporate PACs [6], and while she has supported some campaign finance reform measures, she has not cosponsored legislation to ban corporate PACs outright (Article 5). The question of whether patent reform strengthens innovators or entrenches pharmaceutical monopolies is one Ross has consistently answered in favor of the patent holders.

On foreign policy, Ross has been largely silent on AUMF repeal (Article 2) and Pentagon audit requirements (Article 6) [7]. The lifetime lobbying ban (Article 3) and congressional term limits (Article 20) have not appeared on her legislative agenda. And while she represents the Research Triangle -- home to some of America's most aggressive data collectors -- she has not championed comprehensive data privacy legislation modeled on GDPR (Article 21) [8].

Ross is proof that alignment is not the same as leadership. She votes correctly on many 80% Bill priorities, but rarely puts her name on the hardest fights -- the ones that would upset the donor class that funds Democratic incumbents as reliably as Republican ones [9].

--- R000395: Harold Rogers (Republican, KY, house) ---
Representative Harold "Hal" Rogers has served Kentucky's 5th Congressional District since 1981, making him the longest-serving member of Congress from the Commonwealth and, since 2022, the Dean of the entire U.S. House of Representatives. If the 80% Bill is a test of whether Washington will do what voters overwhelmingly want, Rogers is a walking case study in why it will not. In 45 years in office, the man once crowned the "Prince of Pork" by Citizens Against Government Waste has never met a federal dollar he could not redirect to eastern Kentucky -- or to the contractors who donate to keep him there [1][2].

Rogers spent four decades on the House Appropriations Committee, including a stint as its chairman from 2011 to 2016, and has served on the Defense Subcommittee where the real money lives [3]. He has steered hundreds of millions in earmarks to his district: 137 earmarks worth $251.9 million in just three years, according to one tally, including a $341 million concrete wall to fix a leaking dam and a $5 million wildlife conservation grant that happened to benefit a Namibia-based organization employing his daughter [4]. In January 2026, he applauded a funding package with nearly $40 million in community project funding for eastern Kentucky [5]. The district benefits, the donors benefit, and Rogers wins reelection by forty points. It is a closed loop.

On the 80% Bill issues, Rogers is functionally absent. He has never co-sponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, impose term limits, require the DISCLOSE Act, or ban corporate PACs. He voted with every other Republican present to block dark money disclosure when the DISCLOSE Act came to the House floor [6]. He has introduced no legislation on paid family leave, junk fees, the right to repair, or corporate home buying. On term limits -- a policy supported by over 80 percent of Americans -- Rogers's 45-year tenure is its own rebuttal. He introduced the fewest bills of any member of the Kentucky delegation in 2024, suggesting that at this point in his career, legislation is simply not the point [7].

The money tells the familiar story. Rogers's top career donors include defense contractors Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, and United Technologies, alongside corporate interests that benefit directly from the appropriations he controls [8]. In 2009, Citizens for Responsibility and Ethics in Washington found that Rogers steered roughly $30 million in earmarks to companies whose employees had contributed $48,000 to his political committees, earning him a spot on their Most Corrupt Members of Congress list [9]. The New York Times reported that he used his chairmanship of the Homeland Security Appropriations Subcommittee to push the federal government into testing older technology at a plant in his district, with $100,000 in contributions flowing from parties tied to the effort [10].

The bottom line on Hal Rogers is arithmetic. He has been in Congress longer than many of his constituents have been alive. He sits on the committee that writes the checks, takes money from the companies that cash them, and has never once supported the transparency or structural reforms that Americans overwhelmingly demand. The Prince of Pork does not need the 80% Bill; the 80% Bill needs to retire him.

--- R000575: Mike Rogers (Republican, AL, house) ---
Mike Rogers has represented Alabama's 3rd Congressional District since 2003 — over two decades of public service, give or take a few hundred defense contractor fundraisers. A lawyer from Calhoun County who once served as the youngest member of the Calhoun County Commission, Rogers climbed from the Alabama state legislature to become one of the most powerful figures in American military policy, currently serving as Chairman of the House Armed Services Committee[1][2]. He ran unopposed in 2024, collecting 97.9% of the vote[3].

As Armed Services Chairman, Rogers presides over the largest military budget in human history, a stewardship he approaches with the boundless appetite of a man who has never met a weapons line-item he didn't love. He championed $150 billion in new Pentagon defense spending through reconciliation in 2025[4]. Meanwhile, the Pentagon has failed its audit for the eighth consecutive year — the only federal agency in American history never to pass one — and Rogers' committee appears less interested in accountability than in appropriations[6]. On fair elections, Rogers voted against the For the People Act in 2021, calling it the "For the Politicians Act" — an ironic turn of phrase from a man who has benefited from Alabama's court-challenged congressional maps[7][8]. On the DISCLOSE Act, Rogers voted no[9]. On Medicare drug price negotiation, he has been consistently opposed[10]. In 2025, he voted for the "One Big Beautiful Bill," a reconciliation package that cut over $1 trillion from health programs while accelerating the insolvency of the Medicare and Social Security trust funds[11][12].

Follow the money and the picture snaps into focus. Since 2002, Rogers has received more than $2 million from the defense sector — more than any other House member in the 2022 election cycle, when defense industry cash made up 22% of his total fundraising[13][14]. He is the top House recipient of Lockheed Martin contributions, a company with multiple manufacturing facilities in his district including the Javelin missile production line[13]. He spent years fighting to relocate U.S. Space Command's permanent headquarters from Colorado to Alabama's Redstone Arsenal — using committee leverage to advance what critics noted had a decidedly parochial dimension[15]. He has consistently voted against measures that would expose who funds American politics[7][9].

After twenty-two years and a career defined more by what he blocked than what he built, Rogers represents the armed and funded establishment wing of American conservatism — a man whose constituents overwhelmingly support drug price negotiation, fair elections, Pentagon accountability, and campaign finance transparency, and whose voting record opposes every one of them. He is not a congressman who serves the 80%. He is a congressman who serves the 22% — the fraction of his fundraising that comes from an industry whose budget he controls and whose audits he overlooks.

--- R000579: Patrick Ryan (Democrat, NY, house) ---
Pat Ryan is the rare congressman who actually showed up to Washington with a plan to clean it up — and then introduced six bills to prove it. The West Point grad and two-tour Iraq combat veteran sits on the House Armed Services Committee, co-founded the Democratic Veterans Caucus, and has built a legislative brand around the word "anti-corruption" with an enthusiasm that borders on suspicious for anyone who works in Congress.

His TRUST in Congress Act would ban members, their spouses, and dependent children from trading individual stocks — forcing divestiture or blind trusts for the duration of service. His Close the Revolving Door Act slaps a lifetime lobbying ban on former members of Congress. He introduced the MUSK Act to prevent executive branch officials from making policy decisions that enrich their former employers. He even pushed a resolution to ban members from sitting on corporate boards. In 2023, he publicly called on House leadership to hold a vote on the stock trading ban that week — a move roughly as popular with leadership as a fire alarm during recess.

On housing, Ryan introduced a four-bill package to crack down on Wall Street firms driving up costs in the Hudson Valley, where home prices have surged 47% since 2020. He supports paid family leave — and actually implemented it at his own tech company before running for office. He backs the REPAIR Infrastructure Act. He has championed dairy farmers and farmworker housing, securing $1 million for the Chester Agricultural Center.

So where is the catch? Ryan raised $5.5 million in the 2024 cycle. His OpenSecrets page does not show a formal no-corporate-PAC pledge, and while he talks a big anti-corruption game, the question of whether any corporate PAC dollars slip through the side door remains worth watching. His Armed Services seat also means he is swimming in the same defense contractor waters he pledges to oversee — a tension as old as the committee itself.

Still, among House Democrats, Ryan is one of the more credible reformers: a veteran who walked the walk on anti-corruption legislation, pushed leadership publicly on stock trading, and introduced bills that address at least eight of the twenty 80% Bill policies. The Hudson Valley could do worse. It usually has.

--- R000584: James E. Risch (Republican, ID, senate) ---
James Elroy Risch has represented Idaho in the United States Senate since 2009, having previously served as the state's governor, lieutenant governor, and a fixture in the Idaho state legislature going back to 1974 [1]. A self-made millionaire trial lawyer turned rancher-politician, Risch chairs the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and sits on the Energy and Natural Resources, Intelligence, Small Business, and Ethics committees [2]. With an estimated net worth between $19 million and $88 million — much of it in Idaho farm and ranch land — he is comfortably one of the wealthiest members of Congress [3]. He is, by any measure, the embodiment of the Idaho Republican establishment.

On the 80% Bill issues, Risch's record is a study in selective populism. He has cosponsored the SOIL Act to restrict foreign purchases of American farmland, a genuine point of alignment with the bill's "Protect US Farmland" plank [4]. But that is roughly where the overlap ends. As Foreign Relations chairman, Risch voted against the bipartisan 2023 repeal of the Iraq War AUMFs, arguing that repealing the 2002 authorization would "send the wrong message" — even as 66 senators, including 18 Republicans, disagreed [5]. He voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which contained Medicare drug price negotiation provisions, calling it a "Democrat spending bill" that would "stifle pharmaceutical innovation" [6]. He has shown no interest in banning congressional stock trading, despite actively trading stocks himself — Quiver Quantitative estimated he made $116,000 in the stock market in a single recent month [7]. He introduced legislation to repeal the Corporate Transparency Act, moving in the opposite direction of the DISCLOSE Act's goals [8]. Senate Republicans, Risch among them, unanimously blocked the DISCLOSE Act in September 2022 [9].

Risch opposed the FIRST STEP Act, the landmark bipartisan criminal justice reform bill that passed 87-12 in 2018, placing him in a small minority even within his own party [10]. On paid family leave, junk fees, right to repair, term limits, and banning corporate PACs, his legislative record is one of conspicuous silence. He has not cosponsored any major legislation on these fronts.

The money tells its own story. Risch's top contributing industries over his career include oil and gas, securities and investment, and defense — sectors with a vested interest in the status quo on tax loopholes, Pentagon spending, and deregulation [11]. His PAC donors include Curtiss-Wright Corporation, Honeywell International, Marathon Petroleum, and NextEra Energy [12]. He has received substantial contributions from lobbyists throughout his tenure [13]. For a man who owns 284 acres of Idaho ranch land and sits on the Energy Committee overseeing oil and gas leasing on public lands, the alignment between portfolio, donors, and policy positions is remarkably tidy.

Risch is the rare senator who can claim alignment with one popular plank of the 80% Bill — protecting farmland — while opposing or ignoring the other nineteen. His record suggests a politician whose populist instincts activate precisely when they coincide with his own substantial agricultural holdings, and go dormant the moment they might inconvenience a donor.

--- R000599: Raul Ruiz (Democrat, CA, house) ---
Raul Ruiz has represented California's Inland Empire in Congress since 2013, currently serving the 25th District after redistricting. He is an emergency medicine physician with a medical degree and a master's in public policy from Harvard and a master's in public health from the same institution -- credentials he deploys with the frequency of a cardiologist checking a pulse [1][2]. Before Congress, he started the Coachella Valley Healthcare Initiative and served as associate dean at UC Riverside's School of Medicine [3]. He sits on the powerful House Energy and Commerce Committee, including the Health Subcommittee, making him one of the most strategically positioned Democrats on healthcare legislation in the entire House [4]. He has been in Congress for over a decade, long enough to have a record that speaks clearly -- and what it says is more complicated than his campaign website suggests.

On several planks of the 80% Bill, Ruiz has genuine legislative receipts. He voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which for the first time empowered Medicare to negotiate prescription drug prices -- a centerpiece of the 80% Bill's healthcare plank [5][6]. He cosponsored the Elijah E. Cummings Lower Drug Costs Now Act before that [7]. He has consistently cosponsored the Social Security 2100 Act, which would expand benefits and shore up the program's finances [8]. He was endorsed by the End Citizens United PAC, signaling at least nominal alignment with campaign finance reform, and he voted with all House Democrats for the For the People Act (HR 1), which incorporated the DISCLOSE Act's dark money transparency provisions and anti-gerrymandering reforms [9][10]. He even co-introduced a bill with Reps. Quigley and Aguilar to close tax loopholes for luxury yacht owners -- not quite the carried interest loophole, but proof that he understands the concept of a rich person paying less than they should [11].

But the gaps are telling. Ruiz has not cosponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, impose a lifetime lobbying ban, establish term limits, or audit the Pentagon -- four of the 80% Bill's most popular reforms [12][13]. On ending forever wars, he has no notable record on AUMF repeal despite sponsoring 23% of his bills in the armed forces and national security space [14]. He has not signed onto bills banning corporate purchase of single-family homes or protecting U.S. farmland from foreign acquisition. For a twelve-year incumbent who sponsors bills "primarily in health," his silence on the structural governance reforms that would constrain the very industries funding his campaigns is conspicuous.

And those industries are generous. Health professionals contributed $518,967 to Ruiz in the 2024 cycle alone, making it his top industry by a wide margin [15]. PAC contributions account for 44.57% of his total fundraising, with large individual contributions making up another 48.86% -- meaning small donors under $200 contributed just 6.57% of his war chest [15]. He sits on the committee that regulates pharmaceutical companies, insurance giants, and healthcare conglomerates, and those same sectors write him six-figure checks cycle after cycle. He also maintains his own leadership PAC, DR RAUL PAC, which raised $345,605 in the 2024 cycle [16]. This is a congressman who talks about lowering drug costs while cashing checks from the people who set them.

Ruiz is not a villain in this story -- he has cast real votes that helped real people afford their prescriptions. But the 80% Bill asks a harder question: will you support the structural reforms that would make those good votes unnecessary, by removing the corrupting influence of money, secrecy, and incumbency from the system altogether? On that question, Dr. Ruiz has prescribed himself a heavy dose of silence.

--- R000600: Aumua Amata Coleman Radewagen (Republican, AS, house) ---
Aumua Amata Coleman Radewagen has represented American Samoa as its non-voting delegate since 2015, making her the first woman elected to the role and one of the longest-serving territorial delegates in Congress [1]. A Republican in a territory that votes overwhelmingly Democratic in presidential elections, Radewagen occupies a peculiar niche: she can sit on committees, introduce bills, and give speeches, but when the House holds a final vote on legislation, her voice evaporates into procedural silence [2]. She serves on the Veterans' Affairs Committee and the Small Business Committee, where she holds Vice Ranking Member roles on both [3]. Her 2020 GovTrack report card ranked her the 28th most bipartisan member of the House [4], which sounds impressive until you remember she cannot actually vote on anything that matters on the floor.

The defining issue of Radewagen's tenure is the survival of American Samoa's tuna canning industry, specifically the StarKist cannery in Pago Pago, which employs roughly 2,000 people and accounts for about 80 percent of the territory's private-sector jobs [5]. She has introduced the American Samoa Economic Development Credit bill (H.R. 399) in multiple Congresses, lobbying for tax incentives to keep StarKist from decamping to cheaper operations in Asia [6]. On the 80% Bill's twenty policy priorities, her record is largely a blank page. There is no public evidence she has cosponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, impose a lobbying ban, audit the Pentagon, ban corporate PACs, ban dark money, enact term limits, or address most of the other proposals that command supermajority public support. Her legislative focus has remained tightly parochial, centered on territorial economic relief and Pacific regional affairs [7].

Perhaps the most telling number in Radewagen's congressional career is her attendance record. From January 2019 to January 2026, she missed 624 of 919 roll call votes, a 67.9 percent absentee rate that makes her the most frequently absent member of the House [8]. The geographic reality is brutal: American Samoa sits roughly 7,700 miles from Washington, D.C., and there are no direct flights. But as a non-voting delegate, there is a certain dark comedy in missing votes you were never going to be allowed to cast anyway. Her office has not publicly explained the absences in detail, and GovTrack notes it does not track the reasons behind missed votes [8].

Radewagen's campaign finance profile is modest by congressional standards. Her fundraising has historically been small-scale, befitting a delegate from a territory of roughly 55,000 people [9]. Her top legislative priority, the economic development tax credit, directly benefits the tuna canning industry that dominates American Samoa's economy [6]. Whether that constitutes a conflict of interest or simply competent constituent service depends on your tolerance for a member of Congress whose entire legislative identity revolves around keeping one factory open in a territory whose residents cannot vote for president and whose delegate cannot vote on the House floor. The 80% Bill asks whether members of Congress side with the people or with entrenched interests; Radewagen's record mostly asks whether anyone is paying attention at all.

--- R000603: David Rouzer (Republican, NC, house) ---
Rep. David Rouzer has represented North Carolina's 7th District since 2015, and his career offers a masterclass in the art of ignoring science while demanding federal money to clean up the consequences. As a state senator, Rouzer was the driving force behind North Carolina's infamous 2012 sea-level rise law -- HB 819 -- which banned state agencies from using scientific models predicting accelerated sea-level rise in coastal planning [1]. The chair of the real estate lobby group that pushed the bill declared "God bless him" when it passed [2]. A decade later, after Hurricanes Florence and Isaias hammered the very coast he had legislatively blinded, Rouzer pivoted to demanding restructured FEMA disaster funding [3]. The ocean, it turns out, did not read HB 819.

On January 6, 2021, Rouzer was one of 147 Republican lawmakers who objected to the certification of the presidential election results, citing "unanswered questions" about election procedures in Arizona and Pennsylvania [4]. This places him in direct opposition to the 80% Bill's Article 8 on fair elections. He has shown no support for the Freedom to Vote Act, the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act, or any anti-gerrymandering measures.

Rouzer sits on the House Agriculture Committee, where his top donors -- Live Oak Bank ($16,850), National Chicken Council ($7,500), and assorted agribusiness PACs -- have a direct interest in the policies he oversees [5]. He has not supported the Protect US Farmland Act (Article 9), despite representing a heavily agricultural district. On banning corporate PACs (Article 5), the DISCLOSE Act (Article 16), and overturning Citizens United (Article 13), Rouzer has been a reliable "no."

His record on the remaining 80% Bill articles is a near-complete blank. No support for congressional stock trading bans (Article 1), no interest in Pentagon audits (Article 6), no movement on Medicare drug negotiation (Article 7), no cosponsorship of paid family leave (Article 14), term limits (Article 20), or junk fee bans (Article 19) [6]. He has opposed taxing the ultra-wealthy (Article 4) and closing the carried interest loophole (Article 17).

Rouzer's founding membership in the Congressional Coastal Communities Caucus is admirable in theory, but it is hard to take coastal advocacy seriously from the man who literally made it illegal for North Carolina to plan for rising seas [7]. His district deserves a representative who believes in science before the hurricane hits, not after.
