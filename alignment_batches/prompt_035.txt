--- M001220: Morgan McGarvey (Democrat, KY, house) ---
Morgan McGarvey has represented Kentucky's 3rd Congressional District -- the Louisville area -- since 2023, arriving in Washington as a former Kentucky State Senate Minority Leader with a reputation as a coalition-builder in a deep-red legislature. A Democrat in a safely blue urban district, McGarvey has positioned himself as a reliable progressive vote on most 80% Bill issues, earning a perfect 100 percent score from the AFL-CIO in 2024 and a perfect pro-retiree rating from the Alliance for Retired Americans in 2023 [1][2]. The question, as always with freshman Democrats who talk a good game, is whether the walk matches the talk.

On transparency and anti-corruption issues, McGarvey's record is substantive. He cosponsored the Bipartisan Restoring Faith in Government Act and the TRUST in Congress Act, both of which would ban members of Congress from trading or owning individual stocks [3]. He has advocated for police reform through the George Floyd Justice in Policing Act, including a national ban on no-knock warrants -- a particularly resonant issue in Louisville, where Breonna Taylor was killed during a botched no-knock raid [4]. On the DOJ consent decree for Louisville Metro Police, McGarvey has been vocal in defending the agreement against efforts to dismantle it, arguing it makes all Louisvillians safer [4]. His support for paid family leave, subsidized childcare, and universal pre-K aligns him with several planks of the 80% Bill platform [5].

Where McGarvey gets more complicated is in the gap between his stated principles and the messy realities of congressional fundraising. AIPAC was listed as the top contributor to his campaign in the 2024 cycle, channeling $49,003 in associated funds, on top of $17,950 in his 2022 race [6]. To his credit, McGarvey announced he would no longer accept AIPAC-associated donations -- a move that suggests some willingness to walk away from easy money when the political winds shift [7]. His total fundraising of $1.54 million in the 2024 cycle is modest by congressional standards, and his top industry donors skew toward lawyers and law firms rather than the fossil fuel and financial sector heavyweights that dominate many colleagues' donor rolls [6].

McGarvey has also shown some willingness to buck institutional inertia. He has sponsored or cosponsored 41 bills since taking office, attracting 374 cosponsors from 173 unique members -- and earned recognition for getting the most bipartisan cosponsors of any member of the Kentucky delegation in 2024 [8]. He secured over $245 million in federal grants for Louisville and brought home $12 million in direct community funding, suggesting he is focused on tangible results rather than mere messaging [5].

The bottom line on Morgan McGarvey is that he is a freshman Democrat who checks most of the 80% Bill boxes on paper -- stock trading ban, police accountability, paid leave, workers' rights -- and has begun to put legislative cosponsorship behind those positions. His AIPAC reversal suggests a capacity for course correction, though his relatively short tenure means the full picture of who he serves is still developing. Louisville could do worse, but the standard should be higher than "could do worse."

The campaign was shadowed by allegations from ex-girlfriend Stephanie Grisham, the former White House Press Secretary, who accused Miller of physical abuse during their relationship from 2019 to 2020 [7]. His stated priorities include border security, workforce development, and supporting Ohio's farmers and rural communities [2]. His top donor is a Wall Street private equity giant [5], making support for taxing the ultra-wealthy, closing carried interest loopholes, or banning corporate home purchases a conflict of financial interest. He has expressed generic support for term limits on the campaign trail [2], though whether the grandson of a real estate mogul who was installed in a congressional seat by a former president and a private equity firm truly wants to limit anyone's time in power remains, at best, an open question [9].

--- M001223: Seth Magaziner (Democrat, RI, house) ---
Seth Magaziner is that rare creature in Congress: a Wall Street Democrat who actually tries to bite the hand that fed him -- or at least gums it a little. Before winning Rhode Island's 2nd District, Magaziner served as State Treasurer, where he managed public pension funds with the kind of enthusiasm that made hedge fund managers send thank-you cards and campaign checks.

As Treasurer, Magaziner steered Rhode Island's retirement fund deeper into alternative investments -- hedge funds, private equity, the whole casino. Wall Street fees doubled on his watch, hitting $187 million in a single year and totaling roughly $620 million during his tenure. The returns for teachers and firefighters? Subpar. The returns for Magaziner's campaign account? Spectacular -- $178,000 from the financial sector flowed into his treasurer races, and over $200,000 into his congressional war chest. Nautic Partners, a private equity firm that received $95 million in additional pension commitments under Magaziner, saw its leadership's family members contribute $17,800 to his campaigns. Nothing to see here, folks.

But here's where it gets interesting. Magaziner has become one of Congress's loudest voices on banning congressional stock trading. He co-authored the TRUST in Congress Act and partnered with Republican Chip Roy on the Restore Trust in Congress Act, which attracted 119 bipartisan cosponsors. He's taken the House floor to demand votes on the ban, called Elon Musk "an unelected, unvetted, unconfirmed man-child billionaire," and introduced the Protected Time Off Act guaranteeing workers paid leave. The man has range.

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Magaziner checks some real boxes. Stock trading ban? He's literally the lead Democratic sponsor. Paid family leave? He introduced legislation for it. Protecting Social Security? A campaign cornerstone. But on darker territory -- auditing the Pentagon, banning dark money, closing tax loopholes for the ultra-wealthy -- his record thins out. He hasn't cosponsored the DISCLOSE Act. For a former treasurer who knows exactly how money moves through opaque financial structures, that silence is deafening.

Magaziner represents the Democratic Party's central tension: a politician who can rail against corruption while his own career was built on the cozy relationship between public money and private profit. He's fighting some of the right fights now, which is more than most can say. Whether that's genuine conviction or a savvy rebrand from the guy who charged retirees half a billion in Wall Street fees -- well, that's for Rhode Island voters to decide.

--- M001224: Nathaniel Moran (Republican, TX, house) ---

Moran has used this perch to do precisely none of that. And he serves on the Select Committee on Strategic Competition with China, where he can talk tough about foreign threats while ignoring the domestic ones sitting in his own committee room.

He voted against the Fiscal Responsibility Act in 2023, not because it failed to address inequality, but because it did not cut spending deeply enough. That tells you everything about where his instincts lie.

East Texas voters in TX-1 support all 20 policies in the 80% Bill by wide margins. They want their congressman to ban stock trading, close tax loopholes, and make corporations pay their share. Instead, they got a man who sits on the committee that writes the tax code and has done nothing to change it. Moran has missed only 14 of 1,690 roll call votes — a 99.2% attendance record. He shows up every day. He just does not show up for his constituents.

--- M001225: Kevin Mullin (Democrat, CA, house) ---
Kevin Mullin has represented California's 15th Congressional District -- the San Francisco Peninsula and Silicon Valley corridor -- since 2023. A fourth-generation San Mateo County native, Mullin is the son of Gene Mullin, a three-term state assemblyman and high school government teacher, making politics less a career choice than a family inheritance [1]. Before Congress, Mullin served in the California State Assembly, where he rose to Speaker pro Tempore. He now sits on the powerful House Energy and Commerce Committee, overseeing healthcare, telecommunications, and consumer protection -- a jurisdiction that happens to cover every major industry headquartered in his district, from Meta to YouTube to the biotech firms lining the Peninsula [2][3].

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Mullin checks an impressive number of boxes -- at least on paper. He authored California's first-in-the-nation DISCLOSE Act while in the state legislature, requiring political ads to reveal their funders, and has carried that crusade to Washington as a proud original cosponsor of the federal DISCLOSE Act and co-chair of the Congressional Task Force on Strengthening Democracy [4][5]. He cosponsored H.J.Res.13, a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United, and co-led the Lobbyist Loophole Closure Act to force anyone engaged in paid lobbying to register as such [6][7]. He championed the Freedom to Vote Act, which includes provisions to end partisan gerrymandering [8]. On healthcare, Mullin supports Medicare drug price negotiation and is a member of the Medicare for All Caucus [9]. He has called America's lack of paid family leave a national embarrassment, noting the U.S. is "the only developed nation whose citizens are not federally entitled" to it [10]. He voted yes on the War Powers Resolution asserting congressional authority over military action in Iran, signaling support for reining in the forever-war AUMFs [11]. And he introduced legislation requiring federal law enforcement officers to wear body cameras and visibly identify themselves and their agency [12].

Where it gets interesting is the gap between Mullin's reform rhetoric and the financial ecosystem that sustains him. His 2022 race saw AIPAC spend roughly $600,000 on his behalf in the general election [13]. His district is, by his own description, "the epicenter of life sciences, home to technology like YouTube and Meta" -- and his appointment to Energy and Commerce puts him in direct regulatory oversight of those same companies [3]. The Mullin Victory Fund raised nearly $1.6 million in the 2023-2024 cycle [14]. He has cosponsored 943 bills in three years, a volume that suggests either superhuman legislative ambition or the kind of strategic co-sponsorship that lets you claim credit without bearing risk [1]. For a man who wants to close lobbying loopholes and end dark money, the question is whether his committee perch will be used to regulate the tech and pharma giants in his backyard or to ensure they have a sympathetic ear on the dais.

The bottom line on Kevin Mullin is that he is a Democrat who says the right things on nearly every plank of the 80% Bill -- campaign finance transparency, lobbying reform, gerrymandering, drug prices, paid leave, body cameras. His legislative history on disclosure and dark money is genuinely ahead of most of his colleagues. But representing Silicon Valley while sitting on the committee that regulates Silicon Valley is a conflict of interest that writes itself, and until his reform energy produces results that cost his donors something, the DISCLOSE Act champion remains, ironically, the member whose own financial entanglements could use a bit more disclosure.
