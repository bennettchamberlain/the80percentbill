--- M000133: Edward J. Markey (Democrat, MA, senate) ---
Edward J. Markey has been in Congress so long that when he first took office, the internet did not exist, rotary phones were standard issue, and the phrase "children's online privacy" would have sounded like science fiction. Elected to the House in 1976 at age thirty and promoted to the Senate in a 2013 special election, Markey is the longest-tenured House member ever elevated to the upper chamber — a distinction that speaks either to remarkable staying power or to Massachusetts voters' deep affection for men who refuse to retire [1]. Along the way, he has authored more telecommunications law than most Americans realize exists, including the V-Chip provision of the 1996 Telecom Act (he coined the term), the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act of 1998, and the E-Rate program that wired schools and libraries to the internet [2].

Markey's second act as a senator has been defined by climate ambition. In 2019, he partnered with Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez to introduce the Green New Deal, a resolution so sweeping that it made his earlier co-authorship of the 2009 Waxman-Markey cap-and-trade bill — the only comprehensive climate legislation ever to pass a chamber of Congress — look like a modest suggestion [3]. The Green New Deal also handed him an unlikely political lifeline: when Representative Joe Kennedy III challenged him in the 2020 Democratic primary, Markey wielded the resolution like a shield, rallied the Sunrise Movement and young progressives to his banner, and became the first person to defeat a Kennedy in a Massachusetts election. He celebrated by declaring that "the age of incrementalism is over," which, coming from a man who had spent forty-four years in incremental legislating, carried a certain dramatic irony [4].

On the issues that animate the 80% Bill, Markey's record is extensive and largely aligned. He has championed Medicare drug price negotiation, co-sponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, and pushed for a national climate bank to accelerate clean energy investment [5]. His COPPA 2.0 legislation, which would extend online privacy protections to teenagers, passed the Senate 91-3 in 2024 before dying in the House — a fate familiar to many Markey initiatives that enjoy overwhelming public support yet somehow cannot clear every procedural hurdle Washington has invented [6].

Markey co-founded the Bipartisan Congressional Privacy Caucus in 1999 and has been described by the International Association of Privacy Professionals as "quite possibly the most influential member of the House in the history of privacy legislation" [7]. He has also been a reliable vote for Pentagon spending accountability, lobbying reform, and expanding the social safety net. At seventy-nine, he remains a senator who treats policy papers the way other politicians treat fundraising calls — as the actual point of the job. Whether that makes him an idealist or simply someone who has been around long enough to remember when legislating was the expectation rather than the exception is a question best left to historians, or possibly to the interns who maintain his remarkably active social media presence [8].

--- M000194: Nancy Mace (Republican, SC, house) ---
Nancy Mace has turned performative outrage into an art form so refined it belongs in a gallery — preferably one she can expense to taxpayers. The South Carolina Republican arrived in Congress branding herself as a maverick truth-teller, and to her credit, she once signed a discharge petition to force a vote on banning congressional stock trading, noting that her colleagues "vanish rather than upset House leadership" when asked to support it. It was a rare moment of alignment with the 80% Bill. It did not last.

Mace's tenure has been defined less by policy and more by spectacle. Her signature legislative achievement was a 2024 bathroom bill explicitly targeting incoming Rep. Sarah McBride, the first openly transgender member of Congress. Mace admitted on camera that it "absolutely" targeted McBride personally. Elon Musk cheered her on with a meme. It was the kind of culture-war theater designed to generate cable news hits, not solve problems that keep South Carolinians up at night — like healthcare costs, housing affordability, or junk fees draining their bank accounts.

On the kitchen-table issues that define the 80% Bill, Mace's record ranges from silent to hostile. Ban corporate PACs? Her leadership PAC, "Must Act to Create Excellence," rakes in corporate money with a name that sounds like it was generated by a motivational poster algorithm. Audit the Pentagon? End dark money? Paid family leave? These topics do not trend on social media, so they do not appear to exist in her legislative universe.

Meanwhile, the House Ethics Committee opened an investigation in March 2026 into allegations that Mace overcharged congressional reimbursement programs by roughly $9,500 for her D.C. housing — a residence she co-owned with her ex-fiance. She dismissed the probe, but the irony of a self-styled fiscal hawk allegedly padding her own expense reports writes itself. She has missed 3.1% of roll call votes, worse than the median for House members, raising the question she herself posed in a December 2025 interview: "What's the point of Congress?"

Good question. For Mace, the point appears to be building a personal brand while the 80% of policies Americans actually agree on — term limits, banning dark money, taxing the ultra-wealthy, protecting Social Security — gather dust. South Carolina's 1st District deserves a representative who fights for their priorities, not one who fights for followers.

--- M000312: James P. McGovern (Democrat, MA, house) ---
James P. McGovern's path to Congress began not in a campaign office but in a war zone. As a senior aide to Representative Joe Moakley in the 1980s, McGovern was dispatched to El Salvador to investigate the 1989 murder of six Jesuit priests, their housekeeper, and her teenage daughter by a U.S.-backed military unit. The resulting Moakley Commission report, largely assembled by McGovern, exposed the Salvadoran military's complicity and helped shift American foreign policy toward conditioning aid on human rights — a diplomatic novelty that, in retrospect, seems like it should have been the default setting all along [1]. That investigation forged the central conviction of McGovern's career: that governments should be held accountable for what they do with power, whether the power is exercised with rifles in Central America or with parliamentary procedure in Washington.

McGovern won his Worcester-based House seat in 1996 by defeating a Republican incumbent, and has held it with the kind of margins that suggest his constituents either genuinely admire him or have simply lost track of the alternatives. The Americans for Democratic Action gave him an average rating of 98.5 percent over his first decade in office, while the American Conservative Union awarded him a generous 2.5 percent — numbers that place him roughly where you would expect a Massachusetts Democrat who once worked for a man named McGovern (no relation to the 1972 presidential candidate, though the ideological confusion has surely not hurt) [2]. He has been a member of the Congressional Progressive Caucus since its inception and has never displayed much interest in the bipartisan centrism that editorial boards love to recommend and voters love to ignore.

What distinguishes McGovern from the broader progressive caucus is his combination of procedural power and single-issue tenacity. As chairman of the House Rules Committee from 2019 to 2023 — and now its ranking Democrat — he occupied what is often called the most powerful committee in Congress, the body that determines which bills reach the floor and under what conditions [3]. He used that perch not merely to advance the party's legislative agenda but to hold hearings on hunger in America, a cause he has pursued with the fervor of a man who genuinely cannot understand why the wealthiest nation in history allows millions of its citizens to skip meals. As co-chair of the House Hunger Caucus, he led the creation of a global school feeding program that now serves millions of children in developing countries and has been one of SNAP's most aggressive defenders against periodic Republican attempts to cut the program through the farm bill [4].

McGovern's alignment with the 80% Bill's priorities is broad and consistent. He has supported Medicare drug price negotiation, banning congressional stock trading, eliminating dark money in elections, and mandating police body cameras — positions that poll well with the public and poorly with the donor class [5]. His co-chairmanship of the Tom Lantos Human Rights Commission and his leadership on the Congressional-Executive Commission on China reflect an internationalist streak that connects his El Salvador origins to present-day concerns about authoritarian governance and labor rights [6].

At sixty-six, McGovern remains one of the House's most reliably progressive votes, which in Massachusetts's 2nd District is less a political risk than a job requirement. His career arc — from investigating atrocities in a civil war to managing floor procedure in the people's chamber — is unusual enough to suggest that the route from moral clarity to legislative influence does not always require the usual stops at fundraising dinners and cable news greenrooms. Whether that route is replicable or simply a product of Worcester's particular political ecosystem is an open question, but McGovern's near-three-decade tenure suggests the voters, at least, are satisfied with the results [7].

--- M000317: Nicole Malliotakis (Republican, NY, house) ---
Nicole Malliotakis represents Staten Island and southern Brooklyn on the House Ways and Means Committee, where she occupies the rare position of being the only New York City Republican on the panel that writes America's tax code. She has used this perch to champion the SALT deduction cap increase -- a genuinely popular cause in her district -- while reliably blocking the other nineteen things her constituents actually want.

Take the congressional stock trading ban. Malliotakis has said publicly that "prohibiting trades or requiring a blind trust while in office makes sense." Stirring words. On March 17, 2023, she purchased between $1,000 and $15,000 in New York Community Bancorp shares. Two days later, NYCB's subsidiary was announced as the buyer of 40 Signature Bank branches. The stock jumped 32% overnight. In the days leading up to the trade, Malliotakis had been in direct contact with federal and state regulators about Signature's collapse. Her office said a financial adviser recommended the purchase. The disclosure form listed the trade as made by her "spouse" -- though Malliotakis is unmarried. The Office of Congressional Ethics reviewed the matter and found no wrongdoing, which tells you everything you need to know about the Office of Congressional Ethics.

On Medicare drug negotiation, Malliotakis has introduced bills to reform pharmacy benefit managers and reduce prescription costs for seniors. She has also voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, the law that actually gave Medicare the power to negotiate drug prices for the first time in history. She was among 13 House Republicans who voted no while receiving pharmaceutical industry contributions. Her solution to high drug prices is apparently every reform except the one that worked.

Malliotakis voted to object to counting Arizona's and Pennsylvania's electoral votes on January 6, 2021, based on what even her own party's investigations later confirmed were disproven fraud claims -- not exactly a ringing endorsement of Fair Elections. On the Epstein files, she supported transparency "done in a responsible manner," which is the congressional equivalent of saying you support dinner but only if nobody has to cook.

She sits on Ways and Means but has shown no interest in closing tax loopholes for the ultra-wealthy, auditing the Pentagon, banning dark money, or passing the DISCLOSE Act. She helped pass the "One Big Beautiful Bill" that made Trump-era tax cuts permanent while Medicaid faced hundreds of billions in cuts -- a trade her Bay Ridge constituents rallying against those cuts in March 2025 apparently did not appreciate. On paid family leave, corporate PAC bans, term limits, lobbying reform, right to repair, and junk fee bans, her record ranges from silent to opposed. The 80% Bill asks for twenty popular policies. Malliotakis has managed to find reasons to avoid nearly all of them while trading bank stocks at suspiciously opportune moments.

--- M000355: Mitch McConnell (Republican, KY, senate) ---
Addison Mitchell McConnell III has represented Kentucky in the United States Senate since 1985, making him the longest-serving Senate party leader in American history. A Republican who rose from Jefferson County Judge-Executive to the pinnacle of congressional power, McConnell has spent four decades mastering the procedural levers of the Senate -- and, his critics would argue, using them almost exclusively to block the kinds of reforms that poll at 80 percent or higher with the American public. His personal net worth, estimated between $20 million and $96 million depending on the disclosure year, has grown considerably since he first arrived in Washington on a government salary [1].

On the 80% Bill issues that touch campaign finance and transparency, McConnell is not merely an opponent -- he is the opponent. He led multiple filibusters against the McCain-Feingold Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act, then personally sued the Federal Election Commission to overturn it in McConnell v. FEC [2]. When the Supreme Court's Citizens United decision gutted the law in 2010, McConnell celebrated it as a victory for the First Amendment, declaring that "the Supreme Court took an important step in the direction of restoring the First Amendment rights" of corporations [3]. He has blocked every iteration of the DISCLOSE Act since 2010, ensuring that dark money continues to flow unchecked into American elections [3]. On the congressional stock trading ban, McConnell has remained conspicuously noncommittal, declining to champion a measure that enjoys overwhelming bipartisan public support [4]. Term limits, fair elections, banning corporate PACs -- each of these proposals has found in McConnell a reliable and formidable adversary.

On economic populist issues, McConnell's record is equally telling. He called Social Security and Medicare "the real drivers of the debt" after shepherding a $1.5 trillion tax cut through the Senate in 2017 and signing off on a $675 billion defense budget [5]. He introduced the HEALS Act containing the TRUST Act, which critics described as a fast track for Social Security and Medicare benefit cuts [6]. Pentagon audits, taxing the ultra-wealthy, closing tax loopholes -- these proposals have found no champion in the senior senator from Kentucky, who has consistently prioritized corporate and donor interests over fiscal accountability measures that enjoy broad popular support.

The money tells its own story. McConnell raised nearly $24 million in the 2019-2024 cycle alone, but that figure vastly understates his financial orbit [1]. The Senate Leadership Fund, a super PAC run by his allies, received $67.4 million in contributions and spent over $211 million in the 2024 cycle [7]. One Nation, a dark money organization run by a former McConnell chief of staff, has funneled $115 million in undisclosed donations to support his political network [8]. Top donors to his aligned groups include Chevron, the American Petroleum Institute, Koch Industries, and the National Association of Realtors -- precisely the industries that benefit most from blocking the 80% Bill agenda [8].

The bottom line on Mitch McConnell is this: no single legislator has done more to ensure that popular, bipartisan reforms -- on dark money, on campaign finance, on corporate influence in politics -- remain permanently stalled. He is not a passive obstacle; he is an architect of obstruction, having literally taken his fight against transparency all the way to the Supreme Court. For the 80% Bill, McConnell is not just a "no" vote -- he is the reason many of these measures never reach a vote at all.

--- M000687: Kweisi Mfume (Democrat, MD, house) ---
Kweisi Mfume's life story is, by any honest measure, one of the more remarkable arcs in American politics. Born Frizzell Gerard Gray in Baltimore in 1948, he dropped out of high school at sixteen after his mother died, working three jobs to support his sisters while drifting into gang life on Baltimore's corners [1]. He fathered five children as a teenager, racked up arrests, and by most indicators was headed nowhere good. Then he turned it around so completely that the transformation reads like fiction: he earned his GED, graduated magna cum laude from Morgan State University, picked up a master's in international studies from Johns Hopkins, changed his name to Kweisi Mfume — Ghanaian for "conquering son of kings" — and won a seat on the Baltimore City Council [2]. By 1986, he was in Congress representing Maryland's 7th District.

In his first stint in the House, Mfume rose to chair the Congressional Black Caucus from 1993 to 1995, wielding it as a vehicle to press the Clinton administration on issues from Haiti to economic investment in Black communities [3]. Then he did something almost unheard of in Washington: he voluntarily left Congress. In 1996, he resigned his seat to become president of the NAACP, declaring he could do more for civil rights outside the Capitol than inside it [4]. He proceeded to rescue the organization from near-bankruptcy, paying off millions in debt and restoring its institutional credibility. But the NAACP chapter ended badly. In 2004, Mfume departed amid allegations of sexual harassment of female subordinates, a secret executive committee vote against renewing his contract, and a reported $100,000 settlement paid by the organization [5]. He acknowledged an affair with an NAACP employee and publicly apologized the following year.

Sixteen years later, Mfume engineered a political resurrection. When Elijah Cummings died in October 2019, Mfume ran for his old seat and won the April 2020 special election, defeating Cummings's widow in the Democratic primary [6]. Since returning, he has served on the Foreign Affairs and Oversight committees and voted with the Biden administration's position one hundred percent of the time in the 117th Congress [7]. His issue portfolio leans toward government operations, commerce, and armed forces policy, and he has sponsored legislation focused on African American history education and veterans' affairs.

Here is where the 80% Bill lens gets interesting. In the 2023-2024 cycle, fully sixty-six percent of Mfume's campaign funding came from PACs, with just half a percent from small individual donors [8]. That ratio — two-thirds PAC money, virtually nothing from grassroots contributors — puts him squarely in the category of members whose fundraising apparatus looks nothing like the populist reforms the 80% Bill demands. Banning corporate PACs, passing the DISCLOSE Act, ending dark money: these are policies that would directly reshape how Mfume himself finances his campaigns. Whether he would sign onto reforms that threaten his own funding model is the essential question. His biography proves he is capable of dramatic reinvention. The question is whether that reinvention extends to how he pays for his seat.

The bottom line on Tracey Mann is that he will fight like hell to keep Chinese investors off Kansas farmland -- a genuinely popular position -- but will not lift a finger on the other nineteen items that 80% of Americans support. He is a one-issue populist in a twenty-issue world, and the industries filling his campaign account seem quite content with that ratio.

For a man who opposes banning congressional stock trading, the portfolio speaks volumes.

The bottom line on Jerry Moran is that he is a senator who does the quiet work of appropriations and veteran services competently enough to avoid scrutiny, while reliably opposing every structural reform -- stock trading bans, dark money disclosure, corporate PAC restrictions, tax fairness -- that might disrupt the system that keeps him in office. He will protect Kansas farmland from Chinese buyers but not Kansas voters from undisclosed corporate influence. That is not hypocrisy so much as it is priority-setting, and the priorities are legible in every FEC filing.

Murray aligns with many 80% Bill policies in spirit—paid leave, drug price negotiation, stock trading bans, dark money disclosure—but her deep ties to the lobbying and defense industries raise the eternal question: can you fight the system while being its most senior beneficiary?
