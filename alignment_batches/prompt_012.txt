--- D000096: Danny K. Davis (Democrat, IL, house) ---
Danny K. Davis has represented Illinois's 7th Congressional District — the heart of Chicago's West Side and some of its poorest neighborhoods — since 1997 [1]. Before Congress, he worked as a teacher, served on the Cook County Board, and sat on the Chicago City Council, accumulating the kind of deep community roots that make his seat about as safe as they come [2]. He serves on the Ways and Means Committee, which controls the federal tax code, Social Security, and Medicare — three arenas where the 80% Bill has plenty to say. At 84 years old and in his fourteenth term, Davis is one of the longest-serving members of the House.

On paper, Davis lines up with a large swath of the 80% Bill. He cosponsored the Medicare Negotiation and Competitive Licensing Act to let the government negotiate drug prices long before the Inflation Reduction Act made a more modest version law [3]. He has been a consistent supporter of expanding Social Security benefits and raising the payroll tax cap to fund the program, cosponsoring the Social Security 2100 Act [4]. He backed the FAMILY Act for paid family leave [5], supported the For the People Act's provisions on gerrymandering and fair elections [6], and cosponsored the DISCLOSE Act to expose dark money in political spending [7]. He voted to repeal the 2002 Iraq AUMF [8] and has supported police accountability measures including requirements for body-worn cameras [9].

Where Davis's record becomes more complicated is in the gaps. Despite nearly three decades in office, he has not been a prominent voice on banning congressional stock trading, imposing a lifetime lobbying ban, or establishing congressional term limits — the last of which would, admittedly, have shown him the door years ago. His position on the Ways and Means Committee gives him direct jurisdiction over closing the carried interest loophole and taxing the ultra-wealthy, yet his legislative footprint on these specific reforms has been modest [10]. On auditing the Pentagon, protecting U.S. farmland from foreign buyers, and banning corporate purchases of single-family homes, Davis has not staked out visible positions.

The funding picture is revealing. In recent cycles, Davis has raised relatively modest sums — around $700,000 to $900,000 per cycle — reflecting a safe-seat incumbent who does not need to wage expensive campaigns [11]. His top donors have included public sector unions like AFSCME and SEIU, along with health professionals and lawyers [11]. He has accepted corporate PAC money from health care and insurance interests, a tension worth noting for a member of the committee that writes the rules on Medicare and drug pricing [12]. The amounts are not enormous by congressional standards, but they flow from industries with direct stakes in the legislation Davis oversees.

Danny K. Davis is the kind of veteran legislator who has the right instincts on many popular reforms but whose long tenure has not translated into aggressive championship of the structural changes — stock trading bans, lobbying restrictions, term limits — that might disrupt the comfortable machinery of a safe seat held for a quarter century. His district is one of the poorest in Illinois, and on bread-and-butter issues like Social Security and drug pricing, he has shown up. On the institutional reforms that would change how Washington itself works, he has largely been content to let someone else lead.

--- D000197: Diana DeGette (Democrat, CO, house) ---
Diana DeGette has represented Colorado's 1st Congressional District since 1997, making her the longest-serving member of the state's congressional delegation and the dean of Colorado's House members for nearly two decades [1]. A Democrat from Denver, she sits on the powerful House Energy and Commerce Committee, where she serves as the top Democrat on the Health Subcommittee -- a perch that puts her at the intersection of some of the most lucrative lobbying in Washington [2]. After fifteen terms, DeGette is an institution unto herself, which is precisely the kind of thing that makes the 80% Bill's call for term limits awkward at the Thanksgiving table.

On paper, DeGette aligns with a healthy chunk of the 80% Bill. She voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which included Medicare drug price negotiation -- the first time the federal government was allowed to haggle with Big Pharma rather than simply accepting whatever price was scrawled on the napkin [3]. She cosponsored the For the People Act (H.R. 1), which tackled gerrymandering, dark money, and election integrity in one sprawling package [4]. She signed on to a bipartisan petition demanding a vote on the Restore Trust in Congress Act, which would ban members, their spouses, and dependents from trading individual stocks [5]. She has championed consumer protections and has publicly supported campaign finance reform, with her campaign claiming she has "long championed public financing for elections" [6].

But the fine print tells a more complicated story. DeGette is a member of the Congressional Progressive Caucus, which in 2018 announced its PAC would stop taking corporate money -- yet nearly all of its individual members, DeGette included, continued accepting corporate PAC contributions personally [7]. Over her career, DeGette has taken more than $5 million from corporate PACs, according to her 2026 primary challenger Melat Kiros, a 28-year-old Justice Democrats-backed candidate who has made DeGette's donor list a central campaign issue [6][8]. Among the industries writing those checks: defense contractors, energy companies, and pharmaceutical firms -- the very entities DeGette's Energy and Commerce Committee is supposed to oversee [9]. A 2018 PBS investigation found that members of the committee routinely accepted campaign cash from the drug distributors they were publicly scolding in hearings, with DeGette taking $3,500 from those companies in a single cycle [10]. It is the Washington two-step at its most practiced: denounce the industry at the podium, then cash the check in the back office.

DeGette's nearly three-decade tenure is itself a living argument for the term limits she has never championed. She has never cosponsored a congressional term limits amendment, and she declined to provide voters with positions on key issues in the 2024 Political Courage Test [11]. The 80% Bill asks whether a representative serves the people or the system. DeGette's voting record often says the former; her donor rolls and her reluctance to limit her own power occasionally whisper the latter.

--- D000216: Rosa L. DeLauro (Democrat, CT, house) ---
Rosa DeLauro has represented Connecticut's 3rd District since 1991, making her one of the longest-serving members of the House. A cancer survivor turned progressive champion, she rose to chair the powerful House Appropriations Committee in 2021 and now serves as its ranking member. Married to Stan Greenberg, a globally renowned Democratic pollster and political strategist, DeLauro has spent thirty-five years embedded in Washington's power structure while positioning herself as a warrior for working families [1][2]. At 83, she has brushed aside retirement talk, telling Roll Call she is "staying put" even as colleagues wind down their careers [3].

On the substance of the 80% Bill, DeLauro checks an impressive number of boxes. She is the original author of the FAMILY Act, the most ambitious paid family leave proposal in Congress, a cause she has called her "life's work" since co-writing the original Family and Medical Leave Act in the early 1990s [4]. She voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which finally allowed Medicare to negotiate prescription drug prices, a policy she had championed for years through her Medicare for America proposal [5][6]. She has supported repealing the 2001 AUMF, arguing Congress has "a responsibility to debate the scope and scale of any protracted military engagement" [7]. She voted for the For the People Act (HR 1), which would have ended gerrymandering and overhauled campaign finance law. And she has publicly denounced Citizens United as "easily the worst decision since Bush v. Gore," calling it a green light for "blatant corporate corruption of our electoral processes" [8]. She voted for the Build Back Better Act, which included expanded child tax credits and investments in Social Security solvency, and she has introduced her own Rebuild America Act to increase Social Security benefits [9][10].

Yet the picture gets more complicated where defense dollars meet district jobs. DeLauro's Connecticut district is home to Pratt & Whitney, Sikorsky, and the broader RTX Corporation (formerly Raytheon-United Technologies) ecosystem, which employs thousands of her constituents. She has lobbied hard for defense contracts, including the presidential helicopter program, and issued supportive statements during the UTC-Raytheon merger [11][12]. OpenSecrets data shows defense aerospace has been among her top contributing industries, and she ranked as the second-highest recipient from the defense aerospace sector in the 2021-2022 cycle [13]. For a representative who supports auditing the Pentagon, the coziness with the contractors who would be most scrutinized by such an audit is a tension she has never fully reconciled.

Follow the money deeper and you find a lawmaker who talks about closing tax loopholes for corporations shipping jobs overseas while benefiting from a political ecosystem lubricated by institutional donors. DeLauro has noted that roughly $1.5 trillion in annual tax breaks disproportionately benefit the wealthy and big corporations, and she co-sponsored the Stop Tax Haven Abuse Act [14]. But her household wealth, estimated between $3 million and $16 million depending on the source and year, is intertwined with her husband's career advising the very political class she claims to regulate [2][15]. She has no publicly tracked individual stock trades, which is to her credit on the stock-trading ban, but the broader question of a 35-year incumbent railing against entrenched power while being its living embodiment is the kind of irony that writes itself.

The bottom line: DeLauro is a genuine legislative workhorse on paid leave, drug pricing, and campaign finance reform, with receipts that most of her colleagues cannot match. But her deep ties to defense contractors and her status as a Washington lifer married to a Washington power broker mean she embodies both the promise and the paradox of the 80% Bill agenda. She will fight for most of these reforms, as long as the Pentagon keeps writing checks to Connecticut.

--- D000230: Donald G. Davis (Democrat, NC, house) ---
Donald G. Davis represents North Carolina's 1st Congressional District, a sprawling 22-county swath of eastern North Carolina where tobacco fields meet military bases. Greg Murphy that would exempt small-molecule drugs from price negotiations for eleven years, effectively shielding pharmaceutical profits from the very program the Inflation Reduction Act created [5][6]. He has not co-sponsored major reform legislation on these topics, nor has he publicly championed them. For a man who built his brand on bipartisanship, the bills he has chosen to cross the aisle for are telling: they protect defense budgets and pharmaceutical margins, not the kitchen-table populism that 80 percent of Americans are asking for.

Davis is proof that "moderate" and "populist" are not synonyms. His willingness to break with Democrats is real, but the breaks reliably benefit the industries funding his campaigns rather than the rural North Carolinians sending him to Washington. In a district that went for Trump, survival politics may explain the strategy. Whether it justifies it is a question for the voters of NC-1.

Doggett announced his retirement in December 2025 after redistricting carved up his district, making way for Greg Casar. The 80% Bill scorecard for Doggett is mixed but more favorable than most. He genuinely champions Medicare drug negotiation, closing tax loopholes, and taxing the ultra-wealthy. Durbin is a senator who has been on the right side of many 80% Bill issues for decades—campaign finance reform, drug pricing, war powers, family leave—but whose long tenure in the corridors of power has also made him a creature of the system he proposes to reform. He has called for overturning Citizens United while collecting from the legal industry that thrives on the current judiciary, and he has championed small-dollar elections while maintaining a fundraising operation that runs on big-dollar relationships. His retirement may be the most honest thing he has done for the cause of term limits.
