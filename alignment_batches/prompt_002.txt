--- A000382: Angela D. Alsobrooks (Democrat, MD, senate) ---
Angela Alsobrooks arrived in the United States Senate in January 2025 carrying the kind of resume that makes campaign consultants weep with joy: first female county executive in Prince George's County history, first Black woman to hold that office in Maryland, a former state's attorney who presided over a 50-percent drop in violent crime [1]. She ran on a platform of healthcare as a "fundamental right," universal paid family leave, and letting Medicare negotiate the price of every drug on the market -- not just the handful allowed under the Inflation Reduction Act [2]. On paper, she is exactly the kind of senator the 80% Bill was written for. In practice, the jury is still deliberating.

Alsobrooks' campaign biography reads like a progressive fairy tale set in the Maryland suburbs. She clerked at DLA Piper, prosecuted domestic violence cases, ran a county revenue authority, and then climbed the elected-office ladder with the efficiency of someone who actually returns phone calls [3]. Her Senate race against former Governor Larry Hogan was among the most expensive in the country, and she won it decisively. She now sits on the Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs Committee -- a perch that gives her direct jurisdiction over corporate home buying, junk fees, and the financial structures that keep ordinary Americans up at night refreshing their bank apps [4].

But Alsobrooks' first year has not been entirely smooth sailing. A CNN investigation during her campaign revealed she had improperly claimed property tax deductions for over a decade -- including a senior citizens' tax break she was not remotely old enough to qualify for -- saving herself thousands of dollars on properties in D.C. and Maryland [5]. For a candidate running on closing tax loopholes and taxing the ultra-wealthy, the optics were, to put it charitably, suboptimal. She pledged to repay the money, eventually settling roughly $17,600 in improperly received credits while leaving nearly $30,000 in interest penalties unresolved as of late 2024 [6].

On the 80% Bill's twenty articles, Alsobrooks has positioned herself favorably on several fronts. She has vocally defended Medicare and Medicaid from cuts, endorsed expanding drug price negotiation, and campaigned on paid family leave and affordable housing [2][7]. Her committee assignments align naturally with articles on banning corporate home buying, junk fees, and financial transparency. Yet on thornier items like banning congressional stock trading, the lobbying revolving door, and Pentagon audits, her record is thinner -- she has not yet co-sponsored major legislation on these issues, and her leadership PAC, AlsoPAC, continues to accept the kind of contributions that make campaign finance reformers reach for antacids [8].

Alsobrooks has until 2030 before she faces voters again, which gives her a comfortable runway to either champion the broadly popular reforms that define the 80% Bill or settle into the comfortable middle lane of Senate Democratic politics. Her attendance record is excellent -- missing only 9 of 706 votes in her first year -- and she has shown a willingness to collaborate across the Maryland delegation, partnering with Rep. Sarah Elfreth on Chesapeake Bay restoration legislation [4][9]. The question is whether she will bring that same energy to the fights that do not come with a scenic waterway photo op.

--- B000490: Sanford D. Bishop, Jr. (Democrat, GA, house) ---
Sanford D. Bishop, Jr. has represented southwest Georgia in Congress since 1993, making him one of the longest-serving members of the Georgia delegation and a senior member of the powerful House Appropriations Committee. A former civil rights attorney and state legislator, Bishop is that increasingly rare creature in Washington: a moderate Southern Democrat who has survived in a district redrawn multiple times around him, in part by cultivating a reputation as someone who brings home the bacon for Georgia's peanut farmers, military installations, and rural communities [1]. His seniority on Appropriations -- where he has chaired the Military Construction and Veterans Affairs subcommittee -- gives him genuine clout, which makes the question of how he wields it all the more relevant.

On the 80% Bill's twenty provisions, Bishop's record is a complicated mosaic of Democratic loyalty and conspicuous silence. He has voted in favor of the For the People Act and its election reform and anti-gerrymandering provisions, and he cosponsored the DISCLOSE Act to require transparency in campaign spending [2]. He supported the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare drug price negotiation provisions and has backed paid family leave legislation [3]. On police body cameras, Bishop -- a member of the Congressional Black Caucus -- has been a consistent supporter of police accountability measures, including the George Floyd Justice in Policing Act [4]. These positions put him squarely in line with the 80% consensus.

But Bishop's alignment frays at the edges where money meets policy. He has not cosponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, and he has been quiet on a lifetime lobbying ban for former members of Congress. On taxing the ultra-wealthy and closing the carried interest loophole, Bishop has generally voted with his party but has not been a vocal champion -- a posture that becomes more understandable when you examine his donor profile. He has not taken a strong public position on congressional term limits, which is perhaps unsurprising for a man entering his third decade in the same seat. On the Pentagon audit question, Bishop occupies an inherently conflicted position: as an Appropriations subcommittee chair who directs military spending, he has more reason than most to resist the scrutiny that a genuine audit would impose [5].

Bishop's fundraising tells a story of institutional comfort. His top donors over the years have included defense contractors, agribusiness giants, and financial services firms [6]. Insurance companies, commercial banks, and real estate interests round out a donor portfolio that looks less like a civil rights champion's and more like an Appropriations cardinal's. Corporate PAC contributions have been a significant share of his fundraising, including from the very industries whose spending his committee oversees [7]. When your campaign coffers are filled by the defense industry and you chair the subcommittee that funds military construction, the call to audit the Pentagon might ring a little hollow.

Sanford Bishop is, in many ways, the establishment Democrat that the 80% Bill is designed to test. He will vote the right way on many of these issues when the party asks him to, but he is unlikely to lead the charge on the provisions -- banning corporate PACs, ending dark money, auditing the Pentagon -- that would disrupt the ecosystem that has sustained his career for over thirty years. His constituents in rural Georgia deserve someone who fights for the full package, not just the parts that do not disturb the appropriations process.

--- B000668: Cliff Bentz (Republican, OR, house) ---
Rep. Cliff Bentz is a rancher-lawyer who sits on the Natural Resources and Energy & Commerce committees while personally owning a 100-acre alfalfa farm called Actin Ranch [1] -- which also happens to be one of his largest campaign contributors [2]. He collects $59,100 from the livestock industry he regulates and $47,600 from oil and gas interests whose energy policy he shapes [3], and 51% of his total campaign funding flows from corporate PACs [2]. The man literally writes the rules for the water his own crops drink, having spent eight years on the Oregon Water Resources Commission before coming to Congress [1]. If there's a textbook illustration of "conflict of interest," it's a cattle rancher chairing a Natural Resources subcommittee while cashing livestock lobby checks.

Bentz signed the U.S. Term Limits pledge back in 2020 [4], a commitment that grows more decorative with each passing term. He has shown no interest in banning congressional stock trading, ending dark money, passing the DISCLOSE Act, or banning corporate PACs -- essentially the entire accountability wing of the 80% Bill. He voted to object to Pennsylvania's electoral votes on January 6, 2021, joining 138 other Republicans in challenging the democratic process [5], then pivoted to supporting the bipartisan January 6th Commission [1] -- a display of tactical flexibility that would impress a seasoned contortionist.

His fiscal conservatism, meanwhile, is strictly for other people's money. The Malheur Enterprise revealed that Bentz's staff racked up luxury hotel stays on the taxpayer dime -- the $1,294-a-night Coeur d'Alene Resort, the $338 Davenport Hotel, the $381 Churchill Hotel [6]. His chief of staff, making north of $150,000 annually, stayed in $700-a-night hotels while on public business [6]. Bentz outspent every single House member on Trump's inauguration weekend, burning through $20,750 in taxpayer-funded travel for four staffers -- more than six times the House average of $2,546 [7]. When reporters asked for receipts, he went silent.

Then came the One Big Beautiful Bill. Oregon Governor Tina Kotek personally called Bentz to warn him the bill would slash $15 billion in federal funding to Oregon, gutting Medicaid for a district where 70% of children rely on it [8]. Oregon nurses, Democrats, and even former Republican-allied rural hospital administrators pushed back fiercely [9]. Bentz voted yes anyway, then called Kotek's assessment "misleading" [10]. A rancher who preaches self-reliance while billing taxpayers for spa resorts, who pledges term limits while seeking another term, who represents one of the poorest districts in Oregon while voting to cut their healthcare -- Cliff Bentz is the 80% Bill's photo negative.

--- B000740: Stephanie I. Bice (Republican, OK, house) ---
Stephanie Bice has represented Oklahoma's 5th District since 2021, when she flipped the seat from Democrat Kendra Horn. A former state senator and marketing executive with an MBA, Bice has positioned herself as a "modernizer" of Congress — she chairs the House Administration Subcommittee on Modernization & Innovation — which sounds impressive until you realize the institution she's modernizing still can't stop its own members from trading stocks on insider information.

Speaking of which, Bice was flagged in 2024 for violating the STOCK Act by filing late disclosures on U.S. Treasury bill purchases made through a trust, each worth between $15,001 and $50,000. The trades were made in June and September 2023 but not reported until August 2024 — well past the 45-day deadline. The penalty? A potential $200 late fee. Truly a deterrent for the ages. Meanwhile, Bice has not co-sponsored any legislation to ban congressional stock trading outright, because why would the person modernizing Congress modernize the part where members get to trade on privileged information?

On January 6, 2021, Bice voted to object to certifying electoral votes from Arizona and Pennsylvania — on her very first day as a sworn member of Congress. She later claimed this was about "election security," not overturning the election, a distinction roughly as convincing as saying you pulled the fire alarm because you were curious about the sound.

Bice sits on the powerful Appropriations Committee, where she serves as Vice Chair of two subcommittees. Her top donor industries have included oil and gas, banking, and financial services — sectors that collectively contributed heavily to her campaigns. She enthusiastically backed the One Big Beautiful Bill, Trump's reconciliation package stuffed with tax breaks and energy deregulation, including provisions she personally crafted to give regulatory relief to mineral owners and the Bureau of Land Management.

To her credit, Bice has been a genuine champion of paid family leave, co-founding a bipartisan working group with Democrat Chrissy Houlahan and introducing the More Paid Leave for More Americans Act. That's one out of twenty 80% Bill planks, which earns her a participation trophy but not much else. On banning corporate PACs, dark money reform, the DISCLOSE Act, Pentagon audits, term limits, Medicare drug negotiation, and virtually every other plank of the 80% Bill, Bice has been either absent or opposed. She is, in the end, a polished operator who modernizes the letterhead while leaving the corruption untouched.

--- B000825: Lauren Boebert (Republican, CO, house) ---
Lauren Boebert is a Republican congresswoman now representing Colorado's 4th district, having switched from the 3rd district in 2024 after a razor-thin reelection scare and a divorce made the more heavily Republican eastern plains look like a better bet [1]. Before Congress, she was best known for Shooters Grill, a Rifle, Colorado restaurant where the waitresses openly carried firearms -- a brand she parlayed into a primary upset over five-term incumbent Scott Tipton in 2020 [2]. She earned her GED the same year she first ran for office, sits on the Natural Resources Committee, co-chairs the Congressional Second Amendment Caucus, and is a member of the House Freedom Caucus [3].

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Boebert's record is a study in populist rhetoric meeting party-line reality. She voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which included Medicare drug price negotiation -- a provision supported by roughly 90% of Americans -- and then watched President Biden tour her own district to highlight the clean energy jobs the law created there [4][5]. She was one of only two members of Congress (alongside Marjorie Taylor Greene) to vote against the TRANSPLANT Act reauthorizing the National Marrow Donor Program, citing the national debt -- a concern that apparently vanished when it came time to vote for tax cuts benefiting the ultra-wealthy [6]. On campaign finance reform, dark money disclosure, gerrymandering, and the DISCLOSE Act, her record is a flatline of opposition or silence.

Where Boebert breaks from the GOP pack, and deserves genuine credit, is the Epstein files. In late 2025, she was one of just four Republican signatories on a discharge petition to force the release of Jeffrey Epstein's investigative files, defying direct pressure from President Trump and his team to remove her name [7][8]. The House ultimately voted 427-1 to pass the Epstein Files Transparency Act, and Trump signed it into law, but it would not have reached the floor without Boebert's willingness to buck her own party leader [9]. It is, by a wide margin, her most consequential alignment with the 80% Bill's agenda.

Follow the money and the picture sharpens. Her husband Jayson Boebert earned $478,000 in 2020 and $460,000 in 2019 as a "consultant" for Terra Energy Productions -- income she initially failed to disclose as required by federal ethics law, even as she sat on the Natural Resources Committee pushing to loosen drilling regulations [10][11]. The oil and gas industry is her top donor sector, and she has described her energy policy as "all-of-the-above," which in practice means all-of-the-fossil-fuels [12]. She has also faced FEC complaints alleging misuse of campaign funds for personal expenses, including rent and utilities, and a separate complaint from End Citizens United alleging improperly reported get-out-the-vote expenditures [13][14].

The bottom line: Boebert sells herself as a populist firebrand fighting the Washington establishment, and on the Epstein files she genuinely walked that walk. But on the bread-and-butter economic reforms that 80% of Americans support -- drug prices, junk fees, taxing the ultra-wealthy, reining in dark money -- she votes with the donor class every single time. The gun is real; the populism is mostly theater.

--- B001230: Tammy Baldwin (Democrat, WI, senate) ---
Senator Tammy Baldwin has built a career that, on paper, reads like a greatest-hits album of the 80% Bill. She co-sponsored the Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act and has kept her own assets in a blind trust since 2013 — a move so responsible it practically disqualifies her from Congress. She voted to repeal the Iraq War authorization and was one of just 133 House members who voted against the 2002 invasion in the first place, back when opposing forever wars was considered career suicide rather than common sense.

Baldwin co-sponsored the DISCLOSE Act to drag dark money into the sunlight, only to watch every single Republican vote to keep donors anonymous. She has championed Medicare drug price negotiation since before it was cool, helped pass the Inflation Reduction Act's provisions allowing Medicare to negotiate prices on dozens of drugs, and then pushed the SMART Prices Act to expand negotiation even further. She teamed up with Republican Chuck Grassley on the Farmland Security Act to crack down on foreign shell companies quietly buying up Wisconsin's dairy country — bipartisanship so wholesome it should come with a cheese curd garnish.

On housing, Baldwin co-introduced the Stop Predatory Investing Act to strip tax breaks from corporations hoarding more than 50 single-family homes, and she publicly pushed the Trump administration to follow through on its own rhetoric about corporate homebuyers. She demanded the Senate immediately vote on the Epstein Files Transparency Act, insisting that "victims and the American people deserve answers." Her $52.6 million 2024 campaign war chest was the seventh most expensive Senate race that cycle, though her campaign committee itself kept direct PAC money relatively modest compared to the super PAC bonanza swirling around Wisconsin.

Where Baldwin falls short is in the comfortable silences. She has not championed term limits — understandable, perhaps, for a senator first elected to the House in 1998. She has not led loudly on a post-congressional lobbying ban or Pentagon audit reform, content to let those fights belong to others. And while she talks a good game on fair elections, the $26.3 million that the WinSenate super PAC spent defending her seat suggests the dark money ecosystem she criticizes has been a reliable dance partner. Still, Baldwin checks more boxes on the 80% Bill than most of her colleagues, which in Congress is roughly equivalent to being the tallest person in a room full of people sitting down.

--- B001236: John Boozman (Republican, AR, senate) ---
John Boozman, Republican of Arkansas, has held his Senate seat since 2011 and before that represented Arkansas’s 3rd congressional district in the House for a decade, making him one of the longest-serving members of the Arkansas delegation[1]. A former optometrist who co-founded a private clinic in Rogers, Boozman defeated Democratic incumbent Blanche Lincoln by 21 points in 2010 to become only the second Republican elected to the Senate from Arkansas since Reconstruction[2]. Now the senior senator from the state, he chairs the Senate Agriculture Committee and sits on Appropriations — where he chairs the Military Construction and Veterans Affairs subcommittee — as well as Environment and Public Works, Veterans’ Affairs, and Rules[3]. In short, Boozman controls significant levers over farm policy, defense spending, and veterans’ benefits, which makes his positions on the 80% Bill articles worth examining with care.

On the issues where roughly four in five Americans agree, Boozman’s record is a patchwork of cautious alignment and conspicuous silence. He has taken a genuine interest in protecting U.S. farmland from foreign ownership, holding hearings on the topic as Agriculture Committee chair and authoring a provision in the FY2023 NDAA directing the Pentagon to examine Chinese land purchases near military installations[4]. He cosponsored the Social Security Fairness Act in August 2024 and has publicly stated that “Congress must work to protect the future of Social Security”[5]. He told reporters he would have “no objection” to legislation releasing the Epstein files, noting that “lots of people ran on this issue”[6]. On Medicare drug pricing, he joined a bipartisan letter urging CMS to improve price transparency in Part D — but voted against the Inflation Reduction Act and its historic Medicare drug negotiation provisions, along with every other Senate Republican[7][8].

Where things get more interesting is where Boozman goes quiet. He has taken no public position on banning congressional stock trading, despite actively trading stocks himself — 319 trades totaling over $641,000 in just three years, including purchases of Netflix, Exxon Mobil, Disney, and Visa[9]. He sits on the Appropriations defense subcommittee while holding investments in defense-adjacent ETFs and individual equities[10]. He has not cosponsored the Audit the Pentagon Act, despite chairing the subcommittee that funds military construction and despite the Pentagon’s seventh consecutive failed audit in 2024[11]. On the DISCLOSE Act, Citizens United reform, lobbying bans, term limits, corporate PAC restrictions, paid family leave, police body cameras, carried interest loopholes, right to repair, junk fees, and data privacy, Boozman’s public record is effectively a blank page — a remarkable feat for someone who has served in Congress for a quarter century.

Follow the money and the silence starts to make sense. Boozman’s top contributors read like a who’s who of Arkansas corporate power: Walmart’s PAC for Responsible Government contributed $32,930, with Stephens Inc., Koch Industries, the National Association of Real Estate Investment Trusts, and The Home Depot PAC also among his largest backers[12][13]. Agriculture and food processing industries — Tyson Foods territory — are naturally heavy contributors to the chairman of the Agriculture Committee[14]. His most recent FEC disclosure showed just 34% of fundraising coming from individual donors[15]. A senator bankrolled by corporate PACs and agribusiness giants is unlikely to lead the charge on banning corporate PACs, taxing the ultra-wealthy, or closing the carried interest loophole. The optometrist from Rogers can apparently see farmland threats from Beijing with perfect clarity, but when it comes to the corporate money flowing through his own campaign coffers, the prescription seems to be a deliberate blur.

--- B001243: Marsha Blackburn (Republican, TN, senate) ---
Sen. Marsha Blackburn has spent over two decades in Congress perfecting the art of talking like a populist while cashing checks from the industries she's supposed to regulate. In return, Blackburn introduced the "Internet Freedom Act" to kill net neutrality — because nothing says freedom like letting your donors throttle your constituents' internet. Vice dubbed her "Big Telecom's Best Friend in Congress," a title she has earned with metronomic loyalty.

Then there's pharma. Blackburn has collected over $1.3 million from the pharmaceutical and health products industry over her career. When Medicare drug price negotiation finally passed in the Inflation Reduction Act, Blackburn voted no, calling it "socialist drug price controls." The $215,500 she received from pharma PACs between 2017 and 2022 apparently had nothing to do with it.

To her credit, Blackburn co-sponsored the bipartisan Ban on Congressional Stock Ownership Act, saying it would "restore Americans' trust in democracy." A broken clock, as they say. But on nearly every other 80% Bill policy, her record is a wasteland. She voted against the DISCLOSE Act to expose dark money. She has taken no meaningful action on Pentagon audits despite voting for bloated NDAAs. She introduced a bill to reduce taxes on Social Security benefits — a fine gesture — while opposing the structural funding reforms that would actually keep the program solvent. On paid family leave, term limits, banning junk fees, right to repair, and corporate home buying, her record is either hostile or nonexistent.

Blackburn raised $6.6 million for her 2024 cycle, with over 53% coming from sources classified as "Other" by OpenSecrets — a catch-all that includes leadership PACs and dark money pass-throughs. Her own leadership PAC, MARSHA PAC, funnels additional industry cash through the system. She sits on the Senate Judiciary and Commerce committees, both of which oversee the very industries that fund her campaigns. The fox isn't just guarding the henhouse — she built the henhouse, staffed it, and is charging admission.

The 20 policies in the 80% Bill poll above 80% with voters of all stripes, including Tennessee Republicans. Blackburn's constituents want drug prices negotiated, dark money banned, and the Pentagon audited. Instead, they get a senator who fights harder for AT&T's profit margins than for their broadband speeds.

--- B001260: Vern Buchanan (Republican, FL, house) ---
Vern Buchanan has represented Florida's Gulf Coast in Congress since 2007, currently serving the 16th District that stretches across Sarasota and Manatee counties. Before entering politics, he built an automotive empire that once ranked as the 17th largest private dealership group in the country, with $756 million in annual sales [1]. With an estimated net worth north of $150 million, Buchanan is routinely counted among the wealthiest members of Congress — a distinction that lends a certain flavor to his votes on tax policy from his seat as Vice Chairman of the House Ways and Means Committee [2]. He announced in January 2026 that he will retire at the end of his term, calling it "the right time to pass the torch" after ten terms in office [3].

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Buchanan's record is a study in late-career repositioning. He co-sponsored the Stop Insider Trading Act in January 2026, joining the push to ban congressional stock trading — a move that carries somewhat less political risk when you have already announced your retirement [4]. He has spoken favorably about protecting Social Security and Medicare, calling them "two of the most successful government programs," and has advanced bipartisan bills on seniors' access to care through his chairmanship of the Health Subcommittee [5]. These are not trivial positions, and his legislative work on telehealth expansion and prior authorization reform has drawn support from patient advocacy groups.

The gaps, however, are cavernous. Buchanan voted against the Inflation Reduction Act and its Medicare drug price negotiation provisions, penning an op-ed calling the bill "another example of Washington false advertising" [6]. He has taken no public position on a lifetime lobbying ban, the DISCLOSE Act, ending gerrymandering, banning corporate PACs, banning dark money, paid family leave, police body cameras, or closing the carried interest loophole. On taxing the ultra-wealthy, his record on the Ways and Means Committee — where he has championed the Trump-era tax cuts — suggests the answer is a firm no [7]. He has not co-sponsored legislation to audit the Pentagon or repeal aging war authorizations. The term limits question is especially rich: after twenty years in the House, Buchanan never signed a term limits pledge, preferring to let biology rather than principle determine his departure date.

Buchanan's top industry contributors include health professionals ($209,732 in one recent cycle), real estate interests, and pharmaceutical and insurance companies — precisely the sectors regulated by his committee [8]. His car dealerships received up to $7 million in PPP loans during COVID, with the largest going to Sarasota Ford, even as small businesses in his own district struggled to access the program [9]. He also owns offshore reinsurance companies in Turks and Caicos that sell extended warranties to car buyers — a business structure that, whatever its legality, does not exactly scream populist reformer [1].

Vern Buchanan's career is a masterclass in the art of being wealthy enough to serve in Congress without ever feeling the urgency to change the system that made it possible. His last-minute embrace of the stock trading ban is a nice parting gift, but it arrives after two decades of silence on the structural reforms — dark money disclosure, corporate PAC bans, wealth taxation, lobbying restrictions — that the vast majority of Americans have been demanding all along.
