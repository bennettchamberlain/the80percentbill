--- B001261: John Barrasso (Republican, WY, senate) ---

He opposed the CIA's Center on Climate Change and National Security, introduced legislation to prevent the EPA from limiting carbon emissions, and tried to kill tax credits for electric vehicles. His position is essentially that climate change is real, it is fine, and also here is a bill to make it worse. He frames every pro-fossil-fuel vote as "energy independence," a phrase that has done more heavy lifting in his career than any policy he has actually passed.

Medicare drug negotiation? Not while pharma is writing checks. Term limits? He has been in the Senate since 2007 and has no plans to leave.

Barrasso is the Senate's most reliable friend to extractive industries and its most reliable opponent of popular reform. Wyoming gets a senator who talks about energy independence while serving every interest except theirs.

He voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which included the Medicare drug price negotiation provisions he had long championed through his Medicare-X proposal [7][8]. He has publicly claimed to be "the ONLY Senator running for re-election who isn't taking a single cent from federal lobbyists AND corporate PACs" [9].

That claim, however, deserves an asterisk the size of a mountain. Bennet's record reveals a familiar Washington species: the reformer whose donor base is the thing being reformed. He genuinely sponsors more democracy-repair legislation than most senators will read in a career, from stock trading bans to dark money disclosure to lobbying restrictions. But he does so while cashing checks from the financial elite who benefit most from the status quo. When your top industry donor is the securities sector and you're chairing a tax oversight subcommittee, the phrase "conflict of interest" starts to feel like an understatement. The 80% Bill asks whether lawmakers will side with the supermajority of Americans who want these reforms. Bennet has the voting record -- now the question is whether the money will ever catch up to the message.

--- B001277: Richard Blumenthal (Democrat, CT, senate) ---
Richard Blumenthal is Connecticut's senior senator, a Democrat who has held the seat since 2011 after serving twenty years as the state's attorney general. He is also one of the wealthiest members of Congress, with an estimated net worth between $70 million and $130 million, largely derived from his marriage into the Malkin real estate dynasty, which controls holdings in the Empire State Building and Manhattan commercial properties [1][2]. Before entering politics, Blumenthal obtained five deferments during the Vietnam War era, then joined the Marine Reserves stateside -- a fact that became a campaign controversy when he was caught telling veterans "we have learned something important since the days that I served in Vietnam," a place he had never been [3][4].

On paper, Blumenthal checks many of the 80% Bill boxes. He co-sponsored the Democracy for All Amendment to overturn Citizens United, joined amicus briefs urging the Supreme Court to reconsider its dark money jurisprudence, and pushed the DISCLOSE Act alongside Senator Whitehouse to force transparency in political spending [5][6]. He championed the STOCK Act to ban congressional insider trading and has publicly supported Medicare drug price negotiation, paid family leave, and campaign finance reform [7]. On the Epstein files, Blumenthal has been genuinely aggressive -- invoking an obscure "Rule of Five" law to force DOJ disclosure and leading a bipartisan call for an Inspector General audit when the department dragged its feet [8][9].

But the contradictions are where it gets interesting. Blumenthal vocally demanded investigations into Robinhood during the 2021 GameStop trading frenzy -- on the very same day that a fund controlled by his wife's family purchased between $500,000 and $1,000,000 in Robinhood shares [10]. His family later sold up to $2.5 million in Robinhood stock, and he failed to disclose the sales within the legally required 45-day window under the STOCK Act -- the very law he championed [11]. The Foundation for Accountability and Civic Trust filed a formal ethics complaint, and Blumenthal's defense was that he personally owns no individual stocks and had no knowledge of the trades. For a man who built his brand on consumer protection and corporate accountability, the "I didn't know what my family's money was doing" defense is a breathtaking piece of irony [12].

Follow the money and Blumenthal's story gets more layered. As a member of the Senate Armed Services Committee, he routinely votes for expanded defense authorization bills while securing wins for Connecticut's defense industrial base, including Pratt & Whitney and Sikorsky [13]. His top campaign contributors over the years have included lawyers and law firms, contributing over $1.27 million in the 2019-2024 cycle alone [14]. He does not need corporate PAC money -- his personal fortune means he can largely self-fund and accept the ideologically clean donations -- but that wealth itself is the product of exactly the kind of dynastic, real-estate-empire capital accumulation that the "Tax the Ultra-Wealthy" plank of the 80% Bill targets.

The bottom line on Blumenthal: he talks the talk on nearly every popular reform, and on some issues -- Epstein transparency, campaign finance disclosure -- he has genuinely led. But when the spotlight hits his own portfolio, the senator who champions the STOCK Act cannot seem to keep track of his family's seven-figure stock trades. Connecticut voters should ask whether a man worth nine figures, married into the Empire State Building, is truly the tribune of the people he performs as on C-SPAN.

Bonamici is a competent, well-intentioned legislator who operates comfortably within a broken system rather than working to break it open. In a district that would reward boldness, she offers diligence.

The frustrating thing about Joyce Beatty is that she clearly believes in some of the right things. She got arrested for voting rights. She fights for Juneteenth. It's the congressional paradox: fighting for the people while being bankrolled by the powerful.
