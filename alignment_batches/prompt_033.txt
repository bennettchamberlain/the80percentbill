--- M001188: Grace Meng (Democrat, NY, house) ---
Grace Meng has represented Queens' 6th Congressional District since 2013, becoming the first Asian-American member of Congress from New York. She sits on the House Appropriations Committee — one of the most powerful spending panels in Congress — which makes her both a gatekeeper for federal dollars and an irresistible target for lobbyists looking to steer those dollars their way.

Meng's donor profile tells a familiar story of Democratic establishment fundraising. Real estate interests have been a consistent top industry donor, which is notable for a representative whose district includes Flushing — one of the hottest real estate markets in the country, where corporate buyers and developers have been steadily pricing out working-class families. Yet Meng has not championed legislation to ban corporate homebuying or meaningfully rein in the speculative real estate frenzy transforming her own district. The 80% Bill's call to ban corporate home buying apparently hasn't reached the Appropriations Committee's inbox.

On stock trading, Meng has not been a vocal advocate for a congressional trading ban, keeping her head down on an issue where 86% of Americans want action. She has supported party-line Democratic initiatives on paid family leave and Medicare drug price negotiation, earning partial credit on those planks, but her record on the more structurally transformative elements of the 80% Bill — Pentagon audits, dark money bans, the DISCLOSE Act, term limits — is largely a blank page.

Corporate PACs have been a meaningful part of Meng's fundraising apparatus. In recent cycles she has received contributions from PACs linked to major defense contractors, pharmaceutical companies, and financial institutions. She cosponsored the For the People Act, which contained some election reform provisions, but has not been a leading voice on banning dark money or corporate PAC contributions — perhaps because doing so would mean turning off the fundraising spigot that keeps her campaign competitive.

To her credit, Meng has been a strong advocate on issues like anti-Asian hate crime legislation and food labeling standards, and she has used her Appropriations seat to direct funding toward community health centers and small businesses in her district. But on the 80% Bill's core demand — that Congress stop serving corporate donors and start serving the 80% supermajorities who agree on common-sense reforms — Meng's record is one of cautious incrementalism at best and donor-protective silence at worst. In a district where working families are being squeezed by rising costs and corporate consolidation, "cautious incrementalism" is just a polite way of saying "not enough."

--- M001190: Markwayne Mullin (Republican, OK, senate) ---
Markwayne Mullin is the rare politician who ran for Congress on a term limits pledge, broke it, got elected to the Senate anyway, and then became a walking argument for why term limits exist. When he first ran for Oklahoma's 2nd Congressional District in 2012, he promised to serve only three terms [1]. He served five. The U.S. Term Limits organization publicly called him out for breaking his word, noting it was "a central part of his campaign, a key part of why people trusted him" [1]. Mullin's response was essentially to shrug and run for Jim Inhofe's Senate seat, which he won in 2022. Accountability, Oklahoma style.

With a net worth estimated at $60-66 million [2], Mullin is one of the richest members of the Senate, having built a plumbing empire he sold in 2021 for tens of millions while serving in the House [3]. He sits on the Armed Services Committee and the Environment and Public Works Committee [4], which makes it particularly convenient that his top career donor industry is Oil & Gas, which has contributed over $1.3 million to his campaigns [5]. PACs from Devon Energy, Halliburton, Continental Resources, and OneOK have all written checks [6]. In return, Mullin champions fossil fuel interests and has never shown the faintest interest in closing tax loopholes that benefit energy corporations or taxing the ultra-wealthy -- a category that includes himself.

Then there are the stocks. Mullin violated the STOCK Act by failing to disclose hundreds of thousands of dollars in trades, some reported more than two-and-a-half years past the 45-day legal deadline [7]. His late disclosures revealed millions of dollars in stock and bond transactions [8]. His office blamed the delays on a "third-party operator firm," as if an independent wealth manager absolves a sitting senator of knowing what his own money is doing. Newsweek flagged at least one trade that raised "conflict of interest" concerns [9]. And yet Mullin has not cosponsored the Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act -- the very law designed to prevent exactly what he has been caught doing.

When not trading stocks or cashing oil checks, Mullin keeps busy with extracurriculars. In 2021, he attempted a freelance rescue mission to Afghanistan, trying to bring a large sum of cash into Tajikistan to rent a helicopter, and reportedly threatened a U.S. ambassador who refused to help [10]. In November 2023, the former MMA fighter challenged Teamsters president Sean O'Brien to a physical fight during a Senate hearing, prompting Bernie Sanders to remind him, "You're a United States senator" [11]. Trump has since nominated Mullin to replace Kristi Noem as DHS Secretary [12], because apparently the department responsible for national security needs a man who solves problems with his fists. On every plank of the 80% Bill -- banning stock trading, ending dark money, auditing the Pentagon, paid family leave -- Mullin is absent, opposed, or actively violating the spirit of the reform.

--- M001194: John R. Moolenaar (Republican, MI, house) ---
John Moolenaar spent eight months as a chemist at Dow Chemical before concluding that the real experiments worth running were political ones. Born and raised in Midland, Michigan — Dow's company town — he traded the laboratory for the state legislature in 2003 and has been climbing the Republican ladder with the quiet persistence of a man who understands that patience is its own compound interest [1]. Now representing Michigan's 2nd District after redistricting shuffled him out of the old 4th, Moolenaar serves on the House Appropriations Committee as the state's sole member, a perch he uses to direct federal dollars toward harbor dredging, recreational pathways, and hospital infrastructure across western and northern Michigan [2]. He describes this work as "knowing your district," which is a polite way of saying earmarks are only wasteful spending when someone else requests them.

Moolenaar's most prominent role is chairing the House Select Committee on Strategic Competition between the United States and the Chinese Communist Party, where he has built a bipartisan portfolio of hawkish legislation with genuine substance. He introduced the first bill to revoke China's Permanent Normal Trade Relations, pushed sanctions on PRC military firms aiding Russia, backed the Uyghur Genocide and Accountability Sanctions Act, and proposed restricting U.S. investment in blacklisted Chinese companies — a probe that uncovered $6.5 billion in American capital flowing to firms the government had flagged for advancing CCP military capabilities [3]. He has also demanded that Columbia University sever ties with CCP-backed exchange programs, apparently operating under the assumption that academic freedom should not extend to partnerships with authoritarian surveillance states [4].

On domestic policy, Moolenaar's record is standard-issue conservative with a few notable detours. He voted against the Respect for Marriage Act in 2022 and has expressed opposition to the Affordable Care Act with the conviction of someone who believes the federal government's involvement in healthcare is roughly as welcome as a skunk at a garden party [5]. His campaign finance tells a familiar Appropriations story: PAC contributions account for roughly 41 percent of his fundraising, with large individual donors covering another 52 percent and small donors contributing a mere 2.25 percent — a ratio suggesting his grassroots support is more philosophical than financial [6].

Where Moolenaar's record grows more complicated is on questions of democratic accountability. He signed the amicus brief supporting Texas v. Pennsylvania, the post-2020 Supreme Court challenge that sought to discard millions of votes in four states, including his own Michigan [7]. He subsequently voted against creating an independent commission to investigate the January 6 attack and against holding Steve Bannon in contempt of Congress — positions that sit awkwardly beside his otherwise disciplined attendance record of missing only 31 out of 6,177 roll calls over a decade [8]. Moolenaar is, in short, a legislator who shows up reliably for the work of governing while occasionally lending his name to efforts that would undermine the foundations on which that work rests — a contradiction he has shown no particular interest in resolving.

--- M001196: Seth Moulton (Democrat, MA, house) ---
Seth Moulton built his political brand on a genuinely impressive military resume. A Harvard physics graduate who joined the Marines days after commencement in 2001, he led one of the first infantry platoons into Baghdad during the 2003 invasion and served four tours in Iraq, earning a Navy and Marine Corps Commendation Medal for valor [1]. He later worked as a liaison to General Petraeus and helped establish independent Iraqi media. In 2019, he publicly disclosed his struggle with PTSD, becoming one of the few members of Congress to speak openly about combat-related mental health challenges and proposing mandatory mental health screenings for active-duty troops and veterans [2]. It was a brave, humanizing moment that earned him deserved respect.

Then there is the other Seth Moulton -- the one who briefly ran for president in 2020 on a platform of generational change and reform, only to drop out after four months when he failed to qualify for a single debate [3]. The campaign raised a mere one million dollars, a number that suggests even his donors were not entirely convinced. He positioned himself as a new-generation leader who tried to oust Nancy Pelosi from the speakership, eventually backing her in exchange for a term-limits deal on her leadership [4]. The reformer lane, it turned out, was already crowded with candidates who had actually built coalitions.

The most revealing chapter in Moulton's record may be the one involving stock trades. Despite positioning himself as a governance reformer, Moulton repeatedly violated the STOCK Act by filing late disclosures of his wife's stock transactions. In one instance, his wife sold up to one hundred thousand dollars in Activision Blizzard shares months before the disclosure was filed. In another, a sale of up to two hundred and fifty thousand dollars in Spencer Stuart stock went unreported for roughly six months [5]. His office called it "a bit embarrassing" and blamed the complexity of a two-income household -- a defense that lands differently when the fine for violating federal disclosure law is a mere two hundred dollars [6].

On the policy front, Moulton sits on the House Armed Services Committee as Ranking Member of the Subcommittee on Strategic Forces and serves on the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee, where he has championed high-speed rail [7]. His defense industry donors have taken notice: in the 2018 cycle alone, defense aerospace PACs contributed over five hundred thousand dollars to his campaign, while securities and investment firms chipped in more than three hundred thousand [8]. He has publicly stated he would reject corporate PAC money during his presidential run, a pledge that conveniently expired when the campaign did.

Moulton represents the particular species of Democrat who talks convincingly about reform while operating comfortably within the system he criticizes. He is a genuine war hero who cannot seem to file his stock disclosures on time, a generational-change candidate who has held the same seat since 2015, and a critic of institutional inertia who benefits handsomely from its fundraising apparatus. The contradictions do not make him unusual in Congress -- they make him perfectly typical.

--- M001198: Roger Marshall (Republican, KS, senate) ---
Roger Marshall is the Republican senator from Kansas who brands himself "Doc Marshall," a nod to the twenty-five years he spent delivering babies as an obstetrician in Great Bend before delivering talking points in Washington [1]. A fifth-generation farm kid who became the first in his family to attend college, Marshall earned a chemistry degree from Kansas State and an M.D. from the University of Kansas before trading the delivery room for the Senate floor in 2021. He sits on the Agriculture, Finance, Budget, and Health committees -- a portfolio that positions him at the intersection of nearly every 80% bill on this list, which makes his voting record all the more instructive.

On stock trading, Marshall offers a masterclass in having it both ways. He has publicly declared he is "absolutely" in favor of banning congressional stock trading and that there should be "no day trading for members of Congress or their families" [2]. Stirring words -- except that in October 2021, Business Insider reported Marshall had violated the STOCK Act by disclosing his dependent child's stock purchases up to seventeen months late [3]. It is one thing to say you support locking the barn door; it is another when your own horse has already bolted. On Medicare drug negotiation, Marshall voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which allowed Medicare to negotiate prescription drug prices for the first time and capped insulin at $35 for Medicare enrollees [4]. He has instead pushed his own pharmacy benefit manager reform bills, which target middlemen rather than the pharmaceutical companies themselves -- a distinction that happens to align neatly with the interests of the drugmakers funding his campaigns.

Marshall does earn partial credit on a few 80% items. He supported the Epstein Files Transparency Act, saying he was "ready to rip the band-aid off" on full disclosure [5]. He has introduced legislation to reduce taxation on Social Security benefits [6], though his broader position endorses restructuring benefits for future retirees -- a reform that makes fiscal hawks purr but leaves workers under fifty wondering what will be left. On term limits, he has called for term-limiting the Senate Parliamentarian and federal employees via his TERM Act, but has not co-sponsored a constitutional amendment to term-limit members of Congress themselves [7] -- a distinction so convenient it could have its own lobbying firm.

The money trail tells a familiar Kansas story. Koch Industries was among Marshall's major backers during his 2020 Senate race, which benefited from $29.8 million in outside spending [8]. He serves on the Senate Finance and Agriculture committees while receiving substantial contributions from the agribusiness, insurance, and energy sectors that appear before those very panels. His 98% Trump alignment score [1] and his vote to object to the 2020 electoral results round out the picture of a senator whose populist instincts are carefully curated to never actually threaten the donor class.

The bottom line on Doc Marshall is that he talks a better game than most. He will voice support for banning stock trades while violating the existing disclosure law, champion drug pricing reform that conveniently spares pharma, and push term limits for bureaucrats but not for himself. He is a physician who can diagnose every problem in Washington except the ones his own contributors cause -- and in the Senate, that selective blindness pays very well.

--- M001199: Brian J. Mast (Republican, FL, house) ---
Brian Mast is a Republican representing Florida's 21st congressional district and the current chairman of the House Foreign Affairs Committee. Before Congress, Mast served over twelve years in the U.S. Army as an explosive ordnance disposal technician under Joint Special Operations Command, earning the Bronze Star and Purple Heart before losing both legs to an IED in Afghanistan in 2010 [1]. His sacrifice is genuine and beyond dispute. What came after, however, is a story about how a war hero's credibility can be deployed in the service of some remarkably convenient policy positions — and some even more convenient stock trades.

On the 80% Bill's twenty planks, Mast's record is thinner than one might expect from someone who brands himself a government reformer. He has signed the U.S. Term Limits pledge and publicly endorsed congressional term limits [2]. He has cosponsored legislation to restrict foreign purchases of U.S. farmland and spoken about government transparency [3]. But that is roughly where the populist checklist ends. On banning congressional stock trading — the issue that polls above 80% with voters of both parties — Mast has not merely failed to act; he has actively demonstrated why the ban is needed. In 2021, he posted the second-best stock trading returns in all of Congress, according to Unusual Whales, with gains of up to 564% on Tilray Brands shares he purchased before voting in favor of the MORE Act to legalize cannabis [4]. He bought up to $100,000 in Virgin Galactic stock shortly after its founder's televised spaceflight and weeks after Virgin Galactic's president testified before Mast's own Transportation and Infrastructure subcommittee [5]. He then failed to disclose the trade on time, violating the STOCK Act — something he managed to do on three separate occasions, involving Virgin Galactic, Ideal Power, and an Aphria-to-Tilray share exchange, each reported months or more than a year past the legal deadline [5][6]. His office attributed the pattern to "administrative errors," a phrase that does a lot of heavy lifting when it appears three times.

On foreign policy, Mast is perhaps the most unapologetically hawkish member of the House. He volunteered with the Israeli Defense Forces in 2015 through the Sar-El program [7], wore his IDF uniform on the House floor after October 7, 2023 [8], and as Foreign Affairs chairman has instructed his staff to refer to the West Bank exclusively as "Judea and Samaria" [9]. He suggested that there are "very few innocent Palestinian civilians" and argued that humanitarian aid to Gaza "should be slowed down" [10]. On the question of ending forever wars and repealing the post-9/11 AUMFs — a plank of the 80% Bill supported by large majorities of Americans — Mast's entire career orientation runs in the opposite direction. He has shown no interest in constraining executive war-making authority. On banning dark money, the DISCLOSE Act, corporate PAC bans, Medicare drug negotiation, paid family leave, right to repair, banning junk fees, police body cameras, auditing the Pentagon, and funding Social Security, Mast has been either silent or opposed.

Follow the money and the portrait fills in. Mast's top donors across multiple cycles include Duty Free Americas, owned by the pro-Israel Falic family, and Amway/Alticor, the DeVos family enterprise [11]. Over his career, he has received nearly $700,000 from AIPAC and pro-Israel PACs and individuals [12]. In 2024 alone, he took $13,500 from pro-Israel PACs and $220,637 from pro-Israel individual donors [12]. These are the donors behind the man now chairing the committee that oversees U.S. foreign policy in the Middle East — a committee whose chairman has publicly denied the existence of innocent Palestinian civilians. The alignment between Mast's donor base and his policy positions is not subtle; it is architectural.

Brian Mast gave his legs for his country, and no one can take that from him. But the question the 80% Bill asks is not about personal sacrifice — it is about whether elected officials serve their constituents or their contributors. A congressman who traded cannabis stocks before voting on cannabis legalization, who violated disclosure laws three times, who takes nearly $700,000 from the Israel lobby while chairing the committee that shapes Middle East policy, and who has shown zero interest in the vast majority of reforms that 80% of Americans support, is not a reformer. He is the reform's subject.

Carol Miller's brand is "pro-coal, pro-jobs, pro-Second Amendment," but the 80% Bill asks a simpler question: pro-whom? When your husband's dealerships pocket millions in forgiven government loans, your stock trades go unreported, and your committee work protects the tax breaks of your biggest donors, the answer writes itself.

Rep. Joe Morelle is the rare species of Democrat who actually does some of the things he says he believes in — which, graded on the congressional curve, practically makes him a revolutionary. On the stock trading front, Morelle doesn't just talk the talk. He introduced H.R. 6731 to ban members of Congress, the President, Vice President, and federal judges from trading individual stocks. He voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which included Medicare drug price negotiation — a provision the pharmaceutical industry spent millions trying to kill. He has also pushed to expand drug price negotiation beyond Medicare to private insurance through the Lowering Drug Costs for American Families Act.

On election integrity, Morelle is a true believer — or at least plays one convincingly. His work on dark money transparency, voter protections, and campaign finance reform through the House Administration Committee aligns with several 80% Bill planks. But the absence of a strong stance on term limits, Pentagon audits, or a full corporate PAC ban suggests the reform impulse has its boundaries. Morelle is the kind of Democrat who will fight for the policies that are popular enough to tweet about while quietly cashing the checks that keep the machine running in Rochester's 25th district.

The alignment between her donors and her policy positions is unusually coherent -- she takes money from gun safety groups and votes for gun safety legislation, which is either refreshingly transparent or simply the expected transaction, depending on your cynicism level.

The bottom line on Lucy McBath is that she is a genuine article on the issues that brought her to Washington -- gun violence, healthcare costs, voting rights -- but a conventional Democrat on the structural reforms that would reshape how Washington itself operates. She will fight to lower your insulin costs and expand background checks, but do not expect her to lead the charge on banning her colleagues from trading stocks or imposing term limits on herself. Her story is powerful, her convictions on her signature issues are real, and her blind spots are precisely where the 80% Bill asks the most uncomfortable questions.
