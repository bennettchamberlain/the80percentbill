--- D000617: Suzan K. DelBene (Democrat, WA, house) ---
Suzan DelBene is the walking, legislating embodiment of the phrase "rules for thee, not for me." When you're worth nine figures, that's not a fine -- it's a rounding error.

You cannot credibly champion banning congressional stock trading while your family is sitting on a Microsoft fortune and filing disclosures nearly a year late. You cannot lead the DCCC -- the party's chief fundraising arm that hoovers up corporate PAC money -- and claim to support getting dark money out of politics. DelBene's 14-year tenure in Congress has produced genuine legislative work on privacy, family economic security, and health care access. But her role as DCCC chair and her personal financial entanglements make her the poster child for why Americans don't trust Congress to police itself. When the person leading your party's fundraising operation can't even file her own stock disclosures on time, the 80% agenda isn't just stalled -- it's being held hostage by the people who claim to support it.

--- D000618: Steve Daines (Republican, MT, senate) ---
Steve Daines is Montana's senior senator, a former Procter & Gamble executive who spent six years in China opening factories while the company eliminated thousands of American jobs, and who parlayed a stint at RightNow Technologies into a personal fortune estimated between $1.8 million and $28 million [1]. He co-sponsored a bipartisan bill with Elizabeth Warren to Ban Congressional Stock Trading [3], and he's signed onto Constitutional amendments for Congressional Term Limits, earning honors from U.S. Term Limits [4]. He also pushed bipartisan legislation to Protect US Farmland from purchase by foreign adversaries like China, which passed the Senate 91-7 as part of the NDAA [6].

But that's where the agreeable part ends. In March 2026, minutes before the filing deadline, Daines abruptly announced he would not seek re-election -- leaving Montanans to wonder whether the man who co-sponsored a stock trading ban and term limits pledges was, in the end, just another senator who talked reform while cashing the checks [14].

The bottom line: Steve Daines will champion the 80% Bill positions that cost his donors nothing -- term limits he'll never face, war authorization repeals that are already moot, and farmland protections that play well at the Billings rodeo. But the moment a bill threatens pharmaceutical revenue streams or demands transparency in campaign finance, Montana's senior senator suddenly discovers his inner free-market purist. His career score tells the story of a politician who learned in those Procter & Gamble boardrooms that you can sell anything with the right packaging.

--- D000622: Tammy Duckworth (Democrat, IL, senate) ---
Tammy Duckworth is one of the few members of Congress who has paid for American foreign policy with her own body. A Black Hawk helicopter pilot in the Illinois Army National Guard, she lost both legs and partial use of her right arm when her aircraft was struck by an RPG over Iraq in November 2004. She earned a Purple Heart, an Air Medal, and eventually a Senate seat, winning election from Illinois in 2016. She sits on the Armed Services, Commerce, Foreign Relations, and Veterans' Affairs committees, and in 2018 became the first sitting senator to give birth in office [1]. Her biography is the kind that makes most colleagues' campaign trail heroics look like community theater.

On the twenty provisions of the 80% Bill, Duckworth has a complicated but largely favorable record. She has been a vocal cosponsor of the Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act, joining Senators Ossoff and Kelly to prohibit members, spouses, and dependent children from trading individual stocks [2]. She cosponsored legislation to repeal the 1991 and 2002 AUMFs alongside Senators Kaine and Young, and has spoken forcefully about Congress shirking its war-powers responsibilities for two decades [3]. But there is a revealing asterisk: in 2023, she voted with 85 other senators to retain the far more consequential 2001 AUMF, the blank check that has underwritten every military adventure from Somalia to Syria [4]. Ending "forever wars," it seems, is easier when the wars in question are already over.

She voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which included Medicare drug price negotiation and a corporate minimum tax, and she has championed rooting out Pentagon waste, including cracking down on billions spent on duplicative camouflage uniforms [5][6]. She has supported the For the People Act's provisions on gerrymandering and dark money, and cosponsored efforts to overturn Citizens United [7]. On paid family leave, she is a natural champion, having personally forced a Senate rules change so she could bring her newborn onto the floor. She has voiced support for protecting Social Security and Medicare, though her record on a standalone wealth tax or closing the carried interest loophole is less clearly documented.

Follow the money and the picture gets more interesting. Duckworth sits on the Armed Services Committee, and defense contractors have noticed. Lockheed Martin's PAC contributed $5,000 in 2022, and members of her committee routinely rank among the top recipients of defense industry donations [8][9]. Her leadership PAC, Perimeter PAC, collects from the usual Democratic fundraising ecosystem of law firms, tech executives, and progressive interest groups [10]. None of this is unusual for a senator in her position, but it does add a footnote to her calls for Pentagon accountability: the people writing the checks are often the same ones cashing the Pentagon's.

Duckworth is, on balance, one of the more aligned senators with the 80% Bill's goals. She has put her name on stock trading bans, AUMF repeals, drug price negotiation, and campaign finance reform. But the 2001 AUMF vote is a reminder that even decorated combat veterans can find reasons to keep the machinery of war humming, and the defense dollars flowing into her campaign suggest the military-industrial complex considers her a safe bet. She has earned more credibility than most on these issues—literally bled for the country—but credibility and follow-through are not always the same thing.

Debbie Dingell is a Democrat who inherited more than a congressional seat. The portrait that emerges is of a congresswoman who talks a convincingly populist game while sitting atop an inherited auto-industry fortune and cashing checks from the very sectors she promises to regulate.
