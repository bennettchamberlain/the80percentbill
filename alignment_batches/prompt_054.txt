--- W000821: Bruce Westerman (Republican, AR, house) ---
Bruce Westerman, Republican of Arkansas's 4th congressional district, has served in the House since 2015, when he succeeded Tom Cotton. A Yale-trained forester and former Arkansas House majority leader — the first Republican to hold that title since Reconstruction — Westerman chairs the House Natural Resources Committee, a perch that gives him enormous influence over federal lands, energy, and timber policy[1]. His district spans more than 80 percent forested land in southern and western Arkansas, which makes his forestry credentials genuinely relevant and his donor list genuinely predictable[2].

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Westerman is a study in selective populism. He cosponsored legislation to prohibit the Chinese Communist Party from purchasing American farmland, aligning him with Article 9 (Protect US Farmland) — a position that costs him nothing with his base and even less with his donors[3]. He voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which contained Medicare drug price negotiation provisions central to Article 7, calling the bill "misleadingly named" and predicting it would "worsen inflation"[4]. He opposed paid family leave provisions in Build Back Better[5]. He has shown no public support for banning congressional stock trading (Article 1), ending dark money (Article 13), the DISCLOSE Act (Article 16), banning corporate PACs (Article 5), or congressional term limits (Article 20) — despite now serving his sixth term. He did, however, break from the most extreme wing of his party by voting to certify the 2020 Electoral College results, even after signing an amicus brief asking the Supreme Court to hear Texas v. Pennsylvania, a case seeking to throw out votes in four swing states[6][7].

That stock trading record deserves its own paragraph. In March 2025, after a decade of reporting zero stock trades as a member of Congress, Westerman suddenly disclosed over one hundred individual stock purchases totaling more than $1.6 million — many of them in petroleum companies like ExxonMobil, Chevron, BP, ConocoPhillips, and Shell[8]. These are companies directly regulated by his own committee. Rep. Yassamin Ansari accused him of corruption on the House floor, noting the trades occurred while he was writing legislation described as "an unprecedented giveaway to oil and gas companies"[9]. Westerman blamed an investment advisor who allegedly acted without his knowledge and said he was "already in the process of divesting"[10]. The episode is a walking advertisement for Article 1 of the 80% Bill — banning congressional stock trading — which Westerman has conspicuously never endorsed.

Follow the money and the picture sharpens further. The forest products industry is Westerman's second-largest career contributor, funneling more than $142,000 into his coffers since 2014, with Weyerhaeuser, Potlatch Corp, Deltic Timber, and Koch Industries among his top twenty donors[11]. During the 2016 cycle, he received more timber industry money than any Republican on Capitol Hill except Paul Ryan[12]. Oil and gas interests round out his donor portfolio nicely, given his committee chairmanship. He has authored legislation loosening forest protections on public lands that environmental groups say directly benefits his timber donors[13]. The ExxonMobil PAC has contributed to his campaigns[14].

Westerman is a congressman whose resume — Yale degree, engineering background, legitimate forestry expertise — suggests someone who could be a serious policy voice. Instead, the gap between his credentials and his actions tells the real story: a committee chairman who went a decade without trading a single stock, then bought $1.6 million in oil shares while writing oil-friendly legislation, all while collecting timber money to write timber-friendly laws. The 80% Bill exists because of contradictions exactly like these.

--- W000822: Bonnie Watson Coleman (Democrat, NJ, house) ---
Bonnie Watson Coleman has had the kind of career that makes you wonder why more politicians don't just say what they think. In 2015, she became the first Black woman to represent New Jersey in Congress [1], capping a political career that stretches back over 50 years through the state Assembly and the chairmanship of the New Jersey Democratic Party [2]. In November 2025, she announced she would not seek reelection, stepping down after "tremendous thought and reflection" [3]. She is leaving on her own terms, which in Washington is almost unheard of.

Watson Coleman is a member of the Congressional Progressive Caucus and the Congressional Black Caucus, and she has voted accordingly. Her 2024 GovTrack report card ranked her as the most politically left member of New Jersey's entire delegation [4]. She introduced the CROWN Act to ban hair discrimination, which passed the House but died in the Senate -- a fate that neatly summarizes the progressive experience in a divided Congress [1]. She sits on the Appropriations and Homeland Security committees, where she has pushed for funding on housing, education, and social safety net programs.

Where Watson Coleman has drawn the sharpest lines is on Israel and Gaza. In March 2025, she hosted a press conference demanding an arms embargo after Israel broke the ceasefire and resumed bombing Gaza, joined by Ilhan Omar, Greg Casar, and other Progressive Caucus members [5]. In a Newsweek op-ed, she wrote that "an arms embargo is America's only moral option" and that the United States is "complicit" in genocide [6]. She co-sponsored legislation to condition weapons sales to Israel [7]. These are positions that most Democrats will not touch, and Watson Coleman did not flinch.

Her health battles have been public and defining. In 2018, she was diagnosed with lung cancer and underwent surgery to remove a tumor -- she is now cancer-free [8]. In January 2021, she tested positive for COVID-19 after being forced to shelter with maskless Republican colleagues during the Capitol insurrection, and she wrote a Washington Post op-ed blaming them directly [9].

Her campaign funding tells a less fiery story: 58% of her 2024 cycle funds came from PACs, with the pharmaceuticals and health products industry as her top contributing sector at $118,495 [10]. It is an uncomfortable pairing for a progressive champion, though Watson Coleman has consistently voted for drug price negotiation and expanded Medicare coverage.

She is 80 years old and leaving Congress. The question NJ-12 will have to answer is whether anyone else is willing to be this blunt.

--- W000829: Tony Wied (Republican, WI, house) ---
Tony Wied went from pumping gas to pumping out party-line votes with a speed that suggests the Trump endorsement came with an instruction manual. The former owner of the Dino Stop convenience store chain in northeast Wisconsin, Wied sold his gas stations in 2022 and entered politics in 2024 with zero elected experience, a $500,000 self-loan to his campaign, and a presidential endorsement that cleared a three-way Republican primary. He won the special election to replace Mike Gallagher with 60 percent of the vote in deep-red WI-8 and has been a reliable MAGA foot soldier ever since.

Wied's signature legislative moment came when he voted for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, Trump's sprawling budget reconciliation package signed on July 4, 2025. The bill extended Trump-era tax cuts, raised the SALT deduction cap to $40,000, and -- here is the part Wied does not put in the press releases -- slashed $1.2 trillion from Medicaid and food assistance programs while imposing new work requirements. The Wisconsin Department of Health Services estimated 270,000 Wisconsinites would lose health insurance as a result. Green Bay Mayor Eric Genrich held a press conference to call Wied out directly, saying the bill "literally makes the poor poorer and the rich richer." Wied's response: "This bill is pro-American, pro-Wisconsinite, it puts more money back in people's pockets." Whose pockets, exactly, remains the operative question.

The bill's top-line beneficiaries were corporations and high earners. The Congressional Budget Office estimated it would add trillions to the national debt while delivering the largest benefits to the wealthiest households. Wied also voted for the Lower Health Care Premiums for All Americans Act, a Republican healthcare bill focused on deregulation and "choice" -- the kind of choice where you can pick any plan you want as long as you can afford it.

On the 80% Bill's twenty planks, Wied is a blank page with a Republican letterhead. No action on banning stock trading, ending dark money, auditing the Pentagon, banning corporate PACs, closing tax loopholes, paid family leave, term limits, or the DISCLOSE Act. His campaign received maximum donations from billionaire megadonor Richard Uihlein of ULINE, and his legislative focus areas -- transportation, commerce, armed forces, and crime -- suggest a freshman content to stay in his lane and vote as he is told.

MSNBC noted Wied might be "as close to fiscally conservative as MAGA gets," which is like being the tallest building in Topeka. The man who ran a chain of gas stations now represents a district of 750,000 people, and his voting record so far suggests he brought the same business model to Congress: keep the pumps running, do not ask questions about what is in the tank, and make sure the people at the top get their cut.

--- W000830: George Whitesides (Democrat, CA, house) ---
George Whitesides represents California's 27th district, a swing seat spanning the Santa Clarita Valley, Antelope Valley, and parts of the San Fernando Valley that he flipped from Republican Mike Garcia in 2024 by roughly 8,000 votes [1]. Before entering Congress, Whitesides spent a decade as the first CEO of Virgin Galactic, Richard Branson's space tourism venture, and before that served as NASA's chief of staff under Obama, earning the agency's Distinguished Service Medal [2]. He sits on the House Armed Services Committee and serves as Vice Ranking Member of Science, Space, and Technology -- committees that happen to oversee the exact industries where he built his fortune [3]. His district is home to Edwards Air Force Base, Lockheed Martin's legendary Skunk Works, and Air Force Plant 42, making him both the aerospace industry's former executive and its current congressional landlord.

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Whitesides has one genuinely impressive entry: he has been a vocal champion of banning congressional stock trading. He did not just talk about it -- he sold off between $2.3 million and $5.4 million in individual stock holdings, including shares in defense contractors Lockheed Martin and RTX Corporation, converting everything into passive index funds [4][5]. He co-sponsored the Restore Trust in Congress Act, which would prohibit members, spouses, and dependents from owning individual stocks [6]. He has also positioned himself as a defender of Social Security and Medicare, voting against a budget resolution that would have cut nearly $1 trillion from Medicare, and he co-sponsored the Protecting Healthcare and Lowering Costs Act to preserve Medicaid and ACA subsidies [7][8].

But the list of 80% Bill items where Whitesides has gone conspicuously quiet is considerably longer than the list where he has acted. There is no public record of him championing a lifetime lobbying ban (awkward for a man who walked straight from a CEO suite into Congress), no co-sponsorship of AUMF repeal legislation despite sitting on Armed Services, no vocal push for a Pentagon audit despite the Department of Defense failing its eighth consecutive one [9], and no visible action on the DISCLOSE Act, congressional term limits, the Epstein files, carried interest loopholes, or right to repair. His campaign website speaks of lowering costs in broad strokes but avoids the specific populist planks -- banning corporate purchases of single-family homes, protecting farmland from foreign buyers, banning junk fees -- that poll at 80% or above [10].

Follow the money and the silence starts to make sense. Whitesides raised over $5.2 million for his 2024 campaign, with $700,368 coming from PACs [11]. While 75% of his PAC money came from ideological committees and 23% from labor, his personal portfolio told a different story: he held stock in Lockheed Martin, RTX, JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Comcast, Microsoft, and Johnson & Johnson before divesting [4]. In Congress, he secured billions in NDAA funding for CA-27 defense operations at Skunk Works, Edwards AFB, and Plant 42, plus earmarks for local defense contractors like Techno Planet and Cicoil [12]. He may have sold the stocks, but he is still very much in the business of delivering for the defense-industrial complex that made his career.

Whitesides is the kind of freshman who does one reform loudly -- the stock trading ban is real and commendable -- and then uses it as a shield against questions about everything else. He urged Pentagon oversight of AI contracts with Anthropic [13], which sounds progressive until you realize it is essentially protecting defense procurement norms that benefit his district's contractors. The 80% Bill asks for twenty reforms that Americans overwhelmingly support. Whitesides has meaningfully championed perhaps two of them. For a man who once ran a company whose whole pitch was reaching new heights, his legislative ambitions remain stubbornly earthbound.

--- W000831: James R. Walkinshaw (Democrat, VA, house) ---
James Walkinshaw's path to Congress is a masterclass in the Washington art of succession planning. Spend a decade as chief of staff to a powerful congressman. Win a seat on the county board of supervisors. Wait for your mentor to pass away. Win the special election by 50 points in a district so blue it makes the ocean jealous. It's less a campaign and more an inheritance.

The heir to Gerry Connolly's 11th District throne arrived in September 2025 with an impressively curated resume: ten years running Connolly's legislative operation, a term on the Fairfax County Board where he championed collective bargaining and gun safety, and a campaign website that hits every correct Democratic note about protecting federal workers, lowering drug prices, and standing up to Trump. He was promptly appointed to the Oversight Committee -- the same committee his mentor chaired -- because in Washington, some seats might as well have name plates bolted to them.

But here's where the 80% Bill lens gets interesting. Walkinshaw founded the Congressional Federal Workforce Caucus and introduced 17 bills in his first months, many inherited directly from Connolly. He talks a good game on lowering costs and protecting workers. Yet when it came time to actually fund his campaign, he had no objection to Protect Progress -- the crypto industry super PAC affiliated with Fairshake -- dropping over $1 million in media buys to secure his primary victory. This is the same crypto PAC network that spent over $100 million in 2024 fighting financial regulations. Connolly himself earned an F grade from Stand With Crypto for voting against the industry. Walkinshaw, by contrast, helpfully lists "blockchain technology" as an economic opportunity on his priorities page. Funny how a million dollars can shift the generational perspective.

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Walkinshaw occupies that frustrating middle ground of the modern establishment Democrat: rhetorically aligned with popular reforms, structurally disinclined to pursue them. He'll talk about lowering drug prices but hasn't co-sponsored Medicare negotiation expansion. He'll champion federal workers while accepting crypto PAC money that undermines the very financial oversight his committee is supposed to provide. He introduced the Limit on Sweeping Executive Reorganization Act to check Trump's power -- genuinely useful legislation -- but has been conspicuously quiet on banning congressional stock trading, dark money, or corporate PACs.

The cruelest irony may be that Walkinshaw is genuinely competent. His constituent services are reportedly excellent, his attendance record near-perfect (missing just 2 of 201 votes), and his focus on federal IT modernization and veteran employment is substantive. He's exactly the kind of well-meaning insider who makes the system work just well enough that nobody demands it actually change.

--- Y000064: Todd Young (Republican, IN, senate) ---
Todd Young is the senior senator from Indiana, a Marine veteran, a former Heritage Foundation staffer, and the closest thing the modern Republican Party has to a policy intellectual who still gets invited to leadership meetings. First elected to the House in 2010 and then to the Senate in 2016 — beating fellow Hoosier Marlin Stutzman in a bruising primary backed by Mitch McConnell's operation — Young has carved out a niche as a serious legislator willing to work across the aisle on select issues while remaining reliably conservative on everything else [1]. His landmark achievement is the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022, a bipartisan bill he co-authored to invest billions in domestic semiconductor manufacturing, which he has defended even against Donald Trump's calls to repeal it [2]. He is a member of the Republican Main Street Partnership, carries a 77 percent lifetime rating from the American Conservative Union, and voted with Trump about 84 percent of the time during the first administration [3]. He is, in other words, an establishment Republican who occasionally does something interesting.

Where Young genuinely distinguishes himself from most of his caucus is on ending forever wars. He has repeatedly introduced bipartisan legislation with Senator Tim Kaine to repeal the 1991 and 2002 Authorizations for Use of Military Force against Iraq, arguing that "Congress must not shy away from this debate" [4]. This is one of the twenty issues in the 80% Bill, and Young deserves real credit for it — he has put his name on legislation, made public arguments, and pushed the issue when it was not fashionable within his party. It is a rare instance of a Republican senator aligning with supermajority public opinion on a matter that involves checking executive power, and it should not be dismissed.

The credit stops there. On the remaining nineteen issues where eighty percent of Americans agree, Young's record ranges from indifferent to actively hostile. He has never supported banning congressional stock trading, has shown no interest in the DISCLOSE Act or overturning Citizens United, and has opposed Medicare drug price negotiation [5]. He has not championed closing the carried interest loophole, funding Social Security, or banning junk fees. On campaign finance reform in particular, his posture is grimly ironic: during his 2016 Senate race, 94 percent of outside spending supporting Young or attacking his opponent came from out-of-state Super PACs and dark money groups that do not disclose their donors [6]. He is a beneficiary of the very system the 80% Bill would dismantle, and he has never once suggested that system needs fixing.

The donor ledger tells the rest of the story. Young has received nearly $3 million from the National Rifle Association over his career, earning an A+ grade and a 100 percent rating from the organization [7]. His top contributing industries include securities and investment, insurance, and real estate — sectors with a direct interest in the tax code he helps write from the Senate Finance Committee [8]. He has collected significant contributions from lobbyists, and his campaign apparatus has been fueled by precisely the kind of corporate PAC money that the 80% Bill would ban [9]. None of this is illegal. All of it is clarifying.

Todd Young is the most capable legislator in Indiana's congressional delegation, and that is exactly what makes his selective courage so frustrating. He will cross the aisle to build semiconductors and repeal outdated war authorizations, but he will not touch stock trading bans, dark money disclosure, Pentagon audits, or tax fairness — the reforms that would restructure the power relationships that keep him in office. He proves that the problem in Washington is not always incompetence. Sometimes it is competence deployed in the service of the status quo.

--- Y000067: Rudy Yakym III (Republican, IN, house) ---
Rudy Yakym III came to Congress the way one arrives at a country club — through the right introductions. After the tragic death of Representative Jackie Walorski in an August 2022 car accident, Indiana's 2nd District needed a replacement, and the local Republican precinct committee members selected Yakym, Walorski's former campaign finance director, on the first ballot from a field of twelve candidates [1]. His qualifications were less legislative than logistical: a sales manager at a broadband company, a vice president at the Bradley Company (a commercial real estate firm), and most recently a director of growth initiatives at Kem Krest, an Elkhart distribution company [2]. He held an MBA from Notre Dame and, critically, the endorsement of the party establishment, having served on Governor Holcomb's transition team and on Todd Young's campaign finance committee. He won the general election with over 64 percent of the vote in a district that would elect a scarecrow if it had an R next to its name.

On the twenty issues that command supermajority support among American voters, Yakym's record is strikingly thin for a man who sits on the most powerful tax-writing committee in Congress. As a member of the House Ways and Means Committee, he is positioned to influence tax policy, Social Security, and Medicare — three pillars of the 80% Bill [3]. Yet his signature legislative contribution has been enthusiastic support for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, which he championed as "the most pro-growth, pro-worker, pro-business, pro-family, and pro-America legislation of our generation" [4]. What the bill actually delivered was a permanent extension of the 2017 Trump tax cuts — a windfall tilted heavily toward corporations and high earners — while doing nothing to close the carried interest loophole, fund Social Security, or address junk fees. On banning congressional stock trading, auditing the Pentagon, ending dark money, or any of the structural reforms that would inconvenience the donor class Yakym spent his career serving, he has been perfectly silent.

Yakym's Heritage Action score of 66 percent in the 118th Congress places him squarely in the Republican mainstream — conservative enough to avoid a primary but not so ideological as to alarm the business interests that fund his campaigns [5]. He has shown no interest in Medicare drug negotiation, paid family leave, police body cameras, or the DISCLOSE Act. He has not cosponsored legislation to ban corporate PACs, despite the fact that he owes his seat to a party apparatus fueled by exactly that kind of money. His committee work on Ways and Means focuses on "pro-growth tax, regulatory, and trade policies so manufacturers, innovators, and job creators can expand, hire, and invest" — which is a polished way of saying he is there to protect the tax code as his donors prefer it [6].

The financial picture completes the portrait. Yakym's career arc — from campaign finance director to commercial real estate executive to congressman — is a master class in the revolving door between money and power that the 80% Bill seeks to dismantle [7]. His OpenSecrets profile shows a fundraising operation that leans heavily on PAC money and industry contributions, with the real estate, finance, and insurance sectors well represented among his backers [8]. He sits on the Social Security subcommittee of Ways and Means, a position of enormous consequence for millions of Americans, and his primary legislative achievement has been to vote for a tax bill that added trillions to the deficit while leaving Social Security's solvency crisis unaddressed. In Indiana's 2nd District, where the median household income hovers below the national average and manufacturing jobs have been bleeding out for decades, Yakym represents not the people who sent him to Washington but the system that installed him there.

--- Z000018: Ryan K. Zinke (Republican, MT, house) ---
Ryan Zinke represents Montana's 1st Congressional District and is, depending on your perspective, either a decorated Navy SEAL commander who served his country with distinction or the only Interior Secretary in modern history to resign under the weight of eighteen separate federal investigations. Both things are true. Zinke served in Congress from 2015 to 2017, left to run the Department of the Interior under President Trump, departed that post in December 2018 amid a blizzard of ethics probes, spent a brief interlude as a lobbyist for energy companies he had just finished regulating, then returned to Congress in 2023 as though nothing had happened [1][2][3]. In March 2026, he announced he would not seek re-election, citing health issues and, somewhat poetically, his belief in term limits [4].

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Zinke's record is a masterclass in saying popular things while doing the opposite. He claims to support term limits -- convenient timing, given he's already retiring [4]. On auditing the Pentagon, he sits on the House Appropriations Committee and has expressed support for military spending accountability in the abstract, but has never co-sponsored audit legislation despite the Pentagon failing its financial audit for the eighth consecutive year [5][6]. On protecting US farmland and public lands, Zinke's Interior Department oversaw the largest reduction in federal land protections in American history, shrinking Bears Ears and Grand Staircase-Escalante national monuments by two million acres to open them to oil and gas drilling [7]. He mandated quarterly oil and gas lease sales and ordered the Bureau of Land Management to approve drilling permits within 30 days [8]. If there is a way to square "protect US farmland" with auctioning off 700,000 acres of Western public land for fracking, Zinke has not explained it.

On campaign finance reform, dark money, and the DISCLOSE Act, Zinke's silence is deafening but his fundraising is loud. He has never co-sponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, ban corporate PACs, or require disclosure of dark money donors. He has taken no position on the lifetime lobbying ban -- which is rich, given that after leaving Interior he immediately joined Turnberry Solutions, a D.C. lobbying firm founded by Trump campaign aides, where he worked on energy and defense issues alongside Corey Lewandowski [9][10]. He then signed on as a consultant for Cressman Tubular Products and Oasis Petroleum -- the same oil company whose executives had donated $41,750 to his campaigns while he was in Congress [11][3]. The revolving door did not just spin for Zinke; it installed a VIP lane.

Follow the money and the policy positions write themselves. Over his career, Zinke has collected more than $345,000 from oil and gas companies, including PAC contributions from Halliburton ($3,000), ExxonMobil ($5,000), ConocoPhillips ($5,000), Koch Industries ($2,500), and Peabody Energy ($1,000) [12][13]. Energy and natural resources PACs contributed $126,000 in the 2015-16 cycle alone [12]. The Interior Department Inspector General found that Zinke "knowingly provided incorrect, incomplete, and misleading answers" to federal investigators in two separate probes -- one involving a Montana real estate deal and another concerning his decision to block two Native American tribes from opening a casino in Connecticut [14][15]. The DOJ declined to prosecute, but the IG's findings remain on the public record like a neon "For Sale" sign.

The bottom line on Ryan Zinke is that he is the living embodiment of why 80% of Americans want a lifetime lobbying ban and dark money disclosure. He took oil money, ran the agency that regulates oil companies, resigned under investigation, lobbied for those same companies, returned to Congress, and is now retiring without having supported a single major reform bill on the 80% Bill list. Montana deserves a representative who fights for the reforms its voters overwhelmingly support, not one who treats public service as a rest stop between lobbying gigs.
