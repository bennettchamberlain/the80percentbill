--- L000596: Anna Paulina Luna (Republican, FL, house) ---
Anna Paulina Luna is a Republican representing Florida's 13th congressional district, an Air Force veteran, and the first Mexican-American woman elected to Congress from Florida [1]. Elected in 2022 with Donald Trump's endorsement and reelected in 2024, she serves on the Oversight and Government Reform Committee and the Foreign Affairs Committee, and chairs the Task Force on the Declassification of Federal Secrets [2]. She has built a brand as a populist firebrand willing to buck her own party's leadership on issues of government transparency and congressional corruption — a brand that, upon closer inspection, has a few interesting cracks in the finish.

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Luna checks some genuinely notable boxes. She has been one of the loudest voices in Congress demanding a ban on congressional stock trading, going so far as to file a discharge petition in December 2025 to force a floor vote after House leadership let the issue languish [3]. She was among the original sixteen cosponsors of the Restore Trust in Congress Act, the bipartisan bill to ban members and their spouses from owning or trading individual stocks [4]. She has publicly supported a lifetime lobbying ban for members of Congress and their staff [5]. And as chair of the declassification task force, she has aggressively pushed for the release of the Epstein client files, demanding Attorney General Pam Bondi hand over over five million reviewed documents with no unnecessary redactions [6]. On term limits, she has spoken passionately in their favor at press conferences alongside Governor DeSantis — but when it came time to cosponsor the actual term limits resolution (H.J.Res.12) in January 2025, she declined, prompting the advocacy group U.S. Term Limits to put up billboards in her district calling her out for breaking her pledge [7].

That stock-trading crusade deserves its own paragraph, because it is where Luna's story gets deliciously complicated. In July 2024, financial disclosures revealed that Luna had invested between $250,001 and $500,000 in America First Natural Resources LLC, a private energy company founded and managed by Bruce N. Rosenthal, a donor who has given more than $30,000 to Luna's campaigns and her APL PAC since 2020 [8]. Luna's office insisted this was an "investment," not stock ownership — a distinction roughly as convincing as calling a duck a "non-goose waterfowl." Separate disclosures showed she had purchased $15,000 to $50,000 in shares of Triton Energy weeks before introducing legislation favorable to fracking operations [9]. Ethics groups noted the obvious: a member of Congress investing hundreds of thousands of dollars in a donor's company that could be affected by her committee work is precisely the kind of arrangement her own stock-trading ban purports to address [8]. She has been notably quiet on banning corporate PACs, the DISCLOSE Act, overturning Citizens United, and most campaign finance reforms beyond the stock-trading issue.

Luna's fundraising has leaned heavily on small-dollar donations through WinRed, with PAC contributions making up a relatively modest share of her war chest [10]. But the Rosenthal connection illustrates that the corruption the 80% Bill targets is not always about PAC checks — sometimes it is about the quieter, more personal financial entanglements between a lawmaker and her benefactors. On the remaining planks of the 80% Bill — taxing the ultra-wealthy, Medicare drug negotiation, paid family leave, right to repair, banning junk fees, funding Social Security, police body cameras, ending forever wars, Pentagon audits, fair elections — Luna has shown little legislative interest, leaving the populist cupboard notably bare once you get past the headline issues.

Anna Paulina Luna is the rare member of Congress who genuinely wants to ban stock trading — and who also has a six-figure investment in a donor's energy firm that her own bill would not clearly prohibit. She will fight to release the Epstein files but will not cosponsor the term limits resolution she pledged to support. She is a reformer whose reforms have a curious habit of stopping just short of her own interests, which makes her less a hypocrite than a perfect case study in why the 80% Bill needs to be a package deal.

Laurel Lee came to Congress with a resume tailor-made for credibility on election reform and institutional integrity.
