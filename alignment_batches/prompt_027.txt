--- K000391: Raja Krishnamoorthi (Democrat, IL, house) ---
Raja Krishnamoorthi represents Illinois's 8th Congressional District, a diverse suburban stretch west of Chicago that he has held since 2017. Born in New Delhi and raised in Peoria, Krishnamoorthi is a former state special assistant attorney general and small-business founder who serves on the House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence and previously sat on the Oversight Committee [1]. He has built a reputation as a pragmatic Democrat who talks often about kitchen-table economics, which makes his relationship with the 80% Bill's twenty proposals an illuminating study in saying the right things at the right volume.

On paper, Krishnamoorthi checks several of the 80% Bill's boxes. He cosponsored the TRUST in Congress Act to ban congressional stock trading after the STOCK Act's enforcement proved toothless [2], and he has publicly backed Medicare drug price negotiation, a cause he championed well before it became law in the Inflation Reduction Act [3]. He has spoken favorably about paid family leave and has supported the DISCLOSE Act to increase transparency in campaign finance [4]. He cosponsored the Right to Repair Act and has called for banning junk fees in consumer financial services [5]. On the Oversight Committee, he was vocal about the need for Pentagon accountability and backed audit requirements for the Department of Defense [6].

Where Krishnamoorthi gets quieter is on the proposals that would most directly threaten his own political infrastructure. He has not cosponsored a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United, nor has he backed a lifetime lobbying ban for former members of Congress [7]. Congressional term limits, which poll above 80% with voters, have not earned his name on any bill. On taxing the ultra-wealthy and closing the carried interest loophole, he has offered supportive rhetoric in committee hearings but has not signed onto the most aggressive legislative vehicles [8]. The AUMF repeal to end forever wars has similarly lacked his cosponsorship despite his seat on the Intelligence Committee, where such matters are most directly relevant [1].

The donor picture helps explain some of these gaps. Krishnamoorthi's top funding sources include the securities and investment industry, health professionals, and the tech sector [9]. For the 2024 cycle, he raised over $3.5 million, with significant contributions from corporate PACs including those affiliated with AT&T, Comcast, and major pharmaceutical companies [10]. For someone who champions drug price negotiation, accepting substantial pharma PAC money creates a tension that his constituents might find worth examining. His leadership PAC further extends his fundraising reach into industries that would be directly affected by several 80% Bill provisions [9].

Krishnamoorthi is the type of representative the 80% Bill was designed to test: smart, well-spoken, and genuinely supportive of popular reforms right up to the point where they intersect with the donor class that sustains his career. He will vote for the reforms that have already crossed the political safety threshold, but the structural changes that would reshape how power and money flow through Congress — banning corporate PACs, imposing term limits, ending dark money — remain conspicuously absent from his legislative priorities [7][10].

--- K000392: David Kustoff (Republican, TN, house) ---
David Kustoff has spent his career building a fortress around the financial industry, and the people of Tennessee's 8th District get to watch from outside the moat. A former U.S. Attorney turned House Financial Services Committee stalwart, Kustoff has wielded his committee seat like a shield for Wall Street — applauding Trump's rollback of Dodd-Frank protections while his own stock portfolio quietly churns away in the background.

Since 2020, Kustoff has filed 19 stock transactions, with a heavy tilt toward the technology sector — about 42% of his trades — including purchases in Microsoft and TJX Companies. He has not co-sponsored any legislation to ban congressional stock trading, because why would you lock the door to the vault you're standing inside? When the GameStop saga exposed the rigged casino of retail trading, Kustoff went on Newsmax to warn against — you guessed it — more regulation. The little guy gets squeezed, and Kustoff's prescription is to make sure the squeeze stays legal.

On the 80% Bill's core issues, Kustoff is a ghost. No support for banning corporate PACs, no interest in the DISCLOSE Act, no movement on term limits (he's been comfortably re-elected since 2017 with minimal opposition in his deep-red district). He has shown zero appetite for Pentagon audits, paid family leave, or closing tax loopholes — the kind of kitchen-table issues his rural constituents actually care about. His top donor industries revolve around finance, insurance, and real estate — the very sectors his committee is supposed to oversee.

Kustoff's loyalty to the party establishment runs deep. He chaired the Shelby County GOP, ran George W. Bush's Tennessee campaigns in 2000 and 2004, and was rewarded with a U.S. Attorney appointment. After the 2020 election, he joined the lawsuit asking the Supreme Court to throw out votes in Georgia, Michigan, Pennsylvania, and Wisconsin — a move to overturn the will of millions of voters while claiming to represent the people of West Tennessee.

His official website is a masterclass in saying nothing: press releases about ribbon cuttings, letters to agencies, and carefully worded statements that never threaten the donor class. The 80% Bill asks whether your representative works for you or for the industries that fund their campaigns. With Kustoff, the financial disclosures answer that question before his voting record even gets a chance to.

--- K000393: John Kennedy (Republican, LA, senate) ---
John Neely Kennedy has represented Louisiana in the United States Senate since 2017, sitting on the Judiciary, Appropriations, Banking, and Budget committees -- a portfolio that gives him direct jurisdiction over many of the 80% Bill's core issues. A former state treasurer who served two decades in Baton Rouge before arriving in Washington, Kennedy is best known for his folksy one-liners and his ability to deliver withering soundbites at Senate hearings that go viral on social media [1]. He is, in the parlance of political theater, a character -- the drawling country lawyer who just happens to have a degree from Oxford. Whether the act translates into action on the issues Americans care about most is another question entirely.

On transparency and accountability measures, Kennedy's record is a study in selective outrage. He voted yes on the Epstein Files Transparency Act in 2025 after initially voting against an earlier Democratic effort to force the same disclosures -- a pivot that coincided with overwhelming bipartisan momentum and public pressure [2]. He has backed legislation to expose third-party litigation funding by foreign entities and supported the Preventing Foreign Interference in American Elections Act [3]. But on the DISCLOSE Act, which would require dark money groups to reveal their donors in elections, Kennedy has voted with the Republican bloc to filibuster the measure every time it has come to the floor [4]. On the congressional stock trading ban, Kennedy has not co-sponsored any of the leading bills and has made no public push for a floor vote. The pattern is familiar: transparency is a virtue when it targets foreign adversaries, but an imposition when it might inconvenience domestic donors.

On economic populism, Kennedy talks a populist game while voting a corporate one. He has introduced bipartisan legislation targeting pharmacy benefit managers and middlemen markups on prescription drugs, declaring that "middlemen negotiators like PBMs should not be receiving the benefits of lower drug costs" [5]. Yet he voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which contained the first-ever Medicare drug price negotiation provisions and a $2,000 out-of-pocket cap for seniors [6]. He opposed the billionaire minimum tax and has fought efforts to close corporate tax loopholes, arguing that raising taxes on businesses simply results in "higher prices, lower wages, lower stock prices and lower 401k balances" [7]. On Social Security, he has not championed efforts to strengthen the program's funding through measures like lifting the payroll tax cap. His "Same Care, Lower Cost Act" would equalize Medicare hospital payment rates -- a reasonable reform, but a narrow one compared to the structural changes most Americans say they want [5].

The money tells the familiar Louisiana story. Kennedy raised over $19 million for his 2022 reelection campaign, crushing twelve opponents in an open primary with 62 percent of the vote without a runoff [1]. His top industry contributors include oil and gas, securities and investment firms, insurance companies, and real estate interests -- precisely the sectors that benefit from blocking the 80% Bill's proposals on taxing the ultra-wealthy, banning corporate PACs, and ending junk fees [8]. Kennedy sits on the Banking Committee, which oversees the financial industry that ranks among his top donors, and on the Appropriations Committee, which directs federal spending to the defense and energy sectors that fund his campaigns [8].

The bottom line on John Kennedy is that the colorful quips mask a conventional record. He will roast a Biden nominee with a one-liner that gets ten million views, but he will not vote to let Medicare negotiate drug prices or require dark money groups to disclose their donors. He is a populist in tone and an establishment Republican in practice -- entertaining enough to make you forget, at least until you check the voting record, that he has opposed the majority of reforms that 80 percent of Americans say they want.

--- K000394: Andy Kim (Democrat, NJ, senate) ---
Andy Kim's biography reads like it was written by an overeager screenwriter pitching "The West Wing" reboot. The son of Korean immigrants -- his father a scientist, his mother a nurse -- Kim grew up in Moorestown, New Jersey, became a Rhodes Scholar at Oxford (where he befriended Pete Buttigieg), earned a doctorate in international relations, served as a civilian adviser to Generals Petraeus and Allen in Afghanistan, and then joined the National Security Council under President Obama at age 31 [1]. When ISIS trapped thousands of Yazidis in Iraq's Sinjar Mountains in 2014, it was Kim who drafted the plan Obama signed to airdrop supplies and launch airstrikes [2]. He is, by any measure, absurdly qualified.

What makes Kim unusual in the Senate is that his legislative record actually matches his campaign rhetoric. He introduced the Restoring Trust in Public Servants Act, which would ban stock ownership and trading across all three branches of government -- not just Congress, but the President, federal judges, and senior executive branch officials -- with a penalty of a full month's salary for each month out of compliance [3]. He co-led a bipartisan letter to Speaker Johnson calling for a floor vote on stock trading bans while still in the House [4]. He cosponsored the Ban Corporate PACs Act alongside Senators Slotkin and Warren, calling it "a straightforward and critical step to get big money out of our elections" [5]. His Restoring Trust Act would also impose a lifetime ban on former members lobbying on behalf of foreign entities [3].

On the 80% Bill's housing provisions, Kim cosponsored the American Homeownership Act targeting private equity firms that buy up family homes, and backed Senator Warner's LIFT Homebuyers Act to create below-market mortgages for first-generation buyers [6]. He has pushed publicly for paid family leave, pressing the Labor Secretary nominee on the issue during confirmation hearings [7]. As a former AUMF cosponsor in the House, Kim supported repealing the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force, stating that "Congress must act and be as precise as possible about the potential force we will call upon" [8].

Kim has not been without controversy. He voted four times to block weapons sales to Israel, citing the "unprecedented level of human misery" and "humanitarian catastrophe" in Gaza [9]. This earned him sharp criticism from pro-Israel groups and praise from progressives -- a position that required genuine political courage in a state with a significant and engaged Jewish community. His first year in the Senate produced 67 pieces of legislation: 26 introduced, 18 co-led, and 23 cosponsored [10]. His attendance record is near-perfect, missing just 7 of 730 roll call votes across his career [11].

The donor picture is notably clean for a senator. His top contributor in 2024 was Centerview Partners at $66,200 through a joint fundraising committee [12]. He does not accept corporate PAC money -- a position he holds not just as campaign rhetoric but as active legislation. Kim is that rare creature in Washington: a politician whose voting record is more progressive than his talking points. Whether the Senate will reward or punish that remains to be seen.

--- K000397: Young Kim (Republican, CA, house) ---
Young Kim represents California's 40th Congressional District, covering parts of Orange, San Bernardino, and Riverside counties. Born in South Korea, she immigrated to Guam at age twelve and eventually made her way to California, where she earned a degree from USC and spent over two decades as a staffer for longtime Congressman Ed Royce [1][2]. She was among the first Korean American women elected to Congress in 2020 and now sits on both the House Financial Services Committee and the House Foreign Affairs Committee, where she chairs the Subcommittee on East Asia and the Pacific [3][4]. Her biography is a genuine American success story -- which makes it all the more instructive to watch how she votes once she gets to Washington.

On the 80% Bill's twenty planks, Kim's record is a masterclass in selective populism. She has pushed for cost transparency and co-authored a bipartisan bill to crack down on credit repair scams [5]. She talks a good game on junk fees, championing the Hotel Fees Transparency Act to ensure advertised hotel prices match what consumers actually pay [6]. But when it came time to vote on the CFPB's rule capping credit card late fees from $32 to $8 -- the kind of junk fee that actually hits working families -- Kim voted with her party to kill it [7][8]. On Medicare drug negotiation, she voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which included the historic provision allowing Medicare to negotiate prescription drug prices directly with pharmaceutical companies, a policy supported by roughly 80% of Americans [9]. She has not co-sponsored legislation to ban congressional stock trading, has taken no public position on a lifetime lobbying ban, and has been silent on the DISCLOSE Act, Citizens United, and congressional term limits. On foreign farmland, she has stayed on the sidelines despite her party's own enthusiasm for banning Chinese purchases of American agricultural land [10].

The money trail helps explain the silences. Kim has enthusiastically accepted corporate PAC contributions throughout her career. During her first run in 2018, more than 90% of the $2 million-plus in corporate PAC money flowing into contested Orange County races went to Republican candidates including Kim, with donors like Chevron and Koch Industries writing checks directly to her campaign committee [11]. She now sits on the Financial Services Committee -- a post sometimes called "the cash register of Congress" -- where she oversees the same banking and credit card industries whose PACs fund her campaigns [3][12]. Her vote to block the CFPB's late-fee cap looks less like principled deregulation and more like constituent services for her actual constituents: the ones with lobbyists.

Kim's profile is a familiar Washington type: the immigrant-success-story Republican who supports popular ideas in press releases and opposes them on the House floor. She will tell you she fights for transparency and lower costs, and she means it -- as long as "lower costs" does not involve the pharmaceutical companies, credit card giants, or defense contractors who keep her campaign account healthy. The 80% Bill asks a simple question: will you vote for what your voters overwhelmingly want? Kim's answer, judging by the record, is "only when the donors don't mind."

The donor picture fills in the blanks. It is a remarkable coincidence that a congressman with these donors has found so little to say about these issues. Kean represents a affluent, competitive suburban district where voters overwhelmingly support the 80% Bill's provisions. Whether he represents their interests or his donors' is the question his next election will answer.
