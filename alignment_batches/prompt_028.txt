--- K000403: Mike Kennedy (Republican, UT, house) ---
Mike Kennedy is a doctor, a lawyer, and a congressman — a triple threat to anyone hoping for clear policy positions on anything in the 80% Bill. The freshman representative from Utah's 3rd District arrived in Washington in January 2025 with a resume that screams overachiever and a voting record that screams party line. He earned his MD from Michigan State, his JD from BYU while running a medical practice during the day and taking law classes at night, and apparently still found time to serve in the Utah state legislature for over a decade. You'd think a man with that kind of work ethic could find a single populist bone in his body, but so far the search has come up empty.

Kennedy sits on the Natural Resources, Science, and Transportation committees — a portfolio that positions him perfectly to rubber-stamp the GOP's agenda on public lands transfers, deregulation, and highway spending without ever having to confront uncomfortable questions about corporate PACs, dark money, or why his colleagues are trading stocks like day traders. His legislative focus has been on public lands and energy — which in Utah Republican terms means making it easier for extraction industries to access federal land, not protecting it.

As a physician, Kennedy could be a powerful voice for Medicare drug price negotiation, banning junk fees in healthcare billing, or pushing for paid family leave. Instead, his healthcare legacy from the state legislature consists primarily of authoring Utah's transgender care ban for minors. Pharmaceutical companies contributed to his campaign, and he's shown no interest in biting the hand that feeds. He received $7,935 from pharmaceutical and health products interests — modest by congressional standards, but enough to buy silence on drug pricing reform.

On DOGE and government spending cuts, Kennedy has been a loyal foot soldier, voting for rescission packages while expressing mild concern that the cuts might affect Utah's national parks. That's the Kennedy brand in a nutshell: vote yes, express concern, move on. He told the Deseret News that government shutdowns are a "terrible weapon" and proposed procedural fixes — the kind of technocratic tinkering that sounds reasonable while dodging every structural reform that actually matters, from term limits to lobbying bans to ending dark money in politics.

Utah's 3rd District deserves a representative who uses his triple credentials to fight for the people, not one who uses them to more eloquently defend the status quo.

--- K000404: Kimberlyn King-Hinds (Republican, MP, house) ---
Kimberlyn King-Hinds is the delegate from the Commonwealth of the Northern Mariana Islands, a Republican, and the first woman from the CNMI ever to hold the seat [1]. She cannot vote on final passage of legislation on the House floor. Let that sink in: the person sent to Washington to represent roughly 47,000 American citizens in the middle of the Pacific has about as much legislative power as a particularly persuasive Post-it note.

Born and raised on the island of Tinian -- yes, the same Tinian from which the Enola Gay departed -- King-Hinds earned a JD from the University of Hawaii and spent over a decade practicing family law and civil litigation before pivoting to public service [2]. Her legislative agenda revolves around a "three-pronged approach": revive tourism (including lobbying to restore the Annex VI exemption on U.S.-China flights), extend the CW worker visa program that keeps the islands' economy from collapsing, and lower the cost of living through utility reform [4]. She sits on the Committees on Small Business, Transportation and Infrastructure, and Veterans' Affairs [5]. Her Small Business Committee scored an early win when it unanimously advanced H.R. 3496, the Northern Mariana Islands Small Business Access Act, which would give CNMI entrepreneurs access to SBA microloans for the first time [6].

On the 80% Bill's twenty articles, King-Hinds occupies a fascinating no-man's-land. As a non-voting delegate she cannot cast a floor vote on any of them, and she has not sponsored or cosponsored legislation corresponding to most of the platform's provisions. Her FEC filings show modest fundraising through her "King-Hinds for Congress" committee, and no OpenSecrets profile exists -- a natural consequence of representing a territory so small that big-dollar donors rarely bother [7]. Whether that insulation from mainland money makes her more independent or simply more invisible is a question the CNMI's 47,000 residents have been asking since the seat was created in 2009.

--- L000273: Teresa Leger Fernandez (Democrat, NM, house) ---

As Commissioner of Las Acequias de Chupadero, she defeated a developer's attempt to drain the valley's water, a legal victory that would later become a campaign origin story [2].

Elected to represent New Mexico's 3rd Congressional District in 2020 with endorsements from Elizabeth Warren and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez, Leger Fernandez arrived in Washington as an unapologetic progressive [3]. Her Heritage Action lifetime score of 6 percent confirms that she votes roughly as often with the Heritage Foundation as most people visit the dentist [4]. Her legislative focus has centered on tribal water rights and rural infrastructure. She advanced landmark water-rights settlement legislation for 11 New Mexico tribes, securing $850 million for the Pueblos of Laguna and Acoma and $490 million for the Pueblos of Jemez and Zia [5]. She co-led with Heinrich and Lujan on the Taos Pueblo Water Rights Settlement [6]. Before Congress she had already helped secure nearly a billion dollars for schools, rural health clinics, broadband, and affordable housing across New Mexico as a public-interest lawyer [2].

On the 80% Bill planks, Leger Fernandez has a strong record. She was an original cosponsor of the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act and voted for the Freedom to Vote: John R. She has cosponsored legislation to secure key wins for tribes, water projects, and apprenticeships through appropriations [9].

In a caucus full of representatives who talk about communities they have never lived in, Leger Fernandez has the rare credibility of someone who grew up irrigating fields with 400-year-old water channels. Whether that credibility translates into advancing all 20 planks of the 80% Bill is the question her constituents should be asking.

--- L000397: Zoe Lofgren (Democrat, CA, house) ---
Zoe Lofgren has represented Silicon Valley in Congress since 1995, making her one of the longest-serving members of the House at fifteen terms and counting. A former immigration lawyer and Santa Clara County supervisor, she sits on the Judiciary Committee and serves as Ranking Member of the Science, Space, and Technology Committee. She chaired the House Administration Committee during the 116th and 117th Congresses, giving her direct oversight of election law and ethics rules. Her district, California's 18th, is home to the headquarters of Google, Apple, Cisco, and dozens of other tech giants -- a geographic fact that, as we shall see, has proven remarkably convenient for her fundraising operation [1][2].

On paper, Lofgren checks many of the 80% Bill's boxes. She championed H.R. 1, the For the People Act, which tackled dark money disclosure, gerrymandering through independent redistricting commissions, and campaign finance reform aimed at counteracting Citizens United [3]. She has publicly condemned the Citizens United decision as having "gutted our nation's campaign finance laws, paving the way for special interests, megadonors, and corporations to funnel unlimited and unrestricted money into our elections" [4]. She co-introduced the Redistricting Reform Act to end partisan gerrymandering [5], vowed to protect Social Security from cuts [6], and voted for the Build Back Better Act, which included paid family leave and Medicare drug negotiation provisions. She was even tasked by Speaker Pelosi with drafting the congressional stock trading ban, producing the Combatting Financial Conflicts of Interest in Government Act and signaling it would extend to Supreme Court justices [7].

Here is where the story curdles. The stock trading ban Lofgren drafted was widely criticized as a watered-down shell designed to look like reform while protecting the status quo. She drafted it in "relative isolation," excluding the bipartisan members who had most vigorously pushed for a ban, and the bill conveniently never received a floor vote before the 2022 midterms [8]. Meanwhile, Lofgren's own financial disclosures reveal she holds stock in Apple, Facebook, Intel, Microsoft, Cisco, and Google -- the very companies her committees oversee [9]. On Big Tech antitrust, she has been the Democratic Party's most prominent holdout, voting against bipartisan antitrust bills in the Judiciary Committee and blocking efforts within the Progressive Caucus to advance them. A poll of her own constituents found overwhelming support for tech regulation, yet Lofgren called the bills "poorly-drafted" and "extreme" [10][11]. On term limits, her three-decade tenure speaks louder than any position paper she has declined to write.

Follow the money and the family tree. Campaign contributions from the tech industry have exceeded one million dollars over her career, with Google and its parent Alphabet topping $400,000, Cisco over $300,000, and additional six-figure sums from Microsoft, Oracle, Adobe, Intuit, and Kleiner Perkins [12]. Her daughter, Sheila Zoe Lofgren Collins, is a top attorney at Google -- a detail Lofgren insists has "no influence" on her legislative decisions, even as she serves on the committee that supervises the FTC's antitrust enforcement against Google [13]. The Revolving Door Project formally flagged this as a conflict of interest in a public letter [14].

Lofgren is the paradox that makes voters cynical: a representative who speaks the language of reform fluently, co-sponsors the right bills, and delivers impassioned floor speeches about dark money and corruption -- then quietly ensures the most consequential legislation never quite makes it across the finish line, all while cashing checks from the industry she is supposed to regulate. The 80% Bill asks whether Congress will ban stock trading, rein in Big Tech, and end dark money. Lofgren has made a career of promising all three while delivering on none that would cost her donors a dime [15].

Thirty-one years in Congress, and the only thing Frank Lucas has truly conserved is his own seat.

--- L000557: John B. Larson (Democrat, CT, house) ---
John Larson has represented Connecticut's 1st district since 1999, a quarter-century tenure that makes him one of the longest-serving members of the House and a living argument for the term limits he has never endorsed [1][2]. Before Congress, he was a high school history teacher, a state senator who rose to president pro tempore, and — in a detail that will become relevant — the co-owner of an insurance agency in East Hartford [3]. He sits on the mighty House Ways and Means Committee, where he serves as ranking member of the Social Security Subcommittee, and he has made Social Security expansion his legislative north star [4].

Larson's alignment with the 80% Bill is genuinely impressive on the populist-left flank. His Social Security 2100 Act, which would expand benefits, improve the COLA formula for seniors, and impose the payroll tax on earnings above $400,000, has attracted nearly 200 cosponsors and the endorsement of over 100 advocacy groups [5][6]. He authored Connecticut's original Family and Medical Leave Act in 1987 — the first in the nation — and has co-sponsored the federal FAMILY Act for twelve weeks of paid leave [7]. He voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, championing its Medicare drug price negotiation provisions and celebrating when Connecticut's 120,000-plus Medicare beneficiaries stood to benefit [8]. He voted against the Iraq War, has supported war powers reform, and joined the bipartisan effort to release the Epstein files, voting for full disclosure [9][10].

On campaign finance, Larson talks a convincing game. He co-sponsored the constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United, reintroduced the DISCLOSE Act to drag dark money into the sunlight, and introduced the Redistricting Reform Act to ban partisan gerrymandering through independent commissions [11][12]. He earned an "A" rating from End Citizens United for his election reform efforts [11]. He chaired the Task Force on Election Reform and pushed for a small-dollar donation public financing system modeled on Connecticut's own Citizens Election Program, which passed the House twice as part of the For the People Act [12].

But Larson represents Hartford — the self-proclaimed "Insurance Capital of the World," where 56,000 people work in the industry and insurance contributes $17.6 billion to the state economy [13]. His top career donors are individuals and PACs tied to the insurance industry, which gave him $260,774 in a single cycle alone [14]. He co-owned an insurance agency before entering politics. He sits on Ways and Means, the committee with the most direct power over health care tax policy, insurance regulation, and the very Social Security system the insurance industry would love to privatize. To his credit, Larson has fought privatization tooth and nail. But the insurance money keeps flowing, and one has to wonder whether his populist crusade on Social Security conveniently shields him from harder questions about the industry that bankrolls his campaigns and dominates his district's economy.

Twenty-six years in Congress, no vote for term limits, an insurance agency on his resume, and an insurance industry that writes the checks — John Larson is a genuine Social Security champion who nonetheless embodies the permanent-incumbent, industry-adjacent model of governance that the 80% Bill was designed to disrupt. His legislative record is largely admirable; his funding sources are a reminder that even the good ones play the game.

--- L000562: Stephen F. Lynch (Democrat, MA, house) ---
Stephen Lynch's biography reads like the origin story of a Democratic congressman reverse-engineered for maximum authenticity: raised in the Old Colony Housing Project in South Boston, son of an ironworker father and a post office worker mother, he joined Ironworkers Local 7 out of high school and spent eighteen years walking steel beams before becoming the youngest president in the union's history [1]. He then earned degrees from Wentworth Institute, Boston College Law School, and a master's from Harvard's Kennedy School, assembling credentials the way an ironworker bolts together a skyscraper — one piece at a time, with visible effort. It is the kind of resume that makes political consultants weep with envy, and Lynch has been cashing in on it electorally since winning a special election to Congress in 2001 [2].

Lynch's tenure on the House Financial Services Committee has been defined by a peculiar brand of populist contrarianism. He voted against the 2008 bank bailout — a position that was either principled opposition to rewarding Wall Street recklessness or a canny bet that Southie voters would appreciate the gesture, depending on your level of cynicism [3]. He helped craft the Dodd-Frank Act and has more recently taken up the cause of cryptocurrency regulation as Ranking Member of the Digital Assets, Financial Technology, and Artificial Intelligence Subcommittee, where he has warned with considerable vigor that Americans lost over $5.6 billion to crypto scams in 2023 alone [4]. He introduced the ECASH Act to create a government-backed digital dollar and co-sponsored legislation to prevent members of Congress and their families from trading digital assets — a bill whose very necessity tells you everything you need to know about the institution.

The most interesting chapter of Lynch's career, however, is the one he would probably prefer you forget. He was the only New England congressman to vote against the Affordable Care Act in 2010, arguing that the bill's lack of a public option amounted to a "surrender" to insurance companies [5]. This was technically a left-wing critique, though it arrived at exactly the same destination as the right-wing one: no vote. He was also, for much of his career, openly anti-abortion, declaring in 2001 that he was "pro-life and proud of it," voting to ban so-called partial-birth abortion, and supporting the Stupak amendment restricting abortion coverage in the ACA [6]. He has since reversed course with the enthusiasm of a man discovering that his district's demographics have changed, earning a 100 percent NARAL rating since 2016 and writing an op-ed distancing himself from the pro-life movement [7].

On the 80% Bill's priorities, Lynch is a reliable if occasionally surprising vote. His working-class bona fides make him a natural ally on issues like Medicare drug price negotiation, junk fee bans, and trade enforcement — positions that align with the union halls where his political instincts were forged. His Financial Services perch gives him genuine expertise on corporate accountability and stock trading bans for members of Congress. But his history of strategic evolution on social issues suggests a politician whose convictions, while sincerely held at any given moment, have a shelf life calibrated to the nearest election cycle [8]. In a delegation full of progressive true believers, Lynch remains the Democrat who reminds you that the party's coalition was once considerably more complicated than a Twitter thread.
