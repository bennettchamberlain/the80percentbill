--- E000300: Gabe Evans (Republican, CO, house) ---
Gabe Evans is the freshman Republican representing Colorado's 8th Congressional District, a swing seat he flipped by fewer than 2,500 votes in 2024 after defeating incumbent Democrat Yadira Caraveo [1]. A former Army National Guard officer and police officer who still wears his old patrol boots on the House floor, Evans was appointed to the House Energy and Commerce Committee -- the first incoming freshman to land that seat in fourteen years [2][3]. National analysts have since ranked him the most vulnerable House incumbent in the country heading into 2026, which may explain why his voting record reads like a man trying to please everyone while accountable to very specific someones [4].

Evans checks a few of the 80% Bill's boxes with visible enthusiasm. He signed the U.S. Term Limits pledge, promising to support a constitutional amendment capping congressional tenure -- a popular stance for a freshman who has not yet had time to develop a taste for permanence [5]. He voted for the Epstein Files Transparency Act, citing his law enforcement background investigating sex offenders and human trafficking, making him one of the bipartisan majority that forced the DOJ to release unclassified Epstein records [6]. He has voiced support for protecting Social Security and has positioned himself as a champion of affordable housing, helping pass the Housing for the 21st Century Act [7][8]. On paper, the populist notes are pitch-perfect.

The music changes when money enters the room. Evans sits on Energy and Commerce while representing a district that includes Weld County, which produces 82% of Colorado's crude oil and 56% of its natural gas [9]. He has cosponsored legislation to cut taxes for large oil and gas companies, toured energy facilities operated by firms like Bayswater, and championed what he calls "all of the above" energy policy -- a phrase that in practice tends to mean "mostly oil and gas, plus a photo op at a solar panel" [10][11]. His most consequential vote came on the One Big Beautiful Bill, which he supported with such fervor that he claimed "100% credit" for its passage after it cleared the House by a single vote, 215-214 [12]. That bill slashed Medicaid by roughly $1 trillion over ten years -- the largest cut in the program's history -- threatening coverage for nearly 19,000 of his own constituents in a state where 30% of residents rely on Medicaid [13][14]. When confronted, Evans repeatedly claimed the bill contained no Medicaid cuts, a statement a Medicaid financing expert called "misleading and incorrect" [15].

The throughline is familiar: populist rhetoric on term limits and transparency, party-line loyalty where the donor class demands it. Evans pledged to audit aid to Ukraine but has shown no interest in auditing the Pentagon's $886 billion budget [3]. He talks about protecting families from rising costs while voting for a bill that hands tax breaks to the wealthy and strips healthcare from the working class. His district was designed to be competitive, and Evans governs like a man who knows the oil and gas industry will fund his next campaign whether or not his neighbors can afford to see a doctor.

--- E000301: Sarah Elfreth (Democrat, MD, house) ---
Sarah Elfreth's path to Congress is a case study in how modern Democratic politics actually works, as opposed to how anyone wishes it did. A former lobbyist for the National Aquarium and Johns Hopkins University, she served in the Maryland State Senate representing Annapolis before winning the open seat in Maryland's 3rd Congressional District in 2024 [1]. Her victory was made possible in no small part by more than $4.2 million in outside spending from AIPAC's United Democracy Project super PAC -- one of the largest single-race investments the organization made that cycle [2]. For a representative whose district includes Baltimore and its surrounding communities, the sheer volume of out-of-state money flooding in prompted her primary opponents to cry foul over what they called dark money corrupting the race [3]. The irony of benefiting from exactly the kind of spending the 80% Bill's articles on dark money and corporate PACs seek to eliminate has not been lost on observers.

To her credit, Elfreth has not been a rubber stamp for her biggest backers. Days into her first term, she broke with AIPAC by voting against legislation to sanction the International Criminal Court -- a move that suggested she was willing to think independently even when it risked annoying the people who spent millions getting her elected [2]. She has also staked out positions on the environment that are genuinely substantive: as Vice Ranking Member of the House Natural Resources Committee, she voted against the PERMIT Act over its threats to Chesapeake Bay protections, introduced the Maryland Whole Watershed Program Federal Partnership Act alongside Senator Alsobrooks, and even championed legislation to turn invasive blue catfish into pet food [4][5]. These are not vanity bills; the Chesapeake Bay is the largest estuary in the United States and Elfreth has made its restoration a legislative identity.

Elfreth's relationship with the 80% Bill's twenty articles is, to be diplomatic, complicated. She was the only candidate in her 2024 primary who expressed openness to accepting corporate PAC money, telling voters she would take donations from corporations within her district [6]. Her donor rolls included Republican megadonors, a former AIPAC president, and the son of the New England Patriots' owner -- a coalition that suggests her fundraising strategy was less "small-dollar grassroots" and more "anyone with a checkbook and an opinion on the Middle East" [3][6]. On campaign finance reform, she introduced H.R. 7802 in March 2026 to strengthen disclosure requirements for corporations, Super PACs, and other entities -- a step toward transparency, even if it stops well short of the 80% Bill's call to ban corporate PACs outright [7].

Her attendance record is flawless -- zero missed votes out of 445 roll calls -- and her legislative focus on armed forces, natural resources, and cybersecurity education reflects both her committee assignments and her district's proximity to the Naval Academy and Fort Meade [1][8]. On the broader populist agenda items like banning stock trading, taxing the ultra-wealthy, term limits, and the Epstein files, Elfreth has been largely silent, neither championing nor opposing them. She has criticized U.S. military strikes against Iran as lacking legal justification, which aligns with the "End Forever Wars" article, but she has not formally endorsed the broader anti-interventionist framework [9].

The fundamental tension in Elfreth's first term is between the politician she campaigns as -- an environmentalist, a public policy wonk with a Johns Hopkins master's degree, a champion of Maryland's working waterfront -- and the financial machinery that put her in office. Whether she uses her seat to push for the kind of structural reforms that 80 percent of Americans say they want, or whether she remains cautiously positioned within the boundaries her donor base finds comfortable, will define whether her career becomes a story about conviction or convenience.

--- F000110: Cleo Fields (Democrat, LA, house) ---

His biography reads like a testament to persistence -- or, depending on your perspective, to the gravitational pull of elected office on certain personalities.

On the 80% bills, Fields presents the curious case of a Democrat who sits on the very committee that oversees securities markets and capital flows, yet has not emerged as a vocal champion of banning congressional stock trading. This is particularly awkward given that in September 2025, Fields purchased between $80,000 and $200,000 worth of Oracle stock in three separate trades on September 17, 18, and 23 -- days before Oracle's role in the TikTok deal was publicly announced on September 22 [2]. Fields does sit on the Financial Services Subcommittee on Capital Markets and the Subcommittee on Oversight and Investigations, positions that give him direct jurisdiction over the very transparency and disclosure rules that the DISCLOSE Act and dark money bans would strengthen [1]. The Louisiana Ethics Board also fined Fields for failing to file a campaign finance report from a previous state-level account, a minor infraction that nonetheless suggests a casual relationship with disclosure requirements [5].

The bottom line on Cleo Fields is that he is a veteran politician who returned to Congress after nearly three decades away and promptly demonstrated why Americans want to ban stock trading by members of Congress. He sits on the committee best positioned to do something about financial transparency and congressional ethics, and his most notable financial headline so far involves suspiciously well-timed Oracle purchases. The 80% bills need champions, not spectators with brokerage accounts.

--- F000446: Randy Feenstra (Republican, IA, house) ---
Randy Feenstra represents Iowa's sprawling 4th Congressional District, a deeply conservative stretch of farmland and small towns in the state's northwest corner that he won in 2020 after unseating Steve King in a Republican primary that was less about ideology than about King's tendency to say the quiet parts at maximum volume [1]. Before Congress, Feenstra sold candy — literally, as a sales manager for the Foreign Candy Company, best known for importing Warheads — then served as a city administrator, county treasurer, and Iowa state senator [2]. He sits on both the House Ways and Means Committee and the Agriculture Committee, a dual assignment he notes with pride as the only member holding both, and in October 2025 he announced he would not seek reelection, instead launching a campaign for Iowa governor [3].

Feenstra's signature issue is protecting American farmland from foreign ownership, and on this plank of the 80% Bill he has been genuinely active. He authored the FARMLAND Act to give the Committee on Foreign Investment authority to review large foreign purchases of agricultural land, and he has repeatedly warned about Chinese entities buying acreage near sensitive military sites [4]. He also signed the U.S. Term Limits pledge, putting him ahead of many colleagues on an issue that polls north of 80 percent [5]. But his alignment with broadly popular policy largely stops at the borders of his district's brand. Feenstra voted against the Inflation Reduction Act, which included the Medicare drug price negotiation provisions that allow the government to bargain down prescription costs for seniors [6]. He has taken no public position supporting a ban on congressional stock trading, a lifetime lobbying ban, paid family leave, police body cameras, the DISCLOSE Act, or legislation to overturn Citizens United. On auditing the Pentagon, ending forever wars by repealing the AUMFs, and banning junk fees, Feenstra has been similarly silent.

The farmland hypocrisy is where Feenstra's narrative curdles from garden-variety Republican orthodoxy into something richer. Federal Election Commission records show that between 2020 and 2024, PACs controlled by Syngenta and Smithfield Foods — both owned by Chinese parent companies — wired over $20,000 to Feenstra's congressional campaign [7]. This is the same congressman who sponsored an amendment barring China from buying farmland suitable for ethanol and biodiesel production, then accepted a $5,000 check from Syngenta's PAC [8]. Smithfield Foods, owned by China's WH Group, controls tens of thousands of acres of U.S. farmland, and Boone County in Feenstra's own district contains over 200 acres of Chinese-owned agricultural land [7]. His own party attacked him for it during the 2026 gubernatorial primary, a rare moment when Republican voters got to see the receipts.

Feenstra's broader fundraising picture reinforces the pattern. In the first quarter of 2024, his campaign raised $810,000, but less than three percent — just $22,482 — came from donors in Iowa's 4th District [9]. The rest flowed from PACs and mega-donors in Washington and Virginia, with agribusiness, renewable energy interests, and poultry and egg producers ranking among his top industry contributors [10]. For a congressman who built his brand on representing Iowa farmers, the donor geography suggests his real constituency wears suits, not overalls.

Feenstra is the kind of Republican who understands that populism sells in rural Iowa but practices it selectively enough to keep the PAC money flowing. He will protect farmland from China in a press release and take Chinese-owned company money in a filing. He will sign a term limits pledge while voting against nearly every other reform that would limit the power of entrenched interests. Now running for governor, Feenstra carries with him a congressional record that is less a profile in courage than a masterclass in knowing which populist buttons to press and which donor relationships to protect.

The bottom line on Bill Foster is that he is a genuinely smart man who has chosen to be selectively brave. The bottom line on Deb Fischer is a senator who arrived in Washington promising reform and stayed long enough to become the thing she promised to reform. Her work on paid family leave and farmland protection is genuine and significant -- but on the majority of the 80% Bill's provisions, Fischer votes with her donors, not her constituents.
