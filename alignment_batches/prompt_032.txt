--- M001137: Gregory W. Meeks (Democrat, NY, house) ---
Gregory Meeks has represented southeastern Queens since 1998, making him one of the longest-serving members of the New York delegation. He faced a House Ethics Committee investigation in 2010 over a $40,000 loan from a Queens businessman that he failed to disclose for years. The Ethics Committee ultimately cleared him, but the episode illustrated a comfort level with financial opacity that doesn't exactly scream "transparency champion."

Meeks has been a reliable vote for defense spending and foreign arms sales, serving as a key Democratic facilitator of weapons deals to Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and other Gulf states. Term limits? Meeks is currently in his 14th term. That alone tells you where he stands. He has shown no interest in the DISCLOSE Act, dark money reform, or Pentagon audits. His career is a case study in how a safe-seat Democrat can spend decades in Congress accumulating seniority, committee power, and donor relationships while the district's residents wonder why Queens still has the same problems it had in 1998.

--- M001143: Betty McCollum (Democrat, MN, house) ---
Betty McCollum's path to Congress ran through a high school social studies classroom, a Sears retail floor, and the North St. Paul City Council — a resume that reads less like a political origin story than a census of actual American jobs [1]. Elected to the U.S. House in 2000 as only the second Minnesota woman to serve in Congress since statehood, McCollum brought eight years of experience in the Minnesota state legislature and a DFL sensibility that treats government spending not as an abstraction but as a series of specific decisions about who gets helped and who gets ignored [2]. She has represented the 4th District — anchoring St. Paul and its eastern suburbs — for over two decades, compiling a record that is quietly consequential in ways that rarely generate cable news segments.

McCollum's most significant power base is the House Appropriations Committee, where she serves as the top Democrat on the Defense Subcommittee. This position gives her direct oversight of the largest discretionary spending bill in the federal budget, and she has used it to ask questions that apparently embarrass the Pentagon: Why can't an institution spending over $800 billion a year pass a basic financial audit? Why does Congress keep marking up defense bills from incomplete budget submissions? [3] She has pushed for detailed budget documentation as the only meaningful tool for tracking where defense dollars actually go, operating under the apparently radical assumption that accountability and national security are not mutually exclusive [4].

Beyond the appropriations ledger, McCollum has carved out a distinctive niche on human rights issues that most of her colleagues prefer to avoid. She is the author of the Defending the Human Rights of Palestinian Children and Families Living Under Israeli Military Occupation Act, a bill that would prohibit U.S. taxpayer dollars from being used for the military detention of children — legislation that earned her both international recognition and predictable Washington blowback [5]. She has applied the same principle domestically as Co-Chair Emeritus of the Congressional Native American Caucus, fighting for Indian Health Service funding, tribal sovereignty, and the Indian Programs Advance Appropriations Act to prevent funding lapses that leave Native communities without hospitals and schools [6].

On the bread-and-butter issues that define the 80% Bill, McCollum's record is consistently aligned. She voted for the Affordable Care Act and has fought every subsequent attempt to repeal it, supported paid parental leave for federal employees, and backed the DISCLOSE Act to force transparency on dark money in elections [7]. Her endorsement of campaign finance reform carries a particular irony: her own OpenSecrets profile shows contributions from defense contractors and corporate PACs, a tension she shares with virtually every incumbent who supports reforming the system they operate within [8].

McCollum's legislative style lacks the performative flair that social media rewards, which is perhaps why a representative who has spent a quarter-century quietly shaping defense budgets, defending children's rights on two continents, and pushing for government transparency remains less well-known than colleagues who have accomplished considerably less. Her alignment with the 80% Bill's planks — from Pentagon accountability to healthcare access to election reform — is the product of a career spent treating governance as work rather than spectacle.

--- M001153: Lisa Murkowski (Republican, AK, senate) ---
Lisa Murkowski has served as Alaska's senior senator since 2002, when her father Frank Murkowski — then governor — appointed her to fill his own vacated Senate seat[1], a move that even in Alaska raised eyebrows. She has since won reelection four times, including a historic 2010 write-in campaign after losing her own party's primary to a Tea Party challenger[2]. Murkowski has built a brand as the Senate's most conspicuously independent Republican, the one reporters call first when they need a quote about bipartisan courage. She was one of seven Republicans who voted to convict Donald Trump in his second impeachment trial[3], and she voted to confirm Ketanji Brown Jackson to the Supreme Court[4]. Her independence is real, but it has limits that tend to reveal themselves at illuminating moments.

On the 80% Bill's core issues, Murkowski's record is a study in selective courage. She voted for the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law[5] and was one of the few Republicans to support the CHIPS Act[6], both of which align with broad public consensus on investing in American infrastructure and manufacturing. But she voted against the Inflation Reduction Act[7] — the bill that gave Medicare the power to negotiate drug prices for the first time — despite the policy polling above 80 percent nationally. Her stated concern was the bill's spending levels, though it's worth noting that pharmaceutical and health sector PACs are among her most reliable donors[8]. On the Epstein files, Murkowski initially called for transparency, then voted to block the release[9], then voted for it when the Senate finally passed the measure unanimously[10] — the legislative equivalent of letting someone else test the water temperature before jumping in.

The money side of the ledger tells a consistent story. Murkowski's top industry donors include oil and gas, mining, and electric utilities[8] — sectors with direct stakes in Alaska's resource economy and in the permitting decisions that cross her desk on the Energy and Natural Resources Committee, where she serves as a senior member[11]. She has supported the Willow Project[12], expanded Arctic drilling, and fought EPA regulations on mining near Bristol Bay[13], all positions popular with her donors and, to be fair, with many Alaskans. But when the same donors' interests collide with the 80% Bill's clean energy provisions or its anti-corruption measures, the independence brand gets quiet. She notably agonized publicly over the "One Big Beautiful Bill" reconciliation package before eventually voting yes[14] — a pattern her critics have observed before, where the hand-wringing is the performance and the vote is the product.

Murkowski's brand is built on being the Republican who thinks things through. On some issues — infrastructure, judicial independence — the evidence supports that image. On others — drug pricing, dark money disclosure, fossil fuel regulation — her thoughtfulness seems to arrive at the same destination as her donors' preferences with suspicious regularity. The DISCLOSE Act, which would require transparency in campaign spending, has never found its way onto her list of bipartisan priorities. For the senator who won her seat through the most dramatic act of political independence in modern Senate history, the gap between the brand and the voting record is itself the most revealing thing about her.

She sits on the powerful Ways and Means Committee, the body that controls taxation, Social Security, and Medicare, which makes her one of the few members whose committee assignment actually aligns with her stated values. On the 80% Bill scorecard, Moore looks like a natural ally, and on many counts she delivers.

She voted against the Fiscal Responsibility Act, rejecting Republican austerity demands. On paper, this is a progressive warrior.

But the Ways and Means seat comes with temptations. Moore's leadership PAC, the charmingly named "Giving Willingly Empowering Nationally" (GWEN PAC), raised over $400,000 in the 2021-2022 cycle. The Foundation for Accountability and Civic Trust filed a complaint alleging the PAC operated as a "scam PAC," spending only 5% of its 2024 cycle funds on actual political contributions. Where did the rest go? That's a question Moore hasn't satisfactorily answered, and it's the kind of opacity that the DISCLOSE Act and dark money bans in the 80% Bill are designed to eliminate.

Moore deserves credit for consistency on the populist planks: paid family leave, funding Social Security, protecting Medicare. The question for Moore isn't whether she believes in the 80% Bill's vision. It's whether two decades of incumbency, a leadership PAC with questionable spending patterns, and the gravitational pull of the Ways and Means donor ecosystem have created a gap between her rhetoric and her urgency. Milwaukee deserves the Gwen Moore who walked onto campus as a single mother with something to prove — not the one who's been in Washington long enough to confuse access with accountability.

--- M001169: Christopher Murphy (Democrat, CT, senate) ---
Chris Murphy is the junior senator from Connecticut, first elected in 2012 and returned to a third term in 2024. A Democrat who sits on the Appropriations, Foreign Relations, and Health, Education, Labor and Pensions committees, Murphy is perhaps best known nationally for his impassioned advocacy on gun control following the Sandy Hook Elementary School massacre in his home state [1]. He holds a party leadership role as Senate Democratic Conference Deputy Secretary, and has positioned himself as a vocal critic of unchecked executive war powers and corporate influence in politics [2]. On paper, he looks like a natural champion for the 80% Bill.

And in many respects, he is. Murphy has been a leading voice for Medicare drug price negotiation, calling it "the number one way to lower the cost of prescription drugs" and championing the Inflation Reduction Act's historic provisions allowing Medicare to negotiate directly with pharmaceutical companies [3]. He co-sponsored the DISCLOSE Act to unmask dark money donors and has called for a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United [4]. He introduced the American Homeownership Act to curb Wall Street's buying spree of single-family homes [5]. On war powers, he co-authored the National Security Powers Act with Senators Sanders and Lee to reassert Congressional authority over military action, arms sales, and emergency declarations, and has fought to repeal outdated AUMFs that presidents of both parties have stretched like constitutional silly putty [6]. He supports the FAMILY Act for paid family leave, voted against the 2017 Republican tax bill he called "a giveaway to the rich and powerful," and has publicly demanded full release of the Epstein files, saying "there are Democrats in those files, there are Republicans in those files" and "so what?" -- release them all [7][8][9].

The contradictions, however, live in the fine print of Connecticut's economy. Murphy sits on the Appropriations Committee -- the first Connecticut senator to do so since 1987 -- and he has used that perch to secure increased funding for Sikorsky Black Hawks, Electric Boat Virginia-class submarines, and Pratt & Whitney F-135 engines for F-35 Joint Strike Fighters [10]. He has applauded billion-dollar Raytheon contracts awarded to Connecticut facilities [11]. This is the same senator who laments the "fundamentally broken and worrying process" of passing an $847 billion defense budget, then votes for the defense bill because it "supports Connecticut manufacturers" [12]. Auditing the Pentagon sounds wonderful until the audit might question whether Connecticut really needs another submarine production line.

Follow the money and the tension sharpens. Murphy's top industry contributors include Securities and Investment firms, and he has received contributions from lobbyists throughout his career [13][14]. Connecticut is home to some of the largest insurance and financial services companies in America -- Hartford is literally called "the Insurance Capital of the World" -- and those industries have a permanent seat at Murphy's fundraising table. While he champions closing tax loopholes for the ultra-wealthy in press releases, the carried interest loophole that enriches his Wall Street donors remains conspicuously intact, and Murphy has not co-sponsored the specific legislation to close it [15]. He talks about banning congressional stock trading but has not been a lead sponsor on any of the major bills to do so [16].

The bottom line is that Chris Murphy is genuinely aligned with most of the 80% Bill -- war powers reform, drug pricing, dark money disclosure, housing reform, paid leave, and Epstein transparency. But Connecticut's defense-industrial economy and financial sector donor base create blind spots precisely where the 80% Bill demands the sharpest vision: Pentagon accountability, tax loophole closures, and the cozy relationship between legislators and the industries they oversee. Murphy is proof that even the most progressive-sounding senators can be tethered to the status quo by the very economy they represent.

--- M001176: Jeff Merkley (Democrat, OR, senate) ---
Jeff Merkley is what happens when a Boy Scout gets elected to the Senate and never quite breaks character. The junior senator from Oregon has spent seventeen years building a legislative record so earnestly progressive that it almost feels like performance art — except he keeps actually doing the things he says he'll do, which in Washington qualifies as genuinely radical behavior.

Start with the money. Merkley stopped taking corporate PAC contributions years ago, making him the first member of Oregon's congressional delegation to do so. He doesn't trade individual stocks. His campaign runs on grassroots small-dollar donations. In a body where the median senator treats fundraising calls like a second job, Merkley operates like someone who read the corruption chapter of a civics textbook and took it personally. His estimated net worth of $4.5 to $19.9 million comes largely from rental properties, not stock portfolios — making him wealthy, yes, but at least not wealthy in the way that creates the specific conflicts of interest he spends his days legislating against.

He co-authored the bipartisan ETHICS Act with Josh Hawley to ban congressional stock trading — a bill that actually advanced through committee. He co-introduced the DISCLOSE Act to drag dark money into daylight, requiring any organization spending more than $10,000 on elections to reveal donors. He co-sponsored the Audit the Pentagon Act and was one of just eleven senators to vote against an $858 billion military budget in 2022. He backed Medicare drug price negotiation before it was cool, introducing legislation with Ron Wyden to let Medicare negotiate Part D prices. He champions paid family leave, filibuster reform, and voting rights with the consistency of a metronome.

In October 2025, Merkley held the Senate floor for 22 hours and 37 minutes — the third-longest speech in Senate history — warning about authoritarian overreach during a government shutdown. In March 2026, he introduced the End Prediction Market Corruption Act to ban federal officials from gambling on government actions, prompted by a trader who mysteriously made $400,000 predicting a foreign leader's removal before it was announced.

The Merkley paradox is not hypocrisy — it's futility. He is the senator who does everything right and still can't get most of it passed. He reforms the filibuster proposal, gets 46 votes, falls short. He writes stock trading bans that advance through committee and stall on the floor. He gives 22-hour speeches that trend on social media and change exactly zero votes. The system he fights is designed to absorb exactly this kind of principled opposition and convert it into content rather than law. Merkley is proof that in the modern Senate, being right is necessary but nowhere near sufficient — and that the institution's dysfunction is a feature, not a bug, for the interests that benefit from inaction.

--- M001177: Tom McClintock (Republican, CA, house) ---

He has benefited from the same corporate PAC ecosystem and dark money infrastructure that the 80% Bill seeks to dismantle, while offering no support for the DISCLOSE Act, overturning Citizens United, or banning corporate PACs. McClintock is a study in selective principle. He will cross the aisle to audit the Pentagon and decry gerrymandering, positions that cost him nothing with his base, but when it comes to taxing the ultra-wealthy, funding Social Security, negotiating drug prices, or banning the stock trades that have personally enriched him, the fiscal conservative becomes a fiscal convenientist. The 80% Bill asks whether popular reforms transcend ideology. McClintock's answer is: only the ones that do not threaten my portfolio or my donors.
