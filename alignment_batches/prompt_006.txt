--- B001321: Tom Barrett (Republican, MI, house) ---
Tom Barrett brings an impressive resume to Michigan's 7th Congressional District: twenty-two years in the U.S. Army, over a thousand hours piloting helicopters across Iraq, Kuwait, Guantanamo Bay, and the Korean DMZ, and stints in both the Michigan House and Senate before arriving in Washington in January 2025 [1]. It is the kind of biography that lends itself to campaign ads featuring American flags and stirring music. What it does not appear to lend itself to, unfortunately, is voting in the interest of the mid-Michigan families whose doors he knocked on to get the job.

Barrett wasted little time establishing his priorities. In May 2025, he cast the deciding-margin vote for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, which passed the House 215 to 214 before being signed into law on July 4th [2]. The bill delivered permanent tax cuts weighted toward high earners while imposing the largest reductions to SNAP in American history — an estimated $285.7 billion over the next decade — and adding Medicaid work requirements that mandate 80 hours per month of work, volunteering, or study for able-bodied adults without children under 14 [3]. For the more than 20,000 residents in his district who rely on SNAP, and the thousands more whose healthcare premiums could more than double without extended ACA premium tax credits, this was not an abstraction [4].

The backlash arrived on schedule. By Halloween 2025, protesters gathered outside Barrett's Lansing office in costume, writing messages like "Don't let kids starve Tom!" and "help us afford to live!" in sidewalk chalk on the bricks outside. Barrett responded by calling the chalk "vandalism" and the protest "deeply disturbing" criminal behavior, requesting a law enforcement investigation [5]. The Michigan Advance editorial board was unmoved, noting that sidewalk chalk is neither a crime nor vandalism, but that gutting Medicaid and SNAP funding arguably is. On Veterans' Day, veterans from his own district held a call criticizing his votes for cutting funding to healthcare, food assistance, and veterans' affairs programs — a pointed rebuke for a representative who built his brand on military service [6].

Barrett has defended his record by pointing to the bill's tax relief provisions, including the elimination of taxes on tips and overtime pay, and the permanent extension of the 2017 tax cuts [7]. He has also avoided direct engagement with critics, skipping Medicaid town halls organized by Democratic challengers in August 2025 [8]. For a former helicopter pilot accustomed to hostile environments, the decision to dodge constituents armed with sidewalk chalk and pointed questions suggests that some combat zones are more daunting than others.

Michael Baumgartner wants you to know he served in Afghanistan and Iraq as a State Department officer. He'll remind you roughly every forty-five seconds. He's said the U.S. "can't be the world's policemen." Noble sentiment from a man who has shown zero interest in auditing the Pentagon that funded those very misadventures. The Department of Defense has now failed seven consecutive audits, unable to account for 63% of its $3.8 trillion in assets, and Baumgartner's response has been a deafening silence. It's almost as if criticizing wars is easier when you don't have to confront the defense contractors writing checks.

On the stock trading front, Baumgartner cosponsored H.R. 7008, the Stop Insider Trading Act, in January 2026. A freshman Republican backing a bipartisan crowd-pleaser -- revolutionary. But term limits? Lobbying bans? Corporate PAC reform? The man who spent six years as Spokane County Treasurer counting other people's money has been conspicuously quiet on counting Congress's conflicts of interest.

Baumgartner's "America First" foreign policy brand conveniently aligns with avoiding tough votes on dark money, the DISCLOSE Act, or taxing the ultra-wealthy. He wants to "overhaul foreign aid to serve American interests" while never quite specifying whose American interests he means. His committees give him ample opportunity to address education costs, workforce exploitation, and judicial reform, yet his signature legislative achievement so far is... the Restore College Sports Act, a bill to dissolve the NCAA. The people of Spokane surely sleep better knowing their congressman is taking on the real threat to democracy: college football's governing body.

For a self-described "smarter foreign policy" advocate, Baumgartner has a curious blind spot for the domestic policies that actually enjoy 80% public support. Ban junk fees? Fund Social Security? Protect farmland in his own agricultural district? He'd apparently rather talk about his time in Helmand Province.

--- B001323: Nicholas J. Begich III (Republican, AK, house) ---
Nicholas Begich III, Republican of Alaska's at-large congressional district, arrived in Congress in January 2025 carrying a name that once defined Democratic politics in Alaska — his grandfather Nick Begich Sr. served as a Democrat before vanishing in a 1972 plane crash[1], and his uncle Mark Begich served as Anchorage's mayor and a Democratic U.S. senator[2]. The younger Begich, however, has firmly planted his flag on the other side of the aisle, defeating 25-year incumbent Mary Pettola in the 2024 general election[3] with a campaign heavy on resource development, fiscal restraint, and skepticism of Washington overreach.

Begich has shown flashes of what might be called reform-curious Republicanism. He cosponsored the Stop Insider Trading Act[4], backing a bill to ban members of Congress from trading individual stocks while in office — a position that polls above 80 percent with Americans of both parties. He also signed onto a bipartisan push for greater transparency in congressional pay and benefits. On paper, this puts him on the right side of several 80% Bill articles dealing with government ethics and anti-corruption. Whether this translates into actual votes when leadership applies pressure remains the open question of his young career.

On energy and environment, Begich is an enthusiastic advocate of oil and gas development on Alaska's North Slope and has pushed to open more federal lands to drilling, putting him squarely at odds with the 80% Bill's clean energy and climate provisions. He has vocally opposed what he calls regulatory overreach by the EPA, a position that aligns neatly with his top industry donors: oil and gas companies and mining interests contributed heavily to his 2024 campaign, per OpenSecrets data[5]. His stance on Social Security — he once called the current system a "Ponzi scheme" in a campaign forum before walking the comment back[6] — sits uncomfortably next to his current assurances that he'll protect benefits for Alaska's seniors, a demographic he cannot afford to alienate in a state where the median age trends older than the national average.

The financial picture tells a familiar story. Begich's top donors include ConocoPhillips and Hilcorp Energy[7], and prior to his election, his campaign received significant support from the Congressional Leadership Fund, the super PAC aligned with House Republican leadership[8]. He's on the Natural Resources Committee[9], where he can directly influence the permitting and land-use decisions that his biggest donors care most about. For a freshman congressman who talks about draining the swamp, the pipeline from donor to committee assignment to policy position is remarkably well-plumbed. The question for Begich is whether the reform instincts — the stock trading ban, the transparency push — survive contact with the permanent incentive structure of Washington, or whether they become the garnish on a plate that's fundamentally serving someone else's meal.

--- B001324: Wesley Bell (Democrat, MO, house) ---
Wesley Bell is the kind of Democrat who makes progressives reach for the antacids. The son of a police officer, Bell worked his way through Lindenwood University waiting tables at Shoney's, became a public defender, served on the Ferguson City Council during the post-Michael Brown reforms, and in 2018 made history as the first Black prosecuting attorney in St. Louis County [1][2]. In 2024, he unseated Squad member Cori Bush in a Democratic primary that became the fourth most expensive House primary in American history -- not because of Bell's grassroots army, but because AIPAC's super PAC dumped $8.5 million into the race [3][4]. He now represents Missouri's 1st District and sits on the Armed Services and Oversight committees [5].

On the 80% Bill scorecard, Bell has some genuine bright spots. He co-sponsored the Epstein Files Transparency Act and then went further, introducing his own EPSTEIN Act (H.R. 7814) to establish an independent bipartisan commission for ongoing DOJ oversight of the files [6][7]. His career is a walking argument for police body cameras -- he personally helped implement them on the Ferguson City Council alongside officer training reforms and court overhauls, physically standing between protesters and police to de-escalate tensions [2][8]. On his official congressional site, he affirms that seniors "deserve their earned benefits" from Social Security and Medicare, positioning himself as a defender of both programs [9].

But the scorecard gets complicated fast. Bell voted for the FY2026 NDAA, which continued massive defense spending increases -- hardly the posture of someone prioritizing Pentagon audits or ending forever wars [10]. On the marquee campaign finance reforms -- banning corporate PACs, the DISCLOSE Act, banning dark money -- Bell's record is conspicuously thin for a Democrat, and the reason is not hard to find. On stock trading bans, taxing the ultra-wealthy, paid family leave, right to repair, banning junk fees, and closing tax loopholes, Bell has yet to emerge as a co-sponsor or vocal champion, preferring the safe middle lane that got him elected.

Follow the money and Bell's cautious centrism makes perfect sense. His $4.8 million campaign haul in the 2024 primary was dominated by AIPAC, which bundled nearly two-thirds of all his campaign funds [4][11]. Outside groups spent $18.2 million on the race, with Bell's backers outspending Bush's supporters roughly 4 to 1 [3]. His donor rolls included Republican crossover money -- over $65,000 from donors who also gave to Josh Hawley and Eric Schmitt -- plus contributions from GOP lobbyist Steven Tilley and hedge fund billionaire Daniel Loeb [12][13]. Democratic Majority for Israel PAC added half a million, and Reid Hoffman's Mainstream Democrats PAC kicked in another $1.5 million [3]. This is not a funding profile that produces crusaders against dark money or corporate PACs.

The bottom line on Wesley Bell is that he is a genuine criminal justice reformer who earned his credibility on the ground in Ferguson, but whose path to Congress was paved with exactly the kind of money the 80% Bill aims to eliminate. He will champion Epstein transparency and defend Social Security, but do not expect him to bite the AIPAC hand that fed his campaign or lead the charge on campaign finance reform. Bell is what happens when a well-intentioned moderate gets to Washington on a river of super PAC cash -- grateful, cautious, and unlikely to dam the flow.

--- B001327: Robert P. Bresnahan, Jr. (Republican, PA, house) ---
Rob Bresnahan ran for Congress in 2024 as a fresh-faced outsider — a construction CEO who would drain the swamp, ban congressional stock trading, and fight for working families in northeastern Pennsylvania. He defeated Democrat Matt Cartwright and arrived in Washington in January 2025. Within months, he became the single most prolific stock trader in Pennsylvania's entire congressional delegation.

The numbers are staggering. As of late 2025, Bresnahan has reported over 600 trades totaling more than $7 million. He introduced the TRUST Act in May to ban congressional stock trading and announced plans to establish a blind trust. Then he kept trading. When asked why he didn't simply tell his financial advisors to stop, Bresnahan delivered what may be the most honest sentence ever uttered by a member of Congress: "And then do what with it? Just leave it all in the accounts and just leave it there and lose money and go broke?" A multimillionaire construction magnate, terrified of going broke if he stops day-trading from a position of legislative power.

The Medicaid scandal is where comedy becomes tragedy. On May 15, 2025, Bresnahan dumped up to $130,000 in stock from Centene, Elevance Health, UnitedHealth, and CVS Health — companies that collectively manage roughly half of all Medicaid managed care in America. Seven days later, he voted for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, which slashed Medicaid by nearly $1 trillion. When the bill became law, Centene's stock cratered 40 percent. Over 21,000 of his own constituents are expected to lose Medicaid coverage. He sold first, voted second, and told everyone his financial advisor acted independently — a claim Politico later undermined with audio of Bresnahan admitting he meets with his advisor to discuss "what different positions are coming up."

To his credit, Bresnahan co-sponsored a constitutional amendment for congressional term limits and serves on the Transportation and Infrastructure Committee. His campaign donors, however, tell a familiar story: at least 20 billionaires have contributed to his campaigns, including casino mogul Steve Wynn, Blackstone CEO Stephen Schwarzman, and Leonard Blavatnik, a Ukrainian-born billionaire whose ties to Russian oligarchs have raised bipartisan eyebrows. After receiving his committee assignment, transportation PAC money tripled from $7,500 to $18,500 in a single quarter.

Bresnahan is a case study in the gap between populist branding and congressional reality. He campaigned on every reform voters wanted — stock trading bans, term limits, fighting for the little guy — and then arrived in Washington and did exactly what the system is designed to make you do. At his December town hall in Scranton, constituents confronted him about the trades. A former Republican congressman called for his resignation. He is, in the most literal sense, the reason these reforms are popular in the first place.

--- C000059: Ken Calvert (Republican, CA, house) ---
Ken Calvert, Republican of California's 41st district, has been warming his seat in the House since 1993 — over three decades of continuous service that would make even the most enthusiastic term limits advocate weep. The longest-serving GOP member in California's delegation, Calvert currently chairs the Defense Appropriations Subcommittee, a perch from which he controls the Pentagon's purse strings. He voted for the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, which showered benefits on the ultra-wealthy and corporations while leaving the carried interest loophole intact [5]. He voted for the One Big Beautiful Bill Act in 2025, which extended those tax cuts while slashing $700 billion from Medicaid — all while claiming with a straight face that it "makes no changes to Social Security, Medicare, or veterans' benefits" [6][7]. On the Pentagon audit, Calvert has publicly scolded the Defense Department for its seventh consecutive failed audit, calling it a betrayal of taxpayers — then continued to sign off on ever-larger defense budgets without conditioning funding on actually passing one [8]. The money tells the story with admirable clarity. He chairs the subcommittee that decides how much money those same defense contractors receive. The real estate sector is equally generous — and equally well-served: an ethics complaint filed in August 2024 by End Citizens United alleges that Calvert failed to disclose real estate holdings on his financial disclosure forms, holdings that happen to sit conveniently near the federal earmarks he directs [1][10]. Calvert's three decades in Congress have been a masterclass in the self-reinforcing loop the 80% Bill aims to break: defense contractors fund the chairman who funds the defense contractors, real estate investments appreciate alongside taxpayer-funded infrastructure, and reform legislation dies in committees chaired by the people who benefit most from the status quo. He is, in the most literal sense, the case study for why these twenty bills exist.

She'll reintroduce transparency legislation, hold a press conference, and then let it quietly die in committee -- a ritual as predictable as Seattle rain.

Cantwell's record is a masterclass in selective populism: loud on prescription drugs and Social Security where it's safe, silent on stock trading bans and dark money where it's personal. After 25 years, Washington state deserves a senator who doesn't treat the 80% agenda like a buffet.
