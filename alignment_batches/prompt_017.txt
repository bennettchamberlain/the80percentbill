--- F000478: Russell Fry (Republican, SC, house) ---
Rep. Russell Fry owes his congressional career to one man: Donald Trump. Rice had voted with Trump over 90% of the time and helped draft the 2017 GOP tax cuts, but one act of constitutional conscience was enough to end his career. Fry rode the endorsement to a comfortable victory, proving that in SC-7, loyalty to one person outweighs a decade of conservative bona fides.

Now serving on the powerful Energy and Commerce Committee and the Judiciary Committee, Fry is perfectly positioned to address the policies Americans overwhelmingly support. Instead, he's used his perch to champion energy industry priorities. He sits on the Energy, Commerce Manufacturing and Trade, and Communications and Technology subcommittees — a portfolio tailor-made for corporate lobbying. On stock trading bans: silence. On term limits: Fry helped pass term limits legislation at the state level but has shown no appetite for bringing that fight to Washington, where the stakes — and the career incentives — are considerably different. On corporate PAC bans, the DISCLOSE Act, and dark money reform: nothing. On Pentagon audits, Medicare drug negotiation, paid family leave, and banning junk fees: the cupboard is bare.

Fry did make headlines for writing a letter urging the administration to uphold an offshore drilling ban along the South Carolina coast — a surprisingly populist move that suggests he knows which way the wind blows when his own constituents' beaches and tourism economy are at stake. It's a reminder that even the most party-line Republicans will buck the establishment when the issue hits home.

The residents of Myrtle Beach, Florence, and the Pee Dee region support the 80% Bill's policies by overwhelming margins. Fry has the committee assignments to make real progress on these issues. Whether he has the independence to defy the donor class that put him there is another question entirely.

--- F000479: John Fetterman (Democrat, PA, senate) ---
John Fetterman arrived in the Senate in 2023 as the hoodie-wearing, populist champion of working-class Pennsylvania. Two years later, he ended 2025 as Donald Trump's most reliable Democratic vote in the upper chamber, siding with Republicans over 140 times — a political metamorphosis so complete it makes a caterpillar look indecisive. His 91% Democratic voting rate sounds reassuring until you realize the remaining 9% includes confirming Pam Bondi as Attorney General and being the sole Democrat to vote against a resolution reasserting congressional authority over military force, because Fetterman apparently believes the path to peace in Iran runs through "30,000-pound bombs and the IDF."

He failed to disclose these holdings for over 17 months, violating the very STOCK Act he claims to champion. The fundraising numbers tell the real story of Fetterman's transformation. Fetterman co-sponsored the SMART Prices Act to strengthen Medicare drug negotiation and still claims to support Medicare for All. He sits on the Banking Committee and the Agriculture Committee. But his 13.8% missed vote rate — nearly five times the Senate median — suggests that showing up is optional when you have already decided which party you are performing for on any given Tuesday. Progressive groups like Indivisible PA have asked him to resign, potential primary challengers are circling for 2028, and his approval rating among Democrats has flipped to a net negative. The hoodie stays the same. Everything underneath it has changed.

--- F000480: Vince Fong (Republican, CA, house) ---
Vince Fong, Republican of California's 20th district, is the man who inherited Kevin McCarthy's seat and, by all appearances, his playbook. The son of a Hong Kong immigrant who worked in a Bakersfield pharmacy, Fong earned degrees from UCLA and Princeton before spending nearly a decade as McCarthy's district director -- the political equivalent of an apprenticeship in a family business [1][2]. When McCarthy was ousted as Speaker and resigned from Congress in December 2023, Fong won the special election in May 2024 with the former Speaker's endorsement and the help of a McCarthy-linked PAC that steered over $700,000 into the race [3][4]. He now represents Kern County, which produces roughly 75% of California's oil and sits at the crossroads of the state's agriculture, energy, and air quality crises [5].

On the 80% Bill's twenty planks, Fong's record is a study in absence. He has cosponsored fewer bills than any member of California's delegation in 2024, and there is no public record of him championing stock trading bans, congressional term limits, Pentagon audits, dark money disclosure, the DISCLOSE Act, paid family leave, right to repair, junk fee bans, or any of the other broadly popular reforms on this list [6]. His campaign website's issues page reads like a Central Valley Republican template: secure the border, support law enforcement, expand water storage, and protect oil and gas jobs [7]. These are defensible priorities for his district, but they conveniently avoid every item that polls at 80% or above nationally.

Where Fong is most revealing is on energy and environment. He urged colleagues to oppose California's oil setback legislation, calling it "a setback for desperately needed energy production" [5]. He signed a letter with fifteen other members of Congress condemning Governor Newsom's refinery mandate proposals [8]. His League of Conservation Voters lifetime score is 4%, with a 2025 score of exactly 0% -- a number so pristine it almost looks intentional [9]. An opinion piece in the Fresno Bee put it bluntly: Fong votes to back oil and gas while Valley air pollution "be damned," ignoring the diesel-choked corridors of Highway 99 and I-5 that make his district's residents among the most pollution-exposed in the state [10]. For a representative whose constituents breathe some of the dirtiest air in America, this is not just a policy choice. It is a statement about whose interests come first.

The money tells the familiar story. Fong raised over $1.4 million for his 2024 campaign, with oil, gas, and real estate interests prominently among his donor base [11][12]. The McCarthy Victory Fund, which raised nearly $9.6 million in the 2023-2024 cycle, served as an institutional pipeline connecting establishment Republican donors to Fong's candidacy [13]. His district is safe enough that the general election is a formality, which means the real constituency is not the voters of CA-20 but the donor class that got him there. When your biggest benefactor's PAC spends $700,000 to put you in office, the question of who you represent answers itself.

Fong is still early in his congressional career, which his defenders would say means it is unfair to judge him on bills he hasn't had time to cosponsor. But silence has a voting record too. Every reform he hasn't endorsed, every popular bill he hasn't cosponsored, every environmental vote where he has sided with polluters over his own constituents -- these are choices, not oversights. The son of an immigrant pharmacist now serves the interests of the oil companies whose emissions are making his neighbors sick. Kevin McCarthy would be proud [14].

--- F000481: Shomari Figures (Democrat, AL, house) ---
Shomari Figures arrived in Washington in January 2025 carrying perhaps the most consequential surname in Alabama civil rights history. After cutting his teeth in the Obama White House and as Deputy Chief of Staff to Attorney General Merrick Garland, Shomari won Alabama's redrawn, near-majority-Black 2nd Congressional District in 2024 — a seat the Supreme Court effectively ordered to exist in Allen v. Milligan — and now represents one of the poorest districts in the country[3][4]. He is, in other words, exactly the kind of member Congress needs more of. Which makes what happened in his primary all the more instructive.

On the 80 Percent Bill's core working-class priorities, Figures' freshman record is largely clean. He voted against the House Republican FY2025 budget and against the "One Big Beautiful Bill," submitting an amendment that would have prevented states from bearing the added costs of SNAP cuts his office calculated would cost Alabama $120 million[6][7]. He has decried the bill as "nothing more than a handout for the rich," correctly noting that millionaires receive an $80,000 annual tax break while $50,000 earners get less than $250[7]. He wrote to the VA Secretary demanding answers on DOGE-driven layoffs affecting facilities in Montgomery, Mobile, and Tuskegee[8]. Then there is the money. Figures won his Democratic primary not because his $400,000 in direct fundraising outpaced the field, but because a single outside PAC — Protect Progress, the political arm of the crypto industry's Fairshake network — poured approximately $2.7 million in independent expenditures into his race[11][12]. After winning, Figures signed a joint letter to the DNC calling for "pro-digital language" in the party platform and a "pro-innovation SEC chair"[14]. Among his direct donors, lawyers represent his single largest contributing industry[15]. None of this makes Figures a villain — he is a freshman Democrat in a hostile state doing the work — but the crypto industry did not spend $2.7 million on Shomari Figures because they believed he would fight for transparent financial markets.

Alabama's 2nd District did not get a seat in Congress so a crypto PAC could park a friendly vote there. It got a seat because the Voting Rights Act still means something. The question for Figures is which constituency he is actually serving.

--- F000483: Laura Friedman (Democrat, CA, house) ---
Laura Friedman is a former film producer turned politician who won California's 30th Congressional District in 2024, succeeding Adam Schiff after he moved to the Senate. She won with 68.4% of the vote in a district so blue that Joe Biden carried it with over 72% in 2020 [1]. Before Congress, Friedman served as a Glendale City Councilmember, Mayor, and then eight years in the California State Assembly, where she chaired both the Transportation and Natural Resources committees and built a reputation as a prolific progressive legislator on housing, climate, and consumer protection [2]. She now sits on the House Transportation and Infrastructure Committee and the Science, Space, and Technology Committee [3].

On paper, Friedman is the kind of Democrat the 80% Bill was designed for. In the California Assembly, she authored first-in-the-nation climate legislation, pushed to eliminate parking minimums near transit to boost housing production, co-authored junk fee legislation (AB 8), and championed bills expanding access to healthcare and higher education [4][5]. In her first year in Congress, she led the Cut Red Tape for Housing Act, a bipartisan bill with Rep. Lawler to streamline federal housing investment, and co-led the Raise the Wage Act and the Restoring Essential Healthcare Act to reverse the Republican ban on Medicaid reimbursements for Planned Parenthood [6]. Her office has resolved more than 1,450 constituent cases, returning over $5.5 million to taxpayers — the kind of constituent service metric that suggests someone who actually reads the mail [7]. She introduced the Build HUBS Act in January 2026 to unlock federal housing programs for smaller, community-based developments, and her legislation specifically defines infill housing to protect farmland from sprawl [8].

Where Friedman's alignment with the 80% Bill gets interesting is the freshman question: does she have the institutional weight to champion these causes, or will she be content to cosponsor? She arrived in Washington with genuine policy chops but no federal voting record before January 2025. Her attendance has been stellar — missing just 4 of 440 roll call votes, well below the median [9]. On the Epstein Files Transparency Act, she was part of the overwhelming 427-1 vote [10]. But on the harder items in the 80% Bill — banning congressional stock trading, a lifetime lobbying ban, Pentagon audits, overturning Citizens United — there is not yet a robust record of sponsorship or public advocacy. The question is whether a freshman in the minority party builds that record through bold legislation or quiet cosponsorship.

The money side of Friedman's ledger is relatively clean for a freshman. A single-candidate super PAC called Fighting for Californians raised $280,000 in support of her 2024 campaign [11], and her campaign received $1.38 million in total reported payments during the cycle [12]. Her district's deep-blue tilt means she does not face the same fundraising pressure that pushes swing-district Democrats toward corporate PAC dependence. Still, as a new member, the lobbying playbook is predictable: real estate, entertainment, and tech interests from the LA basin will come knocking, and how Friedman answers the door will say more than any press release [13]. For now, she is a promising but unfinished chapter — a legislator with the right instincts on housing, climate, and consumer protection, who has yet to be tested on the full breadth of reforms that 80% of Americans say they want.

--- G000386: Chuck Grassley (Republican, IA, senate) ---
Chuck Grassley has represented Iowa in the United States Senate since 1981, making him one of the longest-serving senators in American history. A former sheet-metal worker and state legislator from New Hartford, Grassley is now in his nineties and was reelected in 2022 at age eighty-nine, because apparently the concept of retirement is something he believes should apply exclusively to other people [1]. He has served as chairman of both the Senate Judiciary Committee and the Senate Finance Committee, two of the most powerful perches in Washington, and currently holds the title of President pro tempore emeritus [2]. He is also famous for his early-morning runs and his prolific, often baffling Twitter account, where he has waged a decades-long war against the History Channel for not showing enough history.

On the twenty issues in the 80% Bill, Grassley presents a fascinatingly contradictory record. He has been a genuine champion of government transparency and whistleblower protections, authoring the amendments that strengthened the False Claims Act and fighting to force the Pentagon to submit to audits for decades [3]. He was one of the earliest Senate voices demanding that the Department of Defense account for its spending, which puts him squarely on the right side of at least one 80% Bill plank. He voted for the bipartisan repeal of the 2002 Iraq AUMF when it passed the Senate in 2023 [4]. He has expressed support for congressional term limits, though he has served forty-four years in the Senate, a contradiction he has never satisfactorily explained [5]. He cosponsored the FARM Act to restrict foreign purchases of U.S. agricultural land, a natural fit for Iowa's senior senator [6]. And he played a key role in pushing for the release of the Epstein files, using his Judiciary Committee clout to press for transparency [7].

But Grassley's populist impulses crash hard against his institutional loyalties on the rest of the ledger. He has consistently opposed Medicare drug price negotiation, voting against the Inflation Reduction Act in 2022, despite having earlier worked on a bipartisan drug-pricing bill with Senator Ron Wyden that Republican leadership quietly smothered [8]. He has never supported banning congressional stock trading with any legislative teeth, has shown no interest in a lifetime lobbying ban, and has voted against the DISCLOSE Act every time it has come to the floor [9]. On campaign finance reform broadly, Grassley has opposed efforts to overturn Citizens United and has resisted banning corporate PACs or dark money, positions that align neatly with his own fundraising apparatus [10]. He has not championed paid family leave, has opposed taxing the ultra-wealthy, and has been a defender of the carried interest loophole that allows hedge fund managers to pay lower tax rates than Iowa farmers [11].

The donor profile tells the rest of the story. Over his career, Grassley has raised tens of millions from the insurance industry, pharmaceutical companies, agribusiness, and the financial sector [12]. In his 2022 reelection campaign, he raised over $28 million, a staggering sum for an Iowa Senate race, with major contributions from health sector PACs, real estate interests, and corporate agriculture [13]. The insurance and pharmaceutical industries have been particularly generous, which provides useful context for understanding why his bipartisan drug-pricing bill with Wyden mysteriously lost his enthusiastic backing once leadership applied pressure. The National Association of Realtors and the American Bankers Association have also been reliable contributors, industries with obvious stakes in housing policy and tax loopholes that Grassley has been content to leave untouched [14].

Chuck Grassley is the rare senator who has genuinely bucked his party on Pentagon accountability and whistleblower protections — real accomplishments that deserve real credit. But on the broader constellation of reforms that eighty percent of Americans support, he has spent four decades in the Senate building a record that suggests transparency is wonderful, so long as it is applied to everyone except the donor class that keeps funding his campaigns. At ninety-two, he has had more time than almost anyone in Senate history to close tax loopholes, ban dark money, and reform elections. That he has not done so is not an oversight — it is a choice, made over and over, for forty-four years and counting.
