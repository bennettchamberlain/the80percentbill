--- K000009: Marcy Kaptur (Democrat, OH, house) ---
Marcy Kaptur has represented Ohio's 9th Congressional District since 1983, making her the longest-serving woman in the history of the U.S. House of Representatives [1]. Her district runs along the Lake Erie shore from Toledo to the western suburbs of Cleveland, a Rust Belt corridor of manufacturing towns that have weathered plant closures, opioid crises, and decades of economic upheaval. Kaptur sits on the House Appropriations Committee and its Defense subcommittee, a position she has used to steer federal dollars to her district while keeping an eye on Pentagon spending [2]. She is a Democrat of the old-school populist variety, more comfortable talking about trade policy and family farms than culture war flashpoints.

On the 80% Bill, Kaptur's record is among the strongest in the Ohio delegation, though it is not without its blind spots. She cosponsored the STOCK Act and has supported efforts to ban congressional stock trading [3]. She voted for the Freedom to Vote Act and the John Lewis Voting Rights Advancement Act [4]. She cosponsored the FAMILY Act for paid family leave [5] and has been a longtime advocate for expanding Social Security by lifting the payroll tax cap [6]. She voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which included Medicare drug price negotiation [7]. She has backed the DISCLOSE Act and has long called for overturning Citizens United, cosponsoring the constitutional amendment to that end [8]. She supported police body camera mandates as part of the George Floyd Justice in Policing Act [9]. And she has been one of the loudest voices in Congress on protecting American farmland from foreign adversaries, particularly Chinese state-linked purchases in Ohio [10].

Where Kaptur's record gets more complicated is on war powers and the Pentagon. While she voted to repeal the 2002 Iraq AUMF [11], she has historically been a reliable vote for defense appropriations and has not been a leading voice on auditing the Pentagon, despite her seat on the Defense Appropriations subcommittee [12]. She has also not cosponsored the lifetime lobbying ban or the ban on corporate PACs, areas where her long tenure might make reform feel uncomfortably personal [13]. On term limits, her forty-plus years in office speak louder than any cosponsorship record ever could [14].

The financial picture is more straightforward than most. Kaptur's top donors include labor unions, particularly the UAW and building trades unions, along with defense contractors like General Dynamics and Lockheed Martin [15]. She has accepted corporate PAC money throughout her career, though her reliance on labor PACs distinguishes her from colleagues who are bankrolled by Wall Street and pharma [16]. Her career fundraising totals are modest by modern standards, reflecting a safe district and a member who has never needed to chase big-dollar donors to survive [17].

Kaptur is proof that longevity in Congress is not inherently disqualifying, but it does raise an obvious question. She supports nearly every plank of the 80% Bill except the one that would send her home. After four decades in office, she has earned the right to be judged on her record rather than her tenure, and that record is genuinely strong. Whether she would vote for term limits if the bill ever reached the floor is a question only she can answer, and one she has been carefully avoiding for years [18].

--- K000367: Amy Klobuchar (Democrat, MN, senate) ---
Amy Klobuchar has built a Senate career on the proposition that government should work — a stance that sounds unremarkable until you consider how rarely Washington tests the hypothesis. First elected in 2006 after eight years as Hennepin County Attorney, Klobuchar arrived with a prosecutor's instinct for preparation and a Midwesterner's allergy to grandstanding [1]. She promptly became one of the most legislatively productive members of the Senate, sponsoring more enacted bills than any colleague by the end of the 114th Congress. Her focus areas — antitrust enforcement, election security, drug pricing, and campaign finance transparency — read less like a political platform than a to-do list of things most Americans assumed someone in Washington was already handling [2].

On antitrust, Klobuchar has made Big Tech accountability something close to a personal crusade. As Chair of the Senate Judiciary Subcommittee on Competition Policy, Antitrust, and Consumer Rights, she introduced the American Innovation and Choice Online Act with Republican Chuck Grassley — the first digital competition bill to advance in Congress since the dawn of the internet [3]. She has also pushed legislation to block monopolistic mergers and, more recently, introduced the Preventing Algorithmic Collusion Act to stop corporations from using pricing algorithms to quietly fleece consumers. Her book on antitrust, published in 2021, runs over 500 pages, which is either admirable thoroughness or a cry for help, depending on your perspective.

On prescription drug costs, Klobuchar has been equally relentless. She co-authored the SMART Prices Act to expand Medicare's negotiating authority, and provisions from her earlier drug-pricing legislation were signed into law in 2022, producing negotiated price reductions between 38 and 79 percent on select medications [4]. She has noted, with characteristic Minnesota understatement, that there are literally two pharmaceutical lobbyists for every member of Congress — a ratio that helps explain why common-sense pricing reform requires a decade of legislative trench warfare [5].

Klobuchar's commitment to election integrity and campaign finance reform predates its recent fashionability. She co-introduced the For the People Act and the DISCLOSE Act, backed a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United, and partnered with John McCain and Mark Warner on the Honest Ads Act to bring transparency to online political advertising [6]. As Chair of the Senate Rules Committee, she has argued repeatedly that secret money corrodes democratic legitimacy — a position supported by polling but stubbornly resisted by the colleagues who benefit from the status quo.

Her 2020 presidential campaign revealed both her strengths and her vulnerabilities: she polled well on competence and bipartisanship but struggled to generate the grassroots electricity that primary voters increasingly demand [7]. Her prosecutorial record in Hennepin County drew scrutiny, particularly her handling of police-involved cases [8]. Still, the Lugar Center has ranked her in the top fifth of senators for bipartisanship, and her alignment with the 80% Bill's planks — from Medicare negotiation to dark money disclosure to antitrust enforcement — reflects not political calculation but the consistent application of a simple idea: that popular policies should not be this hard to pass.

--- K000375: William R. Keating (Democrat, MA, house) ---
William Keating is the kind of congressman whose career trajectory makes perfect sense if you remember that politics in Massachusetts is less an ideology than a family business. A former postal worker who put himself through Boston College and Suffolk University Law School, Keating entered the Massachusetts House of Representatives in 1977 at the age of twenty-four and spent the next two decades accumulating committee chairmanships in the state legislature the way some people collect frequent flyer miles [1]. He served as Norfolk County District Attorney from 1999 until 2010, when the retirement of Congressman Bill Delahunt opened a seat — prompting Keating to relocate from Sharon to a rental property in Quincy with the kind of geographic flexibility that suggests the district chose him slightly less than he chose the district [2].

Since arriving in Congress in 2011, Keating has settled comfortably into the Foreign Affairs and Armed Services Committees, where he currently serves as Ranking Member of the Europe, Eurasia, Energy and Environment subcommittee. He has positioned himself as a reliable hawk on NATO solidarity and Ukraine support, co-sponsoring resolutions reaffirming Article 5 commitments and introducing the Ukraine War Risk Insurance Act to keep commercial shipping lanes open [3]. These are worthy positions, though one might note that advocating for transatlantic defense spending is considerably easier when your district's economy benefits from the defense industrial base and the Cape Cod coastline is not, in fact, under threat from Russian submarines.

On domestic matters, Keating has pursued the narrow interests of Massachusetts's 9th District with the attentiveness of a man who knows exactly which constituencies keep him employed. He arranged for the Department of Agriculture to purchase $35 million worth of fish from South Coast fishermen for school lunch programs — a move that simultaneously supported local fishing communities and fed children, which is the kind of two-birds-one-stone policy that makes you wonder why it required a congressman's intervention in the first place [4]. He has also championed offshore wind development, securing $2 million for the National Offshore Wind Institute in New Bedford while attempting to balance the concerns of fishermen who worry that wind turbines and trawling nets make poor neighbors [5].

Keating's voting record is that of a dependable party-line Democrat: a 100 percent AFL-CIO rating in 2011, a lifetime score of 95 percent, and a comfortable alignment with leadership on most major legislation [6]. His missed vote rate of 3.3 percent over his tenure — worse than the median of 2.1 percent for current representatives — suggests that even showing up is occasionally optional when your district has not hosted a competitive general election in living memory [7]. On the 80% Bill's planks, Keating lands where most Massachusetts Democrats land: supportive of Medicare negotiation, election reform, and climate action, though rarely the one leading the charge. He is, in the most Massachusetts sense possible, a reliable vote in search of a crusade.

--- K000376: Mike Kelly (Republican, PA, house) ---
Mike Kelly made his fortune selling Chevrolets, Cadillacs, Hyundais, and Kias at the family dealership in Butler, Pennsylvania, and he has spent his time in Congress ensuring that the tax code is as generous to people like Mike Kelly as a zero-percent financing deal on a loaded Escalade. As Chairman of the Ways and Means Subcommittee on Tax, Kelly was a chief architect of the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act [1], which slashed the corporate rate from 35% to 21% and delivered windfalls to corporations and the ultra-wealthy. The 80% Bill calls for taxing the ultra-wealthy and closing tax loopholes. Kelly has spent his career doing the precise opposite — and getting paid handsomely for it.

But the real masterpiece of Kelly's congressional tenure is the ethics investigation. In April 2020, while Kelly was lobbying the Commerce Department for Section 232 tariffs on specialty steel — the kind made at a Cleveland-Cliffs plant in his district — his wife Victoria purchased $23,075 in Cleveland-Cliffs stock [2]. She later sold it for a profit of $64,476. The House Ethics Committee found "substantial evidence" that Kelly violated the congressional code of conduct, noting he "took several actions to specifically benefit Cleveland-Cliffs during the time his wife had a direct financial interest in the company" [3]. Victoria Kelly did not fully cooperate with the investigation. The committee formally reprimanded Kelly and urged the couple to divest their Cleveland-Cliffs holdings. This is the man who chairs the subcommittee that writes America's tax laws — and he cannot even keep his own household's stock trades clean.

On the Ban Stock Trading plank of the 80% Bill, Kelly's position writes itself. He is living proof of why 86% of Americans want congressional stock trading banned. On campaign finance reform, Kelly has been a reliable opponent, voting against H.R. 1 and every effort to ban dark money, require donor disclosure, or limit corporate PAC influence. His dealership empire, valued at tens of millions, also secured between $450,000 and $1,050,000 in PPP loans during COVID [4] — the same program meant for struggling small businesses.

Kelly's most brazen act came after the 2020 election, when he filed a lawsuit asking courts to throw out all 2.5 million mail-in ballots cast in Pennsylvania — most of them by Democrats — or to wipe out the election results entirely and let the Republican state legislature choose the electors [5]. The Pennsylvania Supreme Court unanimously tossed the suit, noting Kelly had waited too long to challenge a law he had no problem with until his side lost. Kelly then appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court. The Fair Elections plank of the 80% Bill calls for protecting every citizen's right to vote. Kelly tried to invalidate millions of votes because he did not like the outcome.

On Medicare drug negotiation, paid family leave, the Pentagon audit, right to repair, and junk fee bans, Kelly has been either absent or opposed. He did introduce the Strong Families Act, a modest bipartisan paid leave proposal that encouraged but did not require employers to offer 12 weeks of leave [6]. Credit where due — though encouraging employers to do something is to legislating what "thoughts and prayers" is to gun control.

Kelly is worth an estimated $12.4 million [7], chairs the subcommittee that writes the tax code, got reprimanded for his wife's stock trades, tried to throw out millions of votes, and took PPP loans for his car dealerships. The 80% Bill was written for moments exactly like this.

--- K000377: Mark Kelly (Democrat, AZ, senate) ---
Mark Kelly is the rare senator whose biography actually lives up to the campaign brochure. A Navy combat pilot, NASA astronaut, and husband to former Congresswoman Gabby Giffords, Kelly won his Arizona Senate seat in 2020 and has since positioned himself as one of the chamber's most aggressive reformers on government accountability. He sits on the Armed Services and Energy committees, and he is one of only a handful of senators who have placed their financial portfolios in qualified blind trusts [1]. He also refuses corporate PAC money and publicly posts his official schedule — a transparency trifecta that exactly zero of his Senate colleagues have fully matched [2].

On the substance of the 80% Bill, Kelly is one of its strongest natural allies. He is the lead co-sponsor of the Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act with Senator Jon Ossoff, legislation that would force members, spouses, and dependents to divest or use blind trusts [1]. He introduced the Ban Corporate PACs Act to prohibit for-profit corporations from funding campaigns [2]. He voted for the Inflation Reduction Act, which he personally helped shape to include Medicare drug price negotiation — the first time the program was empowered to bargain with pharmaceutical companies — along with a $35 insulin cap and a $2,000 annual out-of-pocket ceiling for seniors [3]. He co-sponsored the Freedom to Vote Act to combat gerrymandering [4], led Senate floor speeches on the 16th anniversary of Citizens United calling for its reversal [5], and has vocally supported the DISCLOSE Act to unmask dark money [5]. He voted to repeal the 1991 and 2002 Iraq War AUMFs [6], has pledged to protect Social Security from cuts or privatization [7], and pushed for full release of the Epstein files, urging accountability "regardless of wealth, power, or political party" [8].

Where Kelly gets complicated is the follow-the-money chapter. Despite refusing corporate PACs, he received $198,961 from the defense sector during the 2022 cycle alone — more than any other member of the Senate Armed Services Committee [9]. Individuals and PACs affiliated with Boeing, Lockheed Martin, Northrop Grumman, and Raytheon were among the top defense industry contributors that cycle [9]. For a former fighter pilot turned astronaut, defense ties are perhaps unsurprising, but they do raise the question of whether his hawkish instincts — he said it was "unlikely" he would back a war powers resolution limiting strikes on Iran [10] — are shaped by conviction or by the companies writing checks. His top industry donors across his career have included lawyers, securities and investment firms, and the defense aerospace sector [11]. Kelly has also drawn scrutiny for serving on the boards of gig-economy and restaurant companies before entering the Senate, which may explain his initial reluctance to co-sponsor the PRO Act protecting workers' organizing rights [12].

The bottom line on Kelly is that he is genuinely one of the Senate's loudest voices for the structural reforms Americans overwhelmingly support — banning stock trades, killing dark money, negotiating drug prices. He puts his own money where his mouth is with blind trusts and PAC refusals. But the defense dollars flowing into his campaign coffers, and the occasional hesitation on war powers, suggest that even the reformers have a price range. The 80% Bill would find a willing champion in Kelly on most of its provisions. Whether he would fight just as hard for the ones that inconvenience his donors is the $198,961 question.

--- K000383: Angus S. King, Jr. (Independent, ME, senate) ---
Angus King is that rarest of Washington species: an independent who actually behaves like one. The former two-term governor of Maine arrived in the Senate in 2013 having already made a small fortune in energy conservation, sold a wind energy company to avoid conflicts of interest, and decided that neither party deserved his membership card [1]. He caucuses with Democrats, sits on the Armed Services, Intelligence, Energy and Natural Resources, and Veterans' Affairs committees, and has carved out a reputation as a cybersecurity hawk who co-chaired the Cyberspace Solarium Commission [2]. At 81, he remains one of only a handful of truly unaffiliated senators, which means he gets courted by everyone and owned by no one — at least on paper.

On the 80% Bill scorecard, King lands on the right side of several marquee issues. He voted for the Inflation Reduction Act and its Medicare drug price negotiation provisions, calling it the realization of one of his "first bills as a Senator" [3]. He has been a genuine champion of the DISCLOSE Act, leading Senate Rules Committee hearings on dark money and cosponsoring legislation to force disclosure of donors giving $10,000 or more [4]. He cosponsored the ETHICS Act requiring members to place financial assets in blind trusts [5], and he joined Elizabeth Warren and a bipartisan group to introduce the Streamline Pentagon Spending Act, aiming to cut wasteful Defense Department reporting and enhance civilian budget oversight [6]. On forever wars, King was one of the Senate's most vocal critics of open-ended AUMFs, memorably calling a 2013 Armed Services hearing on war powers "the most astounding and most astoundingly disturbing" he had attended, and voting in 2023 to repeal the Iraq AUMF [7].

Where King gets complicated is on the gap between principle and practice. He initially declined to back a congressional stock trading ban in 2022, with his office offering the usual "reviewing proposals" language, even as his household held at least $243,000 in individual stocks in companies like Marriott, Amazon, and UPS [8]. He eventually came around by cosponsoring the ETHICS Act in 2023, but the delay left a mark. He joined Warren in pushing to close carried-interest loopholes and enforce the corporate alternative minimum tax on companies reporting over $1 billion in profits [9], yet his own net worth — estimated between $4.3 million and $13.2 million — puts him comfortably in the class of senators for whom "tax the ultra-wealthy" is a philosophical exercise rather than a personal one [10].

King's fundraising is relatively modest by Senate standards, and his independent status means he is not beholden to either party's fundraising apparatus. His 2024 cycle was an off-year for his seat, keeping the donor spotlight dim. Still, his committee perch on Armed Services means defense industry attention comes with the territory, even if King has used that seat to push for Pentagon accountability rather than rubber-stamp budgets [6].

The bottom line: Angus King is a legitimately independent voice who has put real legislative muscle behind campaign finance transparency, Pentagon oversight, drug pricing reform, and war powers. His late arrival to the stock-trading-ban party and his own comfortable wealth are worth noting, but on the whole, he is one of the few members of Congress who could read the 80% Bill and find more to agree with than to dodge.

--- K000384: Tim Kaine (Democrat, VA, senate) ---
Senator Tim Kaine has built a fascinating career out of being almost good enough. The Virginia Democrat checks enough boxes on the 80% Bill to earn a gold star from your most lenient substitute teacher, while quietly leaving the door open for the industries that keep his campaign war chest looking healthy.

Let's start with the highlights. Kaine has been one of the Senate's loudest voices on ending forever wars, co-leading the bipartisan effort with Senator Todd Young to repeal the 1991 and 2002 Authorizations for Use of Military Force. He shepherded the repeal through the Senate in 2023, marking the first time in over fifty years the chamber voted to sunset an AUMF. He has co-sponsored the DISCLOSE Act to drag dark money into the sunlight and unveiled the ETHICS Act to ban congressional stock trading, complete with fines pegged to members' monthly pay. On Medicare drug negotiation, Kaine cast a deciding vote for the Inflation Reduction Act, which gave Medicare the power to negotiate prescription drug prices for the first time ever. He has championed a $2,000 annual cap on out-of-pocket drug costs for seniors and pushed to crack down on predatory Pharmacy Benefit Manager practices. He has called for lifting the payroll tax cap to shore up Social Security, and he publicly pressured the DOJ over the Epstein Files Transparency Act, even calling on Bill Clinton to address his appearance in the released documents.

So where does Kaine stumble? Follow the money. His career fundraising leans heavily on lawyers and law firms -- he pulled in $1.9 million from the legal sector in his first Senate race alone, more than any other freshman that cycle. Only 17 percent of his $18 million haul came from small donors, while nearly a third came from the top fraction of America's wealthiest political givers. He receives contributions from lobbyists and has not refused corporate PAC money outright. He has not championed term limits, a lobbying ban for former members of Congress, or the Right to Repair movement. His support for auditing the Pentagon -- an agency that has failed eight consecutive independent audits -- has been conspicuously muted for a senator who sits on the Armed Services Committee.

Kaine occupies a familiar archetype: the senator who supports popular reforms loudly enough to earn applause, but not so aggressively that it threatens the fundraising ecosystem. He will co-sponsor the transparency bill and give the speech about ending dark money, then quietly accept the next check from a K Street bundler. Virginia deserves a senator who doesn't just talk about the 80% -- but fights for it without a footnote.

--- K000385: Robin L. Kelly (Democrat, IL, house) ---
Robin Kelly has represented Illinois's 2nd Congressional District since winning a 2013 special election, making her one of the longer-tenured members of the Illinois delegation. Born in Harlem to a grocer and a postal worker, she earned a Ph.D. in political science from Northern Illinois University before serving in the Illinois state legislature and as Cook County's chief administrative officer [1]. She sits on the powerful Energy and Commerce Committee, where she serves as vice chair, with subcommittee assignments in Health, Communications and Technology, and Innovation, Data, and Commerce [2]. In May 2025, she announced her retirement from the House to run for the U.S. Senate seat being vacated by Dick Durbin, campaigning on a platform she calls "People Over Profits" [3].

On the 80% Bill's policy planks, Kelly has a genuinely strong record in several areas. She is a member of the Medicare for All Caucus and has consistently fought to expand healthcare access and lower prescription drug costs, supporting Medicare drug price negotiation and voting for the Inflation Reduction Act's landmark provisions on the subject [4]. She has been a tireless champion of gun violence prevention — authoring the Kelly Reports, leading the 2016 House floor sit-in alongside John Lewis, and helping pass the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act in 2022, the first major gun safety law in nearly thirty years [5]. She has defended Social Security and Medicare against Republican cuts, and her Senate platform explicitly calls for making billionaires pay their fair share and keeping Social Security solvent [3]. On campaign finance reform, she has stated plainly that "money counts unfortunately — that's why we need campaign finance reform so people can't buy elections or buy votes" [6].

The irony of that quote lands a bit differently when you examine Kelly's donor profile. In the 2023-2024 cycle, Kelly accepted just over $100,000 from pharmaceutical companies and their affiliates — while sitting on the Energy and Commerce Health Subcommittee, the very panel that regulates those companies [7]. Johnson & Johnson alone contributed $6,500 [7]. Some of that pharmaceutical money came from firms implicated in the opioid crisis, a detail that tends to go unmentioned in her healthcare advocacy press releases [7]. It is the eternal Washington two-step: advocate loudly for lowering drug prices while quietly cashing checks from the companies that set them.

Kelly's record on the structural reform provisions of the 80% Bill is harder to pin down. She has voted with Biden's stated position 100 percent of the time in the 117th Congress, which means she supported whatever the Democratic caucus brought to the floor, but active championship of measures like a lifetime lobbying ban, congressional term limits, auditing the Pentagon, or banning corporate PACs is not a hallmark of her tenure [8]. She has not been a prominent cosponsor of the DISCLOSE Act or legislation to overturn Citizens United, despite her stated support for campaign finance reform. The gap between rhetorical enthusiasm and legislative hustle is not unique to Kelly, but it is notable in someone now asking Illinois voters for a promotion.

Kelly is, in many ways, the model of a competent, long-serving Democrat who delivers on the issues her caucus prioritizes while leaving the deeper structural rot untouched. Her gun violence work is real, her healthcare advocacy is substantive, and her defense of the safety net is consistent. But a congresswoman who takes six figures from Big Pharma while sitting on the committee that oversees Big Pharma, and who calls for campaign finance reform without cosponsoring the major bills that would achieve it, embodies the very contradictions the 80% Bill was designed to expose.

--- K000388: Trent Kelly (Republican, MS, house) ---
Representative Trent Kelly has held Mississippi's 1st Congressional District seat since winning a 2015 special election, and in that time he has built a resume that reads like a conservative loyalty test. A retired Major General in the Mississippi Army National Guard, Kelly deployed to Iraq twice -- once during Desert Storm and again in 2005 and 2009 -- earning a Combat Action Badge along the way.[1] He serves on the House Armed Services Committee and the Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence, posts that put him at the intersection of the defense budget and national security.[2] He also signed the amicus brief urging the Supreme Court to overturn the 2020 election results in four swing states, and voted against certifying Biden's Electoral College win on January 6, 2021.[3][4]

Kelly voted against the For the People Act (H.R. 1) in 2021, a sweeping bill that would have addressed at least five planks of the 80% Bill in one stroke: banning dark money, ending gerrymandering through independent redistricting commissions, requiring campaign finance disclosure, reforming elections, and curbing corporate PAC influence.[5] Like every House Republican, he voted no. On Medicare drug negotiation, Kelly opposed the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, which for the first time empowered Medicare to negotiate prescription drug prices -- a policy supported by roughly two-thirds of Americans across party lines, and three-quarters of Medicare enrollees.[6][7] The act also capped insulin costs at $35 per month for Medicare patients, but Kelly chose party discipline over his constituents' pharmacy bills.

To his credit, Kelly has shown genuine interest in protecting U.S. farmland from foreign acquisition -- one of the 80% Bill's twenty planks. He joined a bipartisan letter urging the USDA to disclose foreign purchases of American agricultural land, stating bluntly that "we shouldn't allow foreign countries to buy farmland here."[8] He also co-introduced the Farm Transitions Act to address barriers for young and beginning farmers.[9] This is the kind of crossover position that makes the 80% Bill's theory of change plausible: even the most reliable party-line voters occasionally land where the supermajority already stands.

But follow the money, and the picture clarifies. Kelly sits on the Armed Services Committee, and defense sector PACs have been generous to its members -- to the tune of $5.8 million across the committee during the 2022 cycle alone.[10] Leidos PAC contributed to Kelly even after he objected to certifying the 2020 election, part of a broader pattern in which the five largest defense contractors -- Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, Boeing, Northrop Grumman, and General Dynamics -- funneled nearly $2 million to Republicans who voted against certification.[11][12] The Pentagon, meanwhile, has failed its financial audit for eight consecutive years, and the 80% Bill's call to audit the Defense Department would shine a light on the very spending stream that funds Kelly's campaigns.[13]

Trent Kelly is a decorated combat veteran who has served his country in uniform -- a fact that makes his congressional record all the more striking. He voted to overturn a democratic election, opposed campaign finance transparency, and rejected Medicare drug negotiation, all while collecting checks from the defense industry whose books remain unaudited. On farmland protection he walks with the 80 percent; on nearly everything else, he marches with the donors. The uniform earned him credibility. What he does with it in Congress is another matter entirely.

--- K000389: Ro Khanna (Democrat, CA, house) ---
Ro Khanna has represented California's 17th Congressional District -- the heart of Silicon Valley -- since 2017. A former Obama-era Commerce Department official with a law degree from Yale, Khanna sits on the House Armed Services Committee and the Oversight Committee, positioning him at the intersection of tech policy, defense spending, and government accountability [1]. He is a vice chair of the Congressional Progressive Caucus, co-founder of the No PAC Caucus, and has never accepted a dime from PACs or lobbyists [2]. He represents more billionaires per capita than virtually any other member of Congress, which makes his progressive posture either admirably principled or exquisitely strategic, depending on your level of cynicism.

On the 80% Bill's twenty planks, Khanna is closer to a clean sweep than almost anyone in Congress. He introduced a five-point political reform plan that covers banning congressional stock trading, enacting term limits, imposing a lifetime lobbying ban on former members, and banning PAC and lobbyist money -- four bills in one press release [3][4]. He co-authored the Epstein Files Transparency Act with Republican Thomas Massie, which passed the House 427-1 and was signed into law; he then read on the House floor the names of six "wealthy, powerful men" whose identities had been redacted from the released documents [5][6]. He led the first-ever War Powers resolution to pass a chamber of Congress, halting U.S. involvement in the Yemen war, and authored an amendment blocking federal funds from being used against Iran without congressional authorization -- directly targeting the outdated AUMFs [7]. He has championed Medicare drug price negotiation as part of a deficit reduction plan that also calls for a Pentagon audit, closing the carried interest loophole, restoring the corporate tax rate to 28%, and adding a financial transaction tax [8]. He co-sponsored the Farm System Reform Act with Senator Cory Booker and has been one of the most vocal House members on paid family leave, calling it central to his "Economic Patriotism" agenda [9][10]. He has co-authored legislation to abolish Super PACs and explicitly called for overturning Citizens United [11].

The tension in Khanna's story is not between his votes and his rhetoric -- those are remarkably consistent -- but between his populist agenda and his donor base. His top contributing industry is securities and investment, at $480,500 in the 2024 cycle, followed by internet and electronics companies [12]. Alphabet employees alone have donated over $444,000 to him since 2016; associates of Andreessen Horowitz and Y Combinator are among his top individual donors [12][13]. Nearly 60% of his individual contributions come in sums between $3,300 and $6,600, which is to say this is not a grassroots fundraising operation -- it is a Silicon Valley rolodex [13]. When Khanna backed California's proposed 2026 Billionaire Tax Act, a one-time 5% levy on the assets of state billionaires, his longtime tech allies threatened to primary him. Venture capitalist Chamath Palihapitiya and Y Combinator CEO Garry Tan helped recruit a challenger, and Khanna quickly tempered his endorsement, saying the levy shouldn't "undermine the innovation miracle of Silicon Valley" [14][15].

That moment crystallized the paradox of Ro Khanna: a congressman who genuinely wants to tax billionaires, ban their PACs, and close their loopholes, but whose political survival depends on their continued affection. He can push for a wealth tax in Washington while hedging in his own backyard. He can refuse PAC money while collecting six-figure sums from the same venture capital class that funds the Super PACs he wants to abolish. The 80% Bill asks whether representatives will champion what nearly everyone agrees on. Khanna will -- loudly, legislatively, and with footnotes -- right up until the moment his donors remind him where the district boundaries are.
